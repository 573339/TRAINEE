A. Introduction

This laboratory will provide you with more intense hands-on training for mooring a ship to the pier and taking in mooring lines in preparations for getting the ship underway. The tour of the USS MARLINESPIKE will help you understand some of the shipboard conditions and familiarize you with the equipment learned from the Basic Seamanship classes.

C. Topic Outline:

  1. Introduction
  2. Teamwork and Navy Core Values
  3. Marlinespike Deck Orientation
    1. Pier
      1. Bollards are found on the pier.
      2. The ship’s mooring lines are placed over the bollards.
    2. Anchor and chain -- When dropped from the ship, the anchor restricts the motion of the ship.
    3. Ship’s accommodation ladder
      1. The ship’s accommodation ladder is used to gain access to the quarterdeck from the pier. There will be a quarterdeck watch to grant permission to come aboard the ship.
      2. When at anchor, the accommodation ladder is rigged to the side of the ship with a platform at the bottom where small boats can tie up.
    4. Ship’s weather decks
      1. The decks of the ship are exposed to the weather.
      2. Weather decks have non-skid to prevent slipping, sliding, and injury due to falling.
      3. Items normally found on the weather deck include:
        1. Bitts, chocks, and cleats -- Used for mooring.
        2. Sound-powered telephones/boxes -- Used to stow sound-powered phones for communicating during various ship’s evolutions, for example:
          1. General quarters,
          2. Refueling,
          3. Underway replenishment.
        3. Watertight hatches and scuttles.
        4. Life rings -- Mounted on the bulkhead for easy access.
      4. Man overboard procedures
        1. Any person who sees someone go overboard immediately sound the alarm, “man overboard, man overboard, port (starboard) side.”
          1. Throw a life ring or life jacket into the water, if possible. Keep the person in sight. If a dye marker is available, drop it into the water.
          2. Point directly at the man overboard. Continue pointing until the personnel on the bridge have the man overboard in sight.
        2. The officer of the deck (OOD) will maneuver the ship to a recovery position. At the same time, the word is passed twice over the 1MC announcing system, “Man overboard, man overboard, port (starboard) side.”
        3. Six or more short blasts are sounded on the ship’s whistle.
        4. All personnel will muster with their divisions.
        5. The quartermaster will hoist the Oscar flag.
    5. Forecastle
      1. Is a weather deck forward of the superstructure.
      2. The following equipment any be located on the forecastle:
        1. Anchor windlass and controls,
        2. Anchor chain and securing gear,
        3. Jack staff -- From which the Union Jack (starred blue flag) flies when the ship is not underway.
    6. Communication equipment
      1. Signal lights and flags are used to visually communicate messages to other ships and small boats.
      2. Signal flags are raised on a flag hoist using light lines called halyards.
    7. Bridge (pilothouse) -- Personnel on the bridge have operational control of the ship, including navigation, while underway.
  4. Line Handling Procedures
    1. Take in mooring lines in preparation for getting the ship underway.
      1. Station the special sea and anchor for getting the ship underway.
      2. When all personnel are on station and ready to get underway, make manned and ready reports to the bridge via sound-powered phones.
      3. Sound-powered phone talkers must establish communications (i.e., perform phone checks) between their station and the bridge.
      4. Remove bird’s nest.
        1. Line captain orders shipboard line handlers to remove bird’s nest down to figure-eight turns and fake line down on deck.
        2. CAUTION: Remove the figure-eight turns will let the doubled section go slack and can cause snapback condition on all lines.
      5. Single up all lines.
        1. Order received through sound-powered phones, given to POIC, and relayed to line captain. Line captain acknowledges order.
        2. Line captain orders ship line handlers, “Remove figure eights and round turn slack line.” When there is enough slack to remove line from bollard, order, “Avast slack.”
        3. Line captain orders pier line handlers, “Cast off line #.” Pier line handlers remove the dipped eye from the bollard.
        4. Line captain orders hip line handlers, “Heave around line #.”
        5. When line is through chock, line captain makes report to the POIC, “Line # singled up.”
        6. POIC relays message to OOD on bridge via sound-powered phone talker.
        7. Line captain orders, “Fake down line.”
      6. Take in all lines.
        1. Order received through sound-powered phones, give to POIC, and relayed to line captain. Line captain acknowledges order.
        2. Line caption orders ship line handlers, “Remove figure eights and round turn slack line”. When there is enough slack to remove line from bollard, order, “Avast slack.”
        3. Line captain orders pier line handlers, “Cast off line #.” Pier line handlers remove eye from the bollard.
        4. Line captain orders ship line handlers, “Heave around line #.”
        5. When eye of mooring line is through chock, line captain makes report to the POIC. “Line # on deck.”
        6. POIC relays message to OOD on the bridge via sound-powered phone talker.
        7. Line captain orders, “Fake down line.”
      7. Shift colors when the last line is cast off bollard.
    2. Mooring the ship to the pier
      1. Prepare for entering port.
      2. When all personnel are on station and ready to moor the ship, make manned and ready reports to bridge via sound-powered phones.
      3. Make up heaving line and prepare mooring line.
        1. Line captain orders shipboard line handlers to place an eye of the mooring line through chock and lay it over the top of lifeline.
        2. Make up heaving line and belay it to mooring line using a bowline.
      4. Over all lines.
        1. Order received through sound-powered phones, give to POIC, and relayed to line captain. Line captain acknowledges order.
        2. Line captain gives command to line handler, “Heave line to pier.”
        3. Ship line handler that is heaving the line:
          1. Warns, “Heads up on deck. Heads up on Pier. One-thousand-one, one-thousand-two, one-thousand-three.”
          2. Heaves the line to the pier.
        4. Line captain orders:
          1. Ship line handlers to “Pay out line #.”
          2. Pier line handlers to “heave around on line #.” Pier line handlers heave around.
        5. When enough line is out to the pier, line captain orders ship line handlers, “Avast paying out.”
        6. Pier line handlers place the eye of the mooring line over the bollard.
        7. Shift colors when first mooring line is over bollard.
        8. Line captain orders ship line handlers, “Put a round turn clockwise on the bitt closest to the check.” When completed and hands are clear of bitts, “Heave around line #.” (Repeat until line is tight.)
        9. Line captain order ship line handlers, “Put four figure eights on the bitts.”
        10. Line captain makes report to the POIC, “Line # singled up.”
        11. Automatically prepare to double up by placing second eye of line though chock; make up second heaving line and attach to eye using bowline.
      5. Double up all lines.
        1. Order received through sound-powered phones, given to POIC, and relayed to line captain. Line captain acknowledges order.
        2. Line captain gives command to line handler, “Heave line to pier.”
        3. Ship line handler that is heaving the line:
          1. Warns, “Heads up on deck. Heads up on pier. One-thousand-one, one-thousand-two, one-thousand-three.”
          2. Heaves the line to the pier.
        4. Line captain orders ship line handlers “Pay out line #.”
        5. Pier line handlers will heave around on line #.
        6. When enough line is out to the pier, line captain orders hip line handlers, “Avast paying out.”
        7. Pier line handlers dip the eye and place the mooring line over the bollard.
        8. Line captain orders ship line handlers, “Put a round turn clockwise on the bitt closest to the chock.”
        9. Ensure line handlers keep mooring lines tight. Line captain orders ship line handlers, “Fill bitts with figure eights.”
        10. Line captain makes report to the POIC, “Line # doubled up.”
        11. Automatically bird nest the remainder of the line starting from the top and working around both bitts. Place any line that will not go around between the figure eights and the bird’s nest.
        12. POIC relays message to OOD on bridge via sound-powered phone talker.
      6. Secure the sea and anchor detail.

  5. Marlinespike Internal Tour
    1. Anchor windlass room
      1. The space contains:
        1. Electric Motor -- Provides motive power to operate the windlass.
        2. Power Panels -- Electrical controls.
        3. Chain Locker -- For storing the anchor chain
      2. SAFETY PRECAUTIONS
        1. Stay away from the power panels; you may be shocked or electrocuted.
        2. Beware if moving machinery.
    2. Watertight doors (WTDs)
      1. With individual dogs
      2. Quick acting
    3. Berthing compartment
      1. All compartments aboard ship are designed to make the best use of available space and berthing compartments are no exception.
      2. Common items in berthing compartments include:
        1. Stand up lickers for military and civilian clothes storage.
        2. Bunk and locker combination (coffin locker)
        3. Emergency escape breathing devices (EEBDs) -- Provide oxygen for wearer to escape from a smoke-filled compartment.
      3. Berthing compartments have more than one entrance/exit for emergencies.
    4. Boatswain’s locker -- Used to store line and other deck related gear.
    5. Repair locker
      1. Functions of a repair locker include:
        1. Stowage of equipment needed for firefighting and repair of the ship.
        2. Command and control center for the fire party.
      2. The size and function of the ship determine the number of repair lockers on board.
    6. Pain locker -- A flammable liquid stowage space.
