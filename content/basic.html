  • C.Topic Outline
    • 1.Objectives of Shipboard Damage Control
      • a.Maintain the condition of the ship.
      • b.Contain damage from fire and flooding.
      • c.Perform emergency repairs to the ship and its equipment.
    • 2.Definition of watertight integrity -- A ship’s ability to resist flooding.
    • 3.Purpose of Watertight Compartments -- Every naval ship is divided by decks and bulkheads into watertight compartments to:
      • a.Control the spread of fire and flooding.
      • b.Withstand chemical, biological, and radiological (CBR) attacks.
      • c.Divide the ship into spaces.
      • d.Control stability and buoyancy.
        • (1)Stability -- the ship’s resistance to pitch or roll.
        • (2)Buoyancy -- the ship’s ability to stay afloat.
    • 4.Navy System of Ship Compartment Identification
      • a.The means of locating spaces aboard ship; like an address.
      • b.Made up of four parts:
        • (1)The first part designates the deck or level on which the compartment is located.
          • (a)Decks are numbered from the main deck, (1st deck) to the keel (bottom) of the ship; i.e., 2nd deck, 3rd deck, etc.
          • (b)Each deck above the main deck is called a level.
            • 1)The levels of the ship are preceded by a zero.
            • 2)The first level above the main deck is “01”; the numbers then go upward 02, 03, 04, etc.
        • (2)The second part of the designation identifies the forward - most frame in the compartment.
          • (a)All ships are built with frames (medal ribs) to support the ship’s structure.
          • (b)Frames divide the ship and extend port to starboard.
          • (c)Frame “1” is at the bow of the ship.
          • (d)Frame numbers increase moving toward the stern.
        • (3)The third part of the designation identifies the compartment’s relation to the ship’s centerline.
          • (a)Centerline -- An imaginary lengthwise line through the center of the ship.
          • (b)If a compartment is:
            • 1)On the centerline, its number will be zero.
        • (4)To the port of centerline, it will be identified by an even number; the numbers start adjacent to the centerline on the port side and increase outward towards port: 2,4,6, etc.
        • (5)The fourth part of the designation is a letter that identifies the compartment’s primary use. For example:
          • (a)A -- Storage space
          • (b)E – Engineering space
          • (c)F -- Fuel oil and lube oil stowage space
          • (d)L – Living space
          • (e)M -- Magazine/ammunition space
          • (f)Q – Miscellaneous space, e.g., workshops
    • 5.Shipboard Closures and Their Securing Devices
      • a.Watertight Doors
        • (1)Provide access through watertight bulkheads
        • (2)Two types of watertight doors:
          • (a)Doors with individual dogs.
            • 1)A dog is a small mechanical device used to secure a watertight door.
            • 2)A watertight door will have several dogs.
            • 3)Individual dogged doors are used to enter and exit shipboard spaces that do not have high use, such as paint lockers and storeroom.
            • 4)Quick-acting doors
            • 5)A lever operates all dogs at the same time.
            • 6)Quick-acting doors are used to enter and exit manned shipboard spaces that have a high use.
      • b.Watertight Hatch
        • (1)A horizontal opening for access through decks.
        • (2)Secured by individual dogs or drop bolts.
      • c.Escape Scuttle
        • (1)Located on hatches for quick escapes from a space; if a hatch had not scuttle and was secured, personnel would not be able to escape in case of emergency.
        • (2)A round opening with a quick-acting securing device.
        • (3)May also be found in decks, bulkheads, or overheads of spaces containing only one other means of access.
    • 6.Navy System of Watertight Fitting Identification
      • a.Every watertight fitting (door, hatch, valve) aboard ship has a number for identification.
      • b.Contains three parts:
        • (1)The first part indicates the deck or level on which the fitting is located.
        • (2)The second part indicates the exact frame number on which the fitting is located.
        • (3)The third part indicates the fitting’s relationship to centerline -- even port; odd starboard.
    • 7.Navy Material Conditions of Readiness
      • a.A ship has material conditions of readiness to represent different degrees of watertight integrity; i.e., a different percent of the ship is made watertight by closing doors, hatches, and scuttles.
      • b.Most material conditions of readiness are NOT to be broken without permission from higher authority.
        • (1)X-ray
          • (a)Represented by a black letter “X”.
          • (b)Set when the ship is in no danger of attack such as in well-protected harbors or when in normal during normal working hours.
          • (c)Provides the least protection against fire and flooding.
          • (d)Allows maximum freedom of personnel movement.
          • (e)All closures marked by an “X” are secured.
        • (2)Yoke
          • (a)Represented by a black letter “Y”.
          • (b)Set when the ship is underway (at sea) and in port after normal working hours.
          • (c)When set, all closures marked by an “X” or “Y” are secured.
        • (3)Zebra
          • (a)Represented by a red letter “Z”.
          • (b)Set for general quarters (battle stations).
          • (c)Provides maximum protection for ship and personnel.
          • (d)When set, secure all closures marked by an “X”, “Y”, or “Z”.
    • 8.Navy Special Purpose Damage Control Classifications
      • a.Are modifications to the three basic material conditions of readiness.
      • b.Types of special classifications:
        • (1)Circle X-ray and Circle Yoke
          • (a)Represented by a black “X” or “Y” in a black circle.
          • (b)When conditions X-ray or Yoke are set, close the appropriate marked fittings.
          • (c)Circle X-ray and Circle Yoke fittings may be opened without special permission when:
            • 1)Going to/from battle stations.
            • 2)Going to/from compartments containing equipment that requires inspection.
            • 3)Moving ammunition.
        • (2)Circle Zebra
          • (a)Represented by a red “Z” in a red circle.
          • (b)Close marked fittings when condition Zebra is set.
          • (c)Indicates fittings that may be opened ONLY with the Commanding Officer’s permission for:
            • 1)Preparation and distribution of battle rations.
            • 2)Limited use of sanitary facilities.
          • (d)There must be a watch stationed by an open Circle Zebra fitting to close it immediately after use.
        • (3)Dog Zebra
          • (a)Represented by a red letter “Z” within a black letter “D”.
          • (b)Marked fittings are secured:
            • 1)When condition “Z” is set.
            • 2)During “darken ship” to prevent light from escaping the ship at night. Dog Zebra fittings open to the weather decks.
        • (4)William
          • (a)Represented by a black letter “W”.
          • (b)Attached to vital system fittings which MUST be open during all conditions of readiness; e.g., sea suction valves, fire pumps, fuel valves, etc.
          • (c)Secured marked fittings to:
            • 1)Isolated damaged portions of the ship.
            • 2)Repair equipment.
        • (5)Circle William
          • (a)Represented by a black letter “W” within a black circle.
          • (b)Attached to vital systems.
          • (c)Marked fittings are normally kept open, except during a chemical, biological, and radiological (CBR) attack.
    • 2.Purpose of Damage Control (DC) Central
      • a.A shipboard compartment, which is the central site for coordinating efforts to fight shipboard fires and maintain watertight integrity.
      • b.Maintains damage control charts.
      • c.Ensures the proper material condition of readiness is set and maintained.
      • d.When there is damage to the ship, determines the ship’s condition and the appropriate corrective actions.
    • 3.Damage Control Documents
      • a.Closure Log
        • (4)A record of damage control closures and fittings that are in deviation from the material condition of readiness and special DC classification that are in effect. Every closure or fitting that is locked or inoperative shall be noted.
        • (5)Normally kept in/on:
          • (a)Quarterdeck -- When in port.
          • (b)Bridge -- When at sea.
          • (c)Damage Control Central -- During general quarters.
        • (6)However, if DCC is manned 24 hours-a-day, the closure log is always kept there.
      • b.Compartment Check off List (CCOL)
        • (4)A list of all damage control fitting in a ship’s compartment.
        • (5)A reference for personnel when setting the different material conditions of readiness.
        • (6)Posed near each access to a compartment.
    • 4.Methods of Damage Control Communication
      • a.Effective communication when conducting damage control procedures is vital.
      • b.The following methods of communication are used by DC Central and other damage control personnel:
        • (4)Sound powered telephones - Extensively used aboard ship for voice-powered communications.
        • (5)Ships service telephones
          • (a)Are standard telephones
          • (b)Could be easily knocked out during battle.
        • (6)Ship’s loudspeaker or general announcing system - used to pass warnings or vital information to the entire ship’s crew.
        • (7)Integrated Voice Communications System (IVCS) - A shipboard computer-controlled system, which can connect into other communication systems, such as ship’s general announcing system, sound-powered telephone circuits, etc.
        • (8)Messenger - a person responsible for carrying messages between the scene of a casualty and DC Central.
    • 5.Characteristics of Shipboard Emergency Alarms
      • a.General Alarm
        • (4)A series of single gong tones.
        • (5)When sounded, report to general quarters and prepare for an emergency.
      • b.Chemical Alarm -- A steady high pitched tone.
      • c.Collision Alarm -- Same tone as chemical alarm but sounds in a repeated series of three pulses followed by a short pause.
    • 6.Methods of Correcting Damage to the Ship During Emergencies
      • a.Shoring (portable wooden or metal beams) -- Is constructed to temporarily:
        • (4)Support ruptured decks.
        • (5)Strengthen weakened bulkheads, decks, hatches, and doors.
        • (6)Support equipment that has broken loose from permanent mountings.
        • (7)Patching and Plugging -- There are two general methods of making temporary repairs to holes in the ship’s structure; put something:
          • (a)In the hole (plugging).
          • (b)Over the hole (patching).
      • b.Patching is also used to make emergency repairs to piping systems; e.g., soft patch and jubilee patch.
    • 7.Procedures to apply a jubilee patch to a damaged section of pipe
      • a.Before making emergency repairs to a damaged section of pipe, inform DC Central and secure the cutoff valves on both sides of the damage to stop the fluid flow.
      • b.Obtain a sheet of packing and a jubilee patch from the repair locker. Packing is a material inserted between the damaged section of pope and the jubilee patch to prevent fluid from leaking.
      • c.Place the sheet of packing over the damaged section of pipe to extend over both sides of the hole.
      • d.Open and place the jubilee patch over the packing covering the damaged section of pipe.
      • e.Tighten the bolts to secure the patch.
      • f.Inform DC Central and request permission to open the cutout valves to allow fluid flow through the repaired section of pipe.
      • g.Use a jubilee patch for emergency repairs of a low pressure system only.
    • 8.Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Shipboard Damage Control
      • a.Courage -- You may have to display Courage when performing emergency repairs if your ship is under attack and in danger of sinking, for example:
        • (4)Erecting shoring.
        • (5)Patching ruptured pipes.
        • (6)Plugging a hole in the ship’s structure.
      • b.Commitment -- Be Committed to learning information about damage control to facilitate job performance, for example:
        • (4)Knowing the Navy system of compartment and fitting identification is essential to finding locations aboard ship.
        • (5)Personnel must understand the material conditions of readiness and special purpose damage control classifications to know which openings can be passed through when a condition of readiness is in effect.
        • (6)Survival of the ship and its crew may depend on personnel being able to recognize the ship’s emergency alarms and taking the required actions.
    • 9.Relationship between Teamwork and Shipboard Damage Control -- The ship’s crew must work as a team to:
      • a.Maintain watertight integrity -- For example, everyone must ensure that doors, hatches, and fittings are secure or open according to the material conditions of readiness and special purpose damage control classifications that are in effect.
      • b.During casualties, effectively communicate using sound-powered telephones.
      • c.Prevent fire.
      • d.Perform emergency repairs to the ship.
