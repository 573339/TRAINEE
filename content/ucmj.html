A. Introduction

The Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ) Article 137 requires that 78 UCMJ articles be explained to you.

C. Topic Outline

  1. Introduction
  2. Definition and Purpose of the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ)
    1. Definitions
      1. The Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ) contains the basic criminal laws of the U.S. military.
      2. The term “uniform” is used because the document applies equally to the U.S. Navy, Marines, Army, Air Force, and Coast Guard.
      3. Under the UCMJ, the U.S. Armed Forces can bring criminal charges against personnel who violate military law.
    2. Purpose
      1. Promote justice.
      2. Assist in maintaining good order and discipline in the armed forces.
      3. Promote efficiency and effectiveness in the military.
    1. Persons Subject to the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ)
      1. Article 2, Persons subject to this chapter -- People are subject to the UCMJ if they:
        1. Are members of the regular armed forces.
        2. Are awaiting discharge after an enlistment term has expired.
        3. Have volunteered (from the time of enlistment or acceptance into the armed forces).
        4. Have been inducted formally as a military service member.
        5. Are in the reserves.
        6. Are retired members entitled to receive pay.
        7. Are serving a sentence imposed by court-martial.
      2. Article 3, Jurisdiction to try personnel
        1. All persons subject to Article 2 of the UCMJ can be tried by the armed forces.
        2. A person may be tried by court-martial, even after leaving the service, for offenses committed while under the UCMJ.
    2. Rights of Service Members according to the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ)
      1. Article 137, Articles to be explained
        1. Certain UCMJ articles must be explained to each enlisted member:
          1. Within 14 days of initial on active duty.
          2. After completing six months of active duty.
          3. Upon reenlistment.
        2. Service members have the right to see a copy of the UCMJ.
          1. Will be made available to anyone for review upon their request.
          2. If you want to see the UCMJ while at RTC, ask your Recruit Division Commander (RDC).
      2. Article 138, Complaints of wrongs
        1. Service members who believe they have been wronged by their Commanding Officer (CO) have the right to file a complaint.
        2. If the CO does not act to correct a wrongful situation, the service member may complain to any superior commissioned officer.
      3. Article 139, Redress of injuries to property
        1. If anyone’s personal property is willfully damaged or stolen by another member(s) of the armed services, the Navy may reimburse the victim even if those responsible are not found.
        2. Complaints are made to the CO.
    3. Meanings of the Punitive Articles of the UCMJ
      1. Articles 77 through 134 are the Punitive Articles of the UCMJ.
      2. Most of the offenses are similar to law, such as rape, robbery, and forgery, are serious offenses. Sailors can be court-martialed and sentenced to the brig for committing these crimes.
      3. Some of the offenses for which sailors can be court-martialed are strictly military; for example, insubordinate conduct, missing movement, and mutiny.
      4. There are 61 punitive articles.
        1. Article 92, Failure to obey order or regulation
          1. Any person is punishable under the UCMJ who violates or fails to obey any lawful order or regulation.
            1. Lawful orders
              1. Must be given by competent authority.
              2. Require the performance of military duty.
              3. Must accomplish a military mission.
              4. Must promote the morale, discipline, and usefulness of members of the command.
              5. Must be directly connected with the maintenance of good order.
            2. Unlawful orders could:
              1. Direct the commission of a crime.
              2. Interfere with the private rights or personal affairs of others.
              3. Conflict with human rights that are granted by law.
            3. Regulations -- Many Navy regulations are included in the publication, U.S. Navy Regulations.
          2. Any person is punishable under the UCMJ who is derelict (negligent) in the performance of their duties.
          3. Examples
            1. As a result of failure to obey a lawful order by using racially explicit language, an Airman Recruit:
              1. Was required to forfeit ½ month’s pay $
              2. Lost their nuclear clearance eligibility and was reclassified.
              3. Received an entry in their service record to document the misconduct.
            2. As a result of failure to obey a lawful order by writing letters of a personal nature and having a personal relationship with a female Seaman Recruit while in a trainee status, a male Seaman Recruit was required to forfeit 14 day’s pay $
        2. Article 128, Assault
          1. Any person is guilty of assault who:
            1. Attempts or threatens another with violence or bodily harm.
            2. Consummates (carries out) the threats of violence.
          2. Example
            1. A Fireman Recruit was found guilty of assaulting a Seaman Recruit by pushing him into a locker and rack.
            2. The FR was awarded forfeiture of ½-month’s pay $
        3. Article 86, Absence without leave
          1. A service member is guilty of UA who, without authority, is absent from their place of duty.
          2. Example: For unauthorized absence from 8 March until 31 March, an Airman Recruit was:
            1. Placed on restriction and assigned extra duties for 45 days.
            2. Required to forfeit ½ month’s pay for 2 months $
            3. Recommended for administrative separation from the Navy following completion of restriction and extra duties.
        4. Article 112a, use, possession, etc., of controlled substances.
          1. Controlled substances -- Drugs or other substances that have their use restricted by federal regulations; e.g., marijuana, cocaine, etc...
          2. Any person who wrongfully uses, possesses, manufactures, distributes, imports, or exports a controlled substance may be punished under this article.
          3. Example: For wrongful use of cocaine as a recruit, a seaman was:
            1. Awarded forfeiture of $ pay per month for two months, and restriction and extra duty for 45 days.
            2. Processed for administrative separation from the Navy with an other-than honorable discharge.
        5. Article 121, Larceny and wrongful appropriation
          1. Larceny -- To take something from another person with the intent of permanently keeping the item.
          2. Wrongful appropriation -- To temporarily take something from another person without permission.
          3. Example
            1. A seaman recruit was found guilty of stealing two 675-minute telephone cards (value approximately $500.00) from another seaman recruit.
            2. The perpetrator was awarded forfeiture of $ pay per month for two months.
        6. Article 117, Provoking speeches or gestures.
          1. Any person who uses provoking or insulting words (such as saying or writing racially explicit names) or gestures towards another violates this article.
          2. Words and gestures properly used for training, efficiency, or discipline are exempt from this article.
          3. Example: A fireman recruit was found guilty of wrongful use of profanity and a sexual slur toward a seaman recruit. The recruit was:
            1. Awarded forfeiture of ½-month’s pay $ , restriction and extra duty for ten days.
            2. Administratively separated from the Navy.
        7. Article 91, Insubordinate conduct toward a warrant officer, noncommissioned officer, or petty officer
          1. Any warrant officer or enlisted member is punishable under the UCMJ who:
            1. Strikes or assaults a warrant officer, chief petty officer, or petty officer.
            2. Willfully disobeys the lawful order of a warrant officer, noncommissioned officer, or petty officer.
            3. Treats with contempt or is disrespectful in language or a person's behavior or manners towards a warrant officer, noncommissioned officer, or petty officer.
          2. Example
            1. A seaman recruit was found guilty of using disrespectful language toward a chief because of saying to her, “Chief, you can kiss my #$%.”
            2. The seaman recruit was awarded forfeiture of 1/2 month’s pay $ .
        8. Article 77, Principles
          1. Any person is punishable under the UCMJ who commits an offense or is involved in its commission.
          2. A person who aids a criminal or is responsible for someone else committing a crime is also punishable.
        9. Article 78, Accessory after the fact -- A person that knows a crime has been committed can be punished for assisting the offender to avoid capture.
        10. Article 80, Attempts -- Persons who attempt to commit a crime and fails can be punished under the UCMJ.
        11. Article 81, Conspiracy -- Any person who conspires with any other person to commit a crime can be punished by court-martial.
        12. Article 82, Solicitation -- Any person who solicits or advises another to commit any of the following, can be punished under the UCMJ:
          1. Desertion
          2. Mutiny and sedition (hostile action against the U.S. government)
          3. Misbehavior before the enemy.
        13. Article 83, fraudulent enlistment, appointment, or separation -- Any person who procures their own military enlistment, appointment, or separation by providing false information can be punished under the UCMJ.
        14. Article 84, Unlawful enlistment, appointment, or separation -- Any person who aids the military enlistment, appointment, or separation of any person known to be ineligible because it is prohibited by law, regulation, or order, can be punished under the UCMJ.
        15. Article 85, Desertion -- A member is guilty of desertion who:
          1. Without authority, leaves their unit or place of duty with no intention of returning.
          2. Quits their unit or place of duty to avoid hazardous duty or shirk important service.
          3. Without being regularly separated from one of the armed forces, enlists or accepts an appointment in another U.S. or foreign armed service.
        16. Article 87, Missing movement -- Any person who misses the movement of their ship, aircraft, or unit is in violation of this article.
        17. Article 88, Contempt toward officials -- Any commissioned officer is guilty of contempt toward officials, who uses threatening words against any of the following personnel:
          1. President
          2. Vice President
          3. Congress
          4. Secretary of Defense
          5. Secretary of a military department
          6. Secretary of Homeland Security
          7. Governor or legislature of any state, territory, commonwealth, or possession in which the officer is on duty or present
        18. Article 89, Disrespect toward superior commissioned officer -- Any person who behaves disrespectfully toward their superior commissioned officer is in violation of this article.
        19. Article 90, Assaulting or willfully disobeying superior commissioned officer
          1. A member is punishable under the UCMJ who:
            1. Draws or lifts a weapon or commits violence against a superior commissioned officer.
            2. Willfully disobeys a lawful command of his superior commissioned officer.
          2. In time of war, violation of this article can be punishable by death.
        20. Article 93, Cruelty and maltreatment -- Any person who is guilty of cruelty or maltreatment of any person subject to their orders is punishable by court-martial.
        21. Article 94, Mutiny and sedition -- Any person is in violation of this article who:
          1. Is involved in a mutiny.
            1. Mutiny -- An action intended to override lawful military authority or create a disturbance.
            2. Intended to be carried out by one person or in a group.
          2. Is involved with sedition; i.e., a group attempt to overthrow or destroy civil authorities by using violence.
          3. Fails to do their utmost to prevent mutiny or sedition committed in their presence, or inform their superiors.
        22. Article 95, Resistance, breach of arrest, and escape—any person who resists apprehension, breaks arrest, or escapes from custody or confinement is punishable by court-martial.
        23. Article 96, Releasing prisoner without authority -- A person is guilty of violating this article who:
          1. Without proper authority, releases a prisoner committed to their charge.
          2. Through neglect or design, permits a prisoner to escape.
        24. Article 97, Unlawful detention -- Any person who unlawfully apprehends, arrests, or . a person is punishable by court-martial.
        25. Article 98, Noncompliance with procedural rules -- Any person is guilty of violating this article who:
          1. Is responsible for unnecessary delay to the case of a person accused of violating the UCMJ.
          2. Knowingly fails to enforce or comply with the provisions of the UCMJ that relate to trial proceedings.
        26. Article 99, Misbehavior before the enemy -- Any member of the armed forces can be punished under the UCMJ who in the presence of the enemy:
          1. Runs away.
          2. Shamefully abandons or surrenders personnel, a place, or military property which is their duty to defend.
          3. Through disobedience, neglect, or intentional misconduct, endangers the safety of people, a place, or military property.
          4. Casts away their arms or ammunition.
          5. Is guilty of cowardly conduct.
          6. Quits their place of duty to plunder or pillage.
          7. Causes false alarms in any command, unit, or place under control of the armed forces.
          8. Willfully fails to do their utmost to encounter, capture, and destroy enemy troops, combatants, vessels, or aircraft.
          9. Does not provide all possible relief and assistance to United States and allied armed forces in combat.
        27. Article 100, Subordinate compelling surrender -- A person is in violation of this article who:
          1. Attempts to force a commander to surrender or abandon any place, military property, or member(s) of the armed forces.
          2. Strikes (surrenders) the colors or flag to an enemy without proper authority.
        28. Article 101, Improper use of countersign
          1. Countersign -- A secret word or signal used by guards or sentries to identify personnel wishing to pass.
          2. Parole -- A word known by guard supervisors to verify countersigns.
          3. A person can be punished under the UCMJ who in time of war:
            1. Discloses a countersign or parole to any person not entitled to receive it.
            2. Intentionally gives to another who is entitled, an incorrect parole or countersign.
        29. Article 102, Forcing a safeguard
          1. Safeguard -- Person(s) posted by a commander for the protection of personnel, places, or property of the enemy or neutral forces.
          2. Any person who forces (breaks) a safeguard shall suffer death or other punishment as directed by a court-martial.
        30. Article 103, Captured or abandoned property
          1. All persons shall secure for the United States public property taken from the enemy.
          2. Personnel cannot:
            1. Buy, sell, or trade captured or abandoned property.
            2. Engage in looting or stealing.
          3. Any person violating this article shall be punished by court-martial.
        31. Article 104, Aiding the enemy -- Any person can be punished under the UCMJ who:
          1. Aids, or attempts to aid, the enemy with arms, ammunition, supplies, money, etc.
          2. Without proper authority, knowingly harbors, protects, gives intelligence to, communicates with, or deals with the enemy, either directly or indirectly.
        32. Article 105, Misconduct as prisoner -- A person that is a prisoner in time of war is in violation of this article if they:
          1. For the purpose of securing favorable treatment by their captors, act to the detriment of fellow prisoners.
          2. While in a position of authority, maltreats fellow prisoners without justifiable cause.
        33. Article 106, Spies -- Any person in time of war who is found to be a spy will be punished by death; e.g., an enemy spy looking for vital information at a U.S. Naval Air Station.
        34. Article 106a, Espionage
          1. Any person who, with the intent to cause injury to the United States or an advantage for a foreign nation, transmits or attempts to transmit an object relating to the national defense to a foreign nation is guilty of espionage; e.g., providing the enemy with classified military photographs.
          2. Anyone found guilty of espionage will be punished as directed by a court-martial; espionage can be punishable by death.
        35. Article 107, False official statements -- Any person is punishable under this article who, with intent to deceive
          1. signs a false official document.
          2. makes a false official statement.
        36. Article 108, Military property of the United States: sale, loss, damage, destruction, or wrongful disposition — Any person is punishable under this article who, without proper authority, does or causes any of the following to U.S. military property:
          1. Sells/disposes.
          2. Willfully or through neglect, damages, destroys, or loses.
        37. Article 109, Property other than military property of United States: Waste, spoilage, or destruction -- Any person who willfully or recklessly wastes, spoils, or wrongfully destroys or damages any nonmilitary property of the United States is in violation of this article.
        38. Article 110, Improper hazarding of vessel
          1. Hazard -- To put in danger of loss or injury.
          2. Any person who willfully, wrongfully, or negligently hazards or causes hazarding to any vessel of the armed forces shall suffer death or other punishment as directed by a court-martial.
        39. Article 111, Drunken or reckless operation of vehicle, aircraft, or vessel -- A person is in violation of this article who operates a vehicle, aircraft, or vessel:
          1. While drunk or impaired by drugs.
          2. In a reckless manner.
        40. Article 112, Drunk on duty
          1. Any person, other than a watch/guard or lookout, who is found drunk on duty, can be punished under this article.
          2. watch/guards and lookouts are punishable under Article 113.
        41. Article 113, Misbehavior of sentinel or lookout—Any watch/guard or lookout who is found drunk or sleeping at their post, or leaves their post before regularly relieved, shall be punished by death (in time of war) or as a court-martial directs.
        42. Article 114, Dueling -- Any person who fights, promotes, intends to fight a duel, or has knowledge of a challenge and fails to report it promptly to the proper authority is in violation of this article.
        43. Article 115, Malingering -- A person is a malingerer who, for the purpose of work, duty, or service:
          1. Fakes illness, physical disablement, mental illness, or insanity.
          2. Intentionally inflicts self-injury.
        44. Article 116, Riot or breach of peace -- Any person who causes or participates in any riot or breach of peace is in violation of this article.
        45. Article 118, Murder -- Any person who without justification unlawfully kills a human being can be put to death or imprisoned for life.
        46. Article 119, Manslaughter -- Any person who:
          1. WITH INTENT to Kill or inflict great bodily harm, unlawfully kills a human being in the heat of sudden passion caused by adequate provocation is guilty of VOLUNTARY manslaughter. Example: Catching one’s spouse in the act of adultery and killing both parties.
          2. WITHOUT AN INTENT to Kill or inflict great bodily harm, is guilty of INVOLUNTARY manslaughter when unlawfully killing a human being:
            1. By negligence. Example: Conducting target practice and accidentally killing another person.
            2. While committing an offense other than murder. Example: A passenger in a speeding vehicle being killed in a crash.
        47. Article 120, Rape, sexual assault, and other sexual misconduct
          1. Rape -- Any person subject to this chapter who causes another person of any age to engage in a sexual act by:
            1. Using unlawful force against that other person;
            2. Causing death or grievous bodily harm to any person;
            3. Threatening or placing that other person in fear that any person will be subjected to death, grievous bodily harm, or kidnapping;
            4. Rendering another person unconscious; or
            5. Administering to another person by force or threat of force, or without the knowledge or permission of that person, a drug, intoxicant, or other similar substance and thereby substantially impairs the ability of that person to appraise or control conduct; is guilty of rape and shall be punished as a court-martial may direct.
          2. Subsections of this article include but are not limited to:
            1. Stalking
            2. Rape and sexual assault of a child
            3. Other misconduct such as:
              1. Knowingly and wrongfully viewing the private areas of another person.
              2. Knowingly photographs, video tapes, films or records by any means the private areas of another their knowledge. etc…
        48. Article 122, Robbery -- A person is guilty of robbery when they take something of value from another using fear or violence.
        49. Article 123, Forgery -- A person is guilty of forgery who, with the intent to defraud:
          1. Falsely signs a document or alters a signature.
          2. Knowingly passes a document having a false or altered signature.
        50. Article 123a, Making, drawing, or uttering check, draft, or order without funds
          1. “Uttering a check” is defined as giving a check to another person as a payment.
          2. Knowingly passing a check or draft that has insufficient funds to cover the amount can be punished under the UCMJ.
        51. Article 124, Maiming -- Anyone who intentionally injures, disfigures, or disables another is guilty of maiming; e.g., punching a person and knocking out their teeth, cutting a person.
        52. Article 126, Arson -- Any person who intentionally and maliciously burns the property of another is guilty of arson.
        53. Article 127, Extortion -- A person who commits extortion (blackmail) communicates threats to another with the intent to obtain:
          1. Something of value.
          2. Cancellation of a debt.
          3. Advantage.
        54. Article 129, Burglary -- Any person, who breaks and enters another’s HOME AT NIGHT with the intention of committing a serious crime, such as murder, rape, larceny, robbery, arson, extortion, or assault is guilty of burglary.
        55. Article 130, Housebreaking -- Any person who unlawfully enters a BUILDING or STRUCTURE of another (trespassing) with intent to commit a crime is guilty of housebreaking.
        56. Article 131, Perjury -- A person is guilty of perjury who in court or a criminal investigation:
          1. Lies under oath.
          2. Indicates a false document is true by signing it.
        57. Article 132, Frauds against the United States -- A person violates this article by committing frauds against the U.S., such as:
          1. Falsely demanding money from the government.
          2. Attempting to cheat the government by paying less money than is owed.
          3. Falsifying a receipt for government property.
        58. Article 133, Conduct unbecoming an officer and a gentleman -- Any commissioned officer, cadet, or midshipman can be convicted of conduct unbecoming an officer and a gentleman; e.g., making false official statements, and/or insulting another officer.
        59. Article 79, Conviction of lesser included offense
          1. An accused may be found guilty of a lesser offense when guilt for a more serious offense cannot be proven.
          2. For example, if you are found not guilty for “missing movement,” (e.g., not aboard when your ship leaves port) you could be found guilty of unauthorized absence (UA), a lesser offense.
        60. Article 134, General article -- Any act not covered in other articles of the UCMJ that violates good military order and discipline. For example:
          1. Adultery
          2. Fleeing the scene of an accident
          3. Pimping
          4. Prostitution
          5. Fraternization
    4. Apprehension and Restraint Articles 7 Through 14 of the UCMJ
      1. Article 7, Apprehension (taking a person into custody)
        1. Persons authorized to apprehend violators of the UCMJ may do so with reasonable belief that:
          1. An offense has been committed.
          2. The person apprehended committed the offense.
        2. Commissioned officers, warrant officers, petty officers, and noncommissioned officers have authority to quell quarrels, frays, and disorder among persons subject to the UCMJ.
      2. Article 8, Apprehension of deserters -- Civilian law enforcement officers may apprehend deserters.
      3. Article 9, Imposition of restraint -- No member can be arrested or confined without probable cause.
      4. Article 10, Restraint of persons charged with offenses -- a person charged with a minor offense shall not ordinarily be confined.
      5. Article 11, Reports and receiving of prisoners -- No provost marshal (military chief of police), commander, guard, or master at arms may refuse to keep any prisoner committed to their charge by a commissioned officer, when the officer furnishes a signed statement of the offense charged against the prisoner.
      6. Article 12, Confinement with enemy prisoners prohibited -- No member of the armed forces may be placed in confinement in immediate association with enemy prisoners.
      7. Article 13, Punishment prohibited before trial
      8. Article 14, delivery of offenders to civil authorities -- A member of the armed forces accused of an offense against civil authority may be delivered, upon request, to the civil authority for trial.
    5. Rules Governing Non-judicial Punishment -- UCMJ Article 15, Commanding Officer’s Non-Judicial Punishment (NJP)
      1. Also referred to as “Captains Mast.”
      2. NJP/Captain’s Mast is more serious than administrative corrective measures but less serious than trial by court-martial.
      3. When attached to a shore command and undergoing NJP, the accused may end the NJP proceeding and request a court-martial at any time until the punishment is awarded.
      4. When attached to or embarked in a vessel, the accused cannot refuse NJP.
      5. The accused may select a spokesperson as a representative.
      6. The CO (or their representative) is the only person who can award punishment.
      7. In a Captain’s Mast, the Commanding Officer has a great deal of authority, including:
        1. Dismissal of the case with or without warning.
        2. Referring the case to courts-martial.
        3. Non-judicial punishment within the limits of the UCMJ includes:
          1. Restriction to certain areas of a base or ship.
          2. An oral or written censure or reprimand.
            1. Oral or written censures of a person's behavior.
            2. Reprimand is more severe.
          3. Arrest in quarters -- (Only imposed on officers)
          4. Forfeiture of pay.
          5. Reduction in pay grade.
          6. Confinement on bread and water, or diminished rations.
          7. Extra duties.
          8. Correctional custody -- Can only be imposed on personnel E-3 and below; e.g., hard labor.
    6. Judicial Punishment (Court-Martial)
      1. A “court-martial” is a military trial, similar to trials in civilian courts.
      2. There are three different types of courts-martial.
        1. Summary (SCM)
          1. For trying relatively minor offenses.
          2. One officer acts as judge and jury.
          3. Only enlisted members get an SCM.
          4. Maximum punishments vary according to the pay grade of the accused and the offense committed.
        2. Special (SPCM)
          1. For more serious offenses.
          2. Consists of at least three members (trial and defense counsels, and a military judge).
          3. Punishments are more severe.
        3. General (GCM)
          1. For the most serious offenses.
          2. Consists of all members of the court (trial and defense counsels, military judge, and jury).
          3. Where the most severe punishments are warranted/available, including death.
      3. Court-martial personnel
        1. Article 25, Who may serve on courts-martial -- Any active duty commissioned officer, warrant officer, or enlisted member may serve.
        2. Article 27,Detail of trial counsel and defense counsel -- There will be a trial counsel and defense counsel for each general and special court-martial.
      4. Regulations pertaining to a court-martial
        1. Article 31, Compulsory self-incrimination prohibited
          1. Persons are not required to testify against themselves.
          2. Like civilian courts in the United States.
        2. Article 37, Unlawfully influencing action of court
          1. A person convening a court-martial or commanding officer cannot:
            1. Express disapproval of court ruling.
            2. Punish members of a court because of a ruling.
          2. No person may influence a member of the court in determining a ruling.
        3. Article 38, Duties of trial counsel and defense counsel
          1. Trial counsel -- Must prove beyond a reasonable doubt the guilt of the accused.
          2. Defense counsel -- Represents the accused. A civilian counsel may represent the accused; but the accused must pay the expense.
        4. Article 55, Cruel and unusual punishments prohibited
          1. Cruel and unusual punishments, like flogging (whipping) or branding, are not permitted.
          2. Irons (handcuffs/leg cuffs) are not permitted except for the purpose of safe custody.
    7. Purpose of the U.S. Navy Regulations
      1. The principle regulatory document of the U.S. Navy. It outlines the:
        1. Origin, authority, mission, and elements of the Department of the Navy.
        2. Procedures for conducting ceremonies, honors, and customs.
      2. Specifies procedures for awarding punishment, conducting Captain’s Mast, and anything unique to the Navy that is not covered in the UCMJ.
      3. Describes your rights and responsibilities as a Navy member.
      4. It is your responsibility to know Navy regulations; ignorance is no excuse.
    8. Types of Punitive and Administrative Discharges
      1. Punitive discharges -- Personnel convicted of a crime will most likely be punitively discharged from the Navy.
        1. Dishonorable -- Applies only to enlisted persons and warrant officers who are not commissioned. Awarded only as directed by a general court-martial for serious offenses that call for dishonorable separation as part of the punishment.
        2. Bad Conduct -- Applies only to enlisted persons. Awarded only as directed by a special or general court-martial.
      2. Administrative discharges
        1. Honorable -- Awarded to personnel with good to excellent service.
        2. General -- Awarded in the absence of a sufficiently meritorious military record to deserve an honorable discharge.
        3. Other than honorable -- Awarded when there has been evidence of misconduct and/or security violations.
    9. Reasons for General or Other-Than-Honorable Discharges
      1. Serious misconduct or a pattern of misconduct -- Could result in separation when there:
        1. Are two or more non-judicial punishments, court-martials, or civil convictions (or any combination).
        2. Are three or more UAs, each more than 3 days but less than 30 days.
        3. Is a failure to:
          1. Pay just debts.
          2. Contribute adequate support of dependents.
          3. Comply with civil court rulings concerning dependent support.
      2. Civilian convictions
        1. It is mandatory to administratively process someone who commits an act of violence that did or could have caused the victim to die or be seriously injured.
        2. Members may be separated based on civilian convictions when the:
          1. Offense calls for a punitive discharge for the same or a closely related offense.
          2. Civilian criminal sentence includes confinement for not less than six (6) months.
      3. Alcohol-related incident
        1. Defined as any behavior caused by using alcohol involving civilian or military authorities. It may also include public or domestic disturbances related to alcohol use or the need for medical care resulting from alcohol use.
        2. Members may be separated when they lack potential for continued Naval service and:
          1. Are unable or refuse to participate in, cooperate in, or successfully complete the required rehabilitation treatment program.
          2. There is an alcohol-related incident.
          3. Fail to follow a directed after-care program.
          4. Physical fitness assessment (PFA) failure -- Mandatory separation processing shall occur for all members who fail three PFA cycles in the most recent 4-year period.
    10. Relationship between the Navy Core Values and the Uniform Code of Military Justice.
      1. Service members occupy a unique position in society and represent the military establishment. That status brings with it a responsibility to uphold and maintain the military’s dignity and high standards.
      2. Service members must possess high standards of morale, good order, discipline, and cohesion. As a result, military laws, rules, customs, and traditions include restrictions on personal behavior that may be different from civilian life.
      3. The unique nature of military service requires regulation of behavior that brings discredit on the Military Services, adversely affects good order and discipline, or is inconstant with military customs, traditions, and decorum.
      4. The UCMJ is the legal foundation of good order and discipline in the Military Services and is used to enforce this higher standard.
      5. The Navy Core Values are the principles that define the standard every Sailor is expected to maintain. They relate directly to the UCMJ.
        1. Honor To behave within the guidelines of the UCMJ is the honorable duty of every sailor.
        2. Courage When tempted by others, have the courage to comply with the UCMJ.
        3. Commitment Be committed to behaving according to the rules and regulations of the UCMJ.
