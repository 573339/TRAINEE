A. Introduction

During your Navy career, you will encounter many different types of ships and aircraft. The types of ships and aircraft have evolved to meet the challenge of changing warfare and doctrine. This lesson is an introduction to a brief history and overview of the US Navy ships and aircraft mission and weapon systems. The following Naval Standards will be touched on in this lesson: H038, H239. NAVPERS 18068F, Naval Standards for E1 through E3 may be found in the Recruit Trainee Guide.

  3. Ships history, mission and weapons
    1. Mission--Varies with the type of combatant ship. Some engage primarily in combat with enemy forces, such as carriers, cruisers, frigates and submarines. Others have supporting roles, such as repairs salvage and delivery of supplies. The Naval Reserve forces also completely and independently maintain and operate many surface combatant ships. Their missions are the same as their active duty counterpart.
    2. Surface Combatants
      1. Aircraft Carriers
        1. History:
          1. Nov. 14, 1910, Eugene Ely, a civilian pilot took off from a wooden platform built over the bow of the light cruiser USS Birmingham
          2. March 20, 1922, the USS Langley (CV-1) converted from the collier USS Jupiter (AC-3) was commissioned as the Navy’s first aircraft carrier.
          3. June 4, 1934, the USS Ranger (CV-4), was commission as the first ship designed and built from the keel up as an aircraft carrier.
          4. WWII saw the utility, diversity and speed of the aircraft carrier become the backbone of Navy Task Forces, replacing the Battleship.
          5. The US Navy presently only has Nuclear Powered carriers (CVN) in commission.
        2. Mission-Carry, launch, and retrieve aircraft at sea.
        3. Weapons-Primary weapons system are the aircraft. Other offensive and defensive weapons include; Sea Sparrow, Phalanx CIWS, and Torpedo Counter Measures.
          4. USS George H.W. Bush (CVN-77)
      2. Cruisers (CG)
        1. History:
          1. The word “Cruiser” was first used by the English in 1651.
          2. By 1694, the term was used for ships detached from the main fleet to “cruise” and protect the harbor.
          3. The cruiser as a “named” ship, vice mission, evolved when the world’s navies phased out sail propulsion ships with wooden hulls, creating armored, steel “battleships”.
          4. With the emergence of naval strategy using the large battleship, the need arose for a middle-size combatant—the cruiser
        2. Mission:
          1. Perform primarily in a Battle Force role. Multi-mission, Air Warfare (AW), Undersea Warfare (USW), Naval Surface Fire Support (NSFS) and Surface Warfare (SW). Weapons
            1. MK-41 Vertical Launching System Standard Missile.
            2. Anti-Submarine Rocket (ASROC)
            3. Tomahawk Cruise Missile
            4. MK-46 Torpedoes
            5. MK-45 5-inch/54 caliber lightweight guns
            6. Phalanx Close-In Weapons Systems (CIWS)
            USS Anzio (CG-68)--(G -- Designates guided missiles)
        3. Destroyers (DDG)
          1. History:
            1. Destroyers came about in the late 1890’s as a defense against the new class of ship, the Torpedo Boat. Initially called the “Torpedo Boat Destroyer”, it was later shortened to Destroyer.
            2. Before WWII destroyers were light vessels with little endurance for ocean operations.
            3. After WWII, with the advent of the guided missile, destroyers took on a surface combatant role previously filled by battleships and cruisers.
          2. Mission: provide offensive and defensive support by:
            1. Detecting and engaging enemy submarines, aircraft and surface ships
            2. Conducting land attacks
            3. Performing patrol, search and rescue operations.
          3. Weapons:
            1. AVLS cells for Tomahawk, ESSM and Standard Missiles
            2. AGS 155mm guns, Long Range, Land Attack Projectiles, 155 mm rounds
            3. Mk-46 Close in guns
        4. Frigates (FFG) -- Mission is to:
          1. History:
            1. In the 18th century the term “Frigate” referred to ships that were built for speed and rated with at least 28 carriage mounted guns. These guns were mounted on a single deck.
            2. By the late 19th century, as “iron clad” ships were built; the term was still used because the principle armament was still on a single deck.
            3. The USS Constitution was one of the six original Frigates built and commissioned for the US Navy in 1794.
          2. Mission:
            1. Protection of shipping; anti-submarine Warfare (ASW) combatants for amphibious expeditionary forces, underway replenishment groups and merchant convoys.
          3. Weapons:
            1. MK-46 torpedoes
            2. 76mm 3-inch/62 caliber, MK-75 rapid fire gun
            3. Phalanx CIWS
        5. Littoral Combat (LCS)
          1. History:
            1. During the 1990s, the Navy realized the cold war cruisers and destroyers would be vulnerable along coastal waters as new threats materialized and Navy doctrine moved from deep water warfare to littoral operations.
            2. The initial concept was for a small single mission ship, but as “mission creep” increased it was decided a larger multi-mission ship capable of working close in to coastal waters was needed.
            3. This multi-mission capability was achieved by introducing the module concept where the ship would change out mission packages to fulfill a specific mission.
          2. Mission:
            1. With capability to change mission modules, the LCS is capable of countering diesel submarine threats, littoral mine threats and small surface craft attacks.
          3. Weapons:
            1. Both the Freedom and Independence class LCS carry the 57 mm/70 MK BEA systems capable of firing 220 rounds a minute.
            2. Some mission modules contain weapons systems.
        6. Mine Warfare Ships
          1. History:
            1. Up until 1917, the US Navy used sloops and tugs to locate and clear mines. With the entry into WWI, a special type of ship was needed to locate and clear mines.
            2. The USS Lapwing (AM-1) was commissioned in 1918, and became the first ship built with the specific mission of locating and clearing mines.
            3. At the beginning of WWII, a new class of wooden hulled minesweeper was commissioned. The Yard Mine Sweeper (YMS)
            4. USS YMS-143
            5. In the early 1980’s the Navy developed a new type minesweeper as part of the Mine Countermeasures Force, the Avenger Class- USS Chief MCM-14
          2. Mission:
            1. Clear mines from vital waterways
            2. Designed to find, classify and destroy moored and bottom mines, by using sonar and video systems
          3. Weapons:
            1. Mine neutralization system,
            2. .50 caliber machine guns,
            3. M60 7.62mm machine guns,
            4. MK-19 grenade launchers.
        7. Amphibious ships
          1. History:
            1. Prior to WWII, the US Navy did not have a single ocean going ship capable of discharging tanks or heavy equipment without a crane.
            2. The need to land troops and equipment from the sea forced the United States to focus on developing these specific mission ships.
          2. Mission:
            1. Land large numbers of personnel, equipment, and supplies on enemy shores.
          3. Types:
            1. General Purpose Amphibious Assault ships (LHA) Transport and land marine personnel using helicopters, landing craft, and amphibious vehicles.
            2. Multipurpose Amphibious Assault Ships, (LHD) Transport and land marine personnel and their equipment using helicopters, landing craft, and amphibious vehicles.Perform antisubmarine warfare using helicopters.
            3. Amphibious Transport Docks (LPD) Transport and land personnel and equipment using helicopter and landing craft.
            4. Dock Landing Ships (LSD) Transport and land; troops, cargo, amphibious vehicles, and landing craft air cushion (LCAC). Support amphibious assault, special operations or expeditionary warfare missions. Also, serve as aviation platforms for amphibious ready groups.
          4. Weapons (LHA, LHD, LPD, LSD)
            1. RAM missiles, Sea Sparrow, Phalanx, .50 caliber machine guns
    3. Submarines
      1. History
        1. In 1861, French inventor Brutus De Villeroi convinced the Union Navy that he could build a submersible warship. The 47-foot, oar-propelled Alligator became the first submersible Navy warship.
        2. The Alligator was unable to complete its first mission because it was underpowered, unwieldy and unsafe
        3. In 1900, the Navy purchased and commissioned the USS Holland (SS-1), propelled by a 45-horsepower engine with a 45-hoursepower engine with a speed of 6 knots. The Holland held a crew of six, carried three torpedoes and a topside gun.
        4. Submarines came into their own during WW II, with their success in destroying enemy shipping in both the Atlantic and Pacific.
        5. After WW II and during the cold war, the first nuclear powered submarine, USS Nautilus, was constructed. All US Navy submarines today are nuclear powered, with the last diesel submarine (USS Blueback SS-581) decommissioned in 1990.
      2. Mission -- Varies with the class of submarine.
      3. Classes: The US Navy has three classes of submarines.
        1. OHIO class (Ballistic and Cruise Missile)
          1. Identified as SSBN and SSGN
            1. SS–Submarine
            2. B–Ballistic missile
            3. G–Guided missile
            4. N–Nuclear powered
          2. OHIO class submarines, as well as early version Los Angeles class submarines, have “sail planes” above water; all others have the “planes” below water.
          3. OHIO classes are stealth submarines with the primary mission of nuclear deterrence. They are armed with Trident II ballistic missiles and MK 48 torpedoes.
        2. Virginia class (Attack) (SSN)
          1. Virginia class submarines are designed to seek and destroy enemy submarines and surface.
          2. Weapons include Tomahawk missiles and MK 48 torpedoes.
        3. Seawolf and Los Angles class (Fast Attack) (SSN)
          1. The Seawolf is the latest version of the Fast Attack submarine and will eventually replace the Los Angles class.
          2. The Seawolf and Los Angeles are multi-mission submarines from open ocean anti-submarine (ASW) to surveillance and intelligence gathering
          3. Weapons; The Seawolf and Los Angles carry Tomahawk missiles, MK 48 torpedoes and mines.
    4. Auxiliary Ships and Military Sealift (U.S. Naval Ships (USNS))
      1. History
        1. During the Mexican-American war both the Navy and Army chartered their own ships to provide supplies. This continued during WW I and WW II
        2. In 1948, the Secretary of Defense directed all military sea transport be placed under Navy command. It was then called Military Sea Transportation Service (MSTS).
        3. MSTS was renamed in 1970 to Military Sealift Command (MSC). In 1972, the first Navy oiler was transferred to MSC and manned by a civilian crew under Navy contract.
      2. Mission--Provide support services for the fleet.
      3. Types
        1. Submarine Tenders (AS) -- Service Submarines.
        2. Fast combat support ships (T-AOE, T-AKE) -- Carry fuel, ammunition, and supplies.
        3. Combat Store Ships (AFS)
          1. Refrigerated and dry stores
          2. Carry fuel, ammunition, dry and refrigerated stores
          3. Salvage ships (ARS) Salvage and search and rescue
          4. Special mission ships (T-OGAS) - Surveillance
        4. Salvage Ships (T-ARS) -- Assist ships in distress and salvage sunken vessels by:
          1. Towing Vessels.
          2. Performing manned diving operations.
    5. The Future
      1. Unmanned Surface Vessels (USV)
        1. There are four classes defined by size and mission.
          1. X-class; max size 3 meters, used for maritime interdiction operations, support and special operations.
          2. Harbor class; seven meter Rigid Inflatable Boat (RIB) with speed of 35 knots and used for anti-submarine defense, mine countermeasures, surface patrol, and Maritime interdiction operations.
          3. Snorkler class; seven meter semi-submersible craft with speed of 15+ knots. During operation submerged with only the snorkel above water and used for mine countermeasures, search/neutralization and ASW missions.
          4. Fleet class; eleven meter craft capable of 32-35 knots with a planning or semi-planning hull. The fleet class USV is used to mine countermeasures (MCM) sweep gear, electronic warfare, and surface warfare.
      2. Unmanned Underwater Vehicles (UUV)
        1. Remote Environmental Monitoring Unit System (REMUS)
          1. REMUS 100 class - Swordfish: Designed to search, classify and map shallow water areas.
          2. REMUS 600 class – Kingfish
            1. Increased endurance and advanced sensors over the 100 class.
            2. Littoral Battlespace Sensing (LBS UUV)
          3. Environmental survey of ocean, costal, and inshore waters operating at 70 hours at a speed of five knots and a depth of 1970 feet.
