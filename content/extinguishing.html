• A.Introduction
    • There are many different types of firefighting systems and extinguishers aboard U.S. Navy ships. The types of firefighting equipment will vary according to the different classes of ships. Sailors should be familiar with the major Navy fire extinguishing systems to protect themselves, their shipmates, and their ship.

  • C.Topic Outline
    • 1.Portable Fire Extinguishers
      • a.Navy ships are equipped with three types of portable fire extinguishers:
        • (1)Carbon Dioxide (CO2)
        • (2)Potassium Bicarbonate (PKP)
        • (3)Aqueous Film Forming Foam (AFFF)
      • b.15-pound CO2 portable fire extinguisher
        • (1)Purpose -- Primarily used to combat class “C” fires; however, can be used to extinguish small localized class “B” fires.
        • (2)Components
          • (a)Cylinder -- Holds 15 pound of CO2.
          • (b)Cylinder Valve Assembly
            • 1)Opened by squeezing the squeeze grip to activate extinguisher.
            • 2)To prevent inadvertent activation, the squeeze grip is protected by a licking pin with a tamper seal.
          • (c)Hose and Horn Assembly -- Used to direct the flow of CO2 towards the fire.
        • (3)Operating Procedures
          • (a)Hold extinguisher upright and on the deck.
          • (b)Remove the tamper seal and locking pin from the cylinder valve assembly.
          • (c)Grasp the squeeze grip.
          • (d)Approach the fire, as close as possible, from upwind.
          • (e)Squeeze the grip to open the cylinder valve and release CO2.
          • (f)Direct the CO2 toward the base of the fire and slowly move the horn from side-to-side.
          • (g)The maximum effective range of the extinguisher is four to six feet from the outer end of the horn.
        • (4)SAFETY PRECAUTIONS
          • (a)The “snow” that appears upon the release of CO2 toward exposed skin.
            • 1)Do NOT direct the flow of CO2 toward exposed skin.
            • 2)Grasp horn ONLY by its insulated handle.
          • (b)Ensure the cylinder is on the deck when discharging CO2 to protect the user from shock because of static electricity.
          • (c)18-and 27-pound PKP portable fire extinguishers
        • (5)Purpose -- Used to extinguish class:
          • (a)“B” Fires -- Primarily.
          • (b)“C” Fires -- As a last resort.
        • (6)Components
          • (a)PKP Cylinder -- Stores PKP extinguishing agent.
          • (b)PKP Cap -- Removed to fill extinguisher.
          • (c)CO2 Cartridge -- Stores CO2; CO2 supplies the propellant force to discharge PKP onto the fire.
          • (d)Puncture Lever -- Punctures the CO2 cartridge to release CO2 into the PKP cylinder.
          • (e)Locking Pin with Seal -- Secures the puncture lever.
          • (f)Hose and Nozzle -- Directs the flow of PKP onto the fire.
        • (7)Operating Procedures
          • (a)Pull the locking pin and seal.
          • (b)Push the puncture lever to charge the extinguisher.
          • (c)Approach the fire, as close as possible, from upwind.
          • (d)Squeeze the nozzle grip.
          • (e)Direct the chemical (PKP) at the base of the fire.
          • (f)The effective range of PKP extinguishers are:
            • 1)18-pound extinguisher: 19 feet.
            • 2)27-pound extinguisher: 21 feet.
          • (g)Use a rapid, side-to-side sweeping motion.
        • (8)SAFETY PRECAUTION: In a confined space, discharge the extinguisher in short bursts. Long discharges will reduce visibility and make breathing difficult.
      • c.AFFF Portable Fire Extinguisher
        • (1)Purpose
          • (a)Extinguish small class “B” and “A” fires.
          • (b)Protect a small flammable liquid spill from ignition by forming a vapor-tight seal over the liquid.
        • (2)Components
          • (a)Cylinder
            • 1)Stores 2 ½ gallons of a premixed solution of AFFF concentrate and fresh water.
            • 2)Also contains compressed air to pressurize the AFFF solution.
          • (b)Pressure Gauge -- Indicates pressure within the cylinder.
          • (c)Cylinder Valve Assembly
            • 1)Opened by squeezing the operating lever to activate the extinguisher.
            • 2)To prevent inadvertent activation, the operating lever is protected by a pull pin and chain with a tamper seal.
          • (d)Hose and Nozzle Assembly -- Used to direct the flow of AFFF solution toward the fire.
        • (3)Operating Procedures
          • (a)Check the pressure gauge to ensure it is within the indicated range.
          • (b)Pull the pull pin on the valve assembly.
          • (c)With one hand, carry the extinguisher by the handle; with the other hand, hold the discharge hose above the nozzle.
          • (d)To discharge AFFF, squeeze the operating lever.
          • (e)The initial discharge range of the extinguisher is 15 feet. This decreases during discharge.
          • (f)Move the nozzle from side-to-side applying the agent to the base of the fire.
    • 2.Standard Navy Fire Hose
      • a.Connects to a fireplug to deliver water to a fire.
      • b.One section of fire hose is 50 feet long.
      • c.Four sizes (diameter)
        • (1)1 ½-inch - for fighting fires.
        • (2)1 ¾-inch - Replacing 1 ½-inch hose.
        • (3)2 ½-inch - for fighting fires and dewatering.
        • (4)4-inch - Fore eductor overboard discharge when dewatering.
      • d.Has a male fitting at end (threads on outside); female fitting at the other end (threads on inside).
      • e.A hose must be coiled before it is stowed.
        • (1)Lay hose full length out on deck.
        • (2)Grasp male end.
        • (3)Double hose back on top of itself until male end is three to five feet from female end.
        • (4)Starting at the fold of the hose, roll hose inward on the part of the hose leading to the male end.
        • (5)The completed coil will have the female end on the outside of the coil and the male end on the inside; this procedure protects the threads on the male end of the hose from damage.
        • (6)Using two people to coil a fire hose will save time and effort (teamwork).
    • 3.Fixed CO2 Fire Extinguishing Systems
      • a.There are two types of fixed CO2 fire extinguishing systems installed aboard Navy ships:
        • (1)Hose and reel
        • (2)Total flooding
      • b.CO2 Hose and Reel Fire Extinguishing System.
        • (1)Purpose -- To extinguish class “C” fires in main machinery spaces.
        • (2)Components
          • (a)Cylinders -- Store liquid CO2.
          • (b)Flexible Hose -- Stowed on mounted reel.
          • (c)Horn -- Directs CO2 towards the fire.
      • c.CO2 Total Flooding System
        • (1)Purpose -- to extinguish large fires in spaces NOT normally occupied by personnel:
          • (a)Flammable liquid stowage lockers
          • (b)Paint lockers
        • (2)Components
          • (a)CO2 Cylinders -- Stores liquid CO2.
          • (b)Alarms and Indicators -- Sound or illuminate to warn personnel that CO2 is being released.
          • (c)Time Delay Device -- Delays the flow of CO2 for 30 seconds (non-machinery spaces) or 60 seconds (machinery spaces) to allow personnel to escape from the space.
        • (3)SAFETY PRECAUTIONS: For fixed CO2 fire extinguishing systems.
          • (a)Immediately leave a space when the CO2 warning devices are activated.
          • (b)Wear a SCBA when entering a space flooded by CO2.
          • (c)Halon 1301 Fire Extinguishing System
        • (4)Purpose -- Protects machinery spaces and flammable liquid storage rooms from class “B” fires that cannot be extinguished by initial watchstander actions.
        • (5)Components
          • (a)CO2 Cylinder -- Stores CO2; CO2 is used to activate the Halon system.
          • (b)Audible/visual alarms -- Warn personnel the Halon system has been activated.
          • (c)Time Delay Device -- Delays the release of Halon for 30 seconds (non-machinery spaces) or 60 seconds (machinery spaces) to allow time to secure the space and ventilation to shut down.
          • (d)Halon Cylinders -- Store Halon 1301.
        • (6)SAFETY PRECAUTIONS
          • (a)Evacuate the space when the Halon system activated alarm sounds.
          • (b)Halon produces toxic gases upon contact with flames or surfaces above 900oF.
          • (c)The release of Halon causes air turbulence, noise, obscured vision, and cold temperature.
          • (d)Do NOT enter a space flooded with Halon without wearing an SCBA.
      • d.Firemain System
        • (1)Purpose -- Used to supply firefighting water to the ship.
        • (2)Components
          • (a)Fire Pump -- Provides seawater pressure in the system.
          • (b)Pipes -- Through which seawater flows.
          • (c)Cutout Valves -- Used to isolate sections of the firemain that are damaged so that other sections can operate normally.
          • (d)Fireplug/Firestation -- Provides connections from the firemain to fire hoses.
          • (e)Y-Gate -- Can be connected to the fireplug to provide two hose connections.
            • 1)Starting at the fold of the hose, roll hose inward on the part of the hose leading to the male end.
            • 2)The completed coil will have the female end on the outside of the coil and the male end on the inside; this procedure protects the threads on the male end of the hose from damage.
            • 3)Using two people to coil a fire hose will save time and effort (teamwork).
        • (3)SAFETY PRECAUTIONS (firemain system)
          • (a)Do NOT use a solid stream of water to combat fires in energized electrical equipment.
            • 1)Water is a conductor of electricity.
            • 2)A firefighter could be shocked or electrocuted by electricity traveling from the affected equipment, through the stream of water, to the nozzle.
          • (b)Do NOT use a solid stream of water to combat Class “B” fires.
            • 1)Will spread the area of burning fuel.
            • 2)Could cause a violent fire reaction.
      • e.Aqueous Film Forming Foam (AFFF) System
        • (1)Purpose -- Used to protect spaces from class “B” fires, such as:
          • (a)Flight decks.
          • (b)Machinery spaces.
        • (2)Uses a solution of AFFF concentrate and seawater to fight fires.
        • (3)Components
          • (a)AFFF tank -- Stores AFFF concentrate.
          • (b)Eductor -- Mixes AFFF concentrate with seawater from the firemain.
          • (c)Non-collapsible rubber hose and nozzle.
            • 1)Stowed on a reel.
            • 2)Hose comes in two sizes: ¾-inch and 1 ½-inch.
        • (4)High Capacity AFFF System -- Larger tanks are installed on ships that require large quantities of AFFF because of additional outlets for:
          • (a)Hanger and flight decks.
          • (b)Well decks.
            • 1)There are well decks at the stern of some amphibious ships.
            • 2)They can be flooded to float and launch landing craft.
    • 4.Navy Vari-Nozzle
      • a.Three sizes:
        • (1)¾-inch
        • (2)1 ½-inch
        • (3)2 ½-inch
      • b.The bail is used to start/stop the flow of water.
        • (1)Open - Pull bail all the way back.
        • (2)Closed - Push bail all the way forward.
      • c.Can vary the spray pattern from a solid stream to a fog by rotating the black shroud at the tip end of the nozzle.
    • 5.Portable Inline Eductor (Portable AFFF System)
      • a.Purpose -- Inline eductor is used in a portable AFFF system to mix AFFF concentrate with seawater.
      • b.A flexible pick-up tube assembly is inserted into a can of AFFF concentrate.
      • c.Seawater, supplied by a P-100 pump or the firemain system, passes through the eductor and causes suction in the pickup tube which draws AFFF concentrate from a 5-gallon can.
    • 6.Relationship between Teamwork and Portable and Fixed Fire Extinguishing System -- Using teamwork (two people) to coil a fire hose will save time and effort.
    • 7.Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Portable and Fixed Fire Extinguishing Systems.
      • a.Commitment -- Be committed to learning information about portable and fixed fire extinguishing systems to enhance your ability to extinguish a fire.
