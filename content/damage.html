A. Introduction

  5. Navy System of Ship Compartment Identification
    1. The means of locating spaces aboard ship; like an address.
    2. Made up of parts:
      1. The first part designates the or on which the compartment is located.
        1. Decks are numbered from the main deck, (1st deck) to the keel (bottom) of the ship; i.e., 2nd deck, 3rd deck, etc.
        2. Each deck above the main deck is called a level.
          1. The levels of the ship are by a zero.
          2. The first level above the main deck is “01”; the numbers then go upward 02, 03, 04, etc.

  6. Shipboard Closures and Their Securing Devices
    1. Watertight Doors
      1. Provide access through .
      2. Two types of watertight doors:
        1. Doors with dogs.
          1. A dog is a small device used to secure a watertight door.
          2. A watertight door will have several dogs.
          3. Individual dogged doors are used to enter and exit shipboard spaces that do not have high use, such as paint lockers and storeroom.
        2. Quick-acting doors
          1. A lever operates dogs at the same time.
          2. Quick-acting doors are used to enter and exit shipboard spaces that have a high use.
    2. Watertight Hatch
      1. A horizontal opening for access through .
      2. Secured by individual dogs or drop bolts.
    3. Escape Scuttle
      1. Located on hatches for from a space; if a hatch had not scuttle and was secured, personnel would not be able to escape in case of emergency.
      2. A round opening with a quick-acting securing device.
      3. May also be found in decks, bulkheads, or overheads of spaces containing only one other means of access.
  7. Navy System of Watertight Fitting Identification
    1. Every watertight fitting (door, hatch, valve) aboard ship has a number for identification.
    2. Contains parts:
      1. The first part indicates the or on which the fitting is located.
      2. The second part indicates the frame number on which the fitting is located.
      3. The third part indicates the fitting’s relationship to -- even port; odd starboard.
  8. Navy Material Conditions of Readiness
    1. A ship has material conditions of readiness to represent different degrees of watertight integrity; i.e., a different percent of the ship is made watertight by closing doors, hatches, and scuttles.
    2. Most material conditions of readiness are NOT to be broken without permission from authority.
    3. X-ray
      1. Represented by a black letter “”.
      2. Set when the ship is in no danger of attack such as in well-protected harbors or when in during normal working hours.
      3. Provides the protection against fire and flooding.
      4. Allows maximum freedom of personnel movement.
      5. All closures marked by an “X” are secured.
    4. Yoke
      1. Represented by a black letter “Y”.
      2. Set when the ship is (at sea) and in after normal working hours.
      3. When set, all closures marked by an “X” or “Y” are secured.
    5. Zebra
      1. Represented by a red letter “”.
      2. Set for general (battle stations).
      3. Provides protection for ship and personnel.
      4. When set, secure all closures marked by an “X”, “Y” or “Z”.
  9. Navy Special Purpose Damage Control Classifications
    1. Are modifications to the three basic material conditions of readiness.
    2. Types of special classifications:
      1. Circle X-ray and Circle Yoke
        1. Represented by a black “X” or “Y” in a black circle.
        2. When conditions X-ray or Yoke are set, close the appropriate marked fittings.
        3. Circle X-ray and Circle Yoke fittings may be opened without special permission when:
          1. Going to/from stations.
          2. Going to/from compartments containing equipment that requires inspection.
          3. Moving ammunition.
      2. Circle Zebra
        1. Represented by a red “Z” in a red .
        2. Close marked fittings when condition Zebra is set.
        3. Indicates fittings that may be opened ONLY with the Commanding Officer’s permission for:
          1. Preparation and distribution of battle rations.
          2. Limited use of sanitary facilities.
        4. There must be a stationed by an open Circle Zebra fitting to close it immediately after use.
      3. Dog Zebra
        1. Represented by a red letter “Z” within a black letter “”.
        2. Marked fittings are secured:
          1. When condition “” is set.
          2. During “ ship” to prevent light from escaping the ship at night. Dog Zebra fittings open to the weather decks.
      4. William
        1. Represented by a black letter “”.
        2. Attached to system fittings which MUST be open during all conditions of readiness; e.g., sea suction valves, fire pumps, fuel valves, etc.
        3. Secured marked fittings to:
          1. Isolated damaged portions of the ship.
          2. Repair equipment.
      5. Circle William
        1. Represented by a black letter “W” within a black circle.
        2. Attached to systems.
        3. Marked fittings are normally kept open, except during a chemical, biological, and radiological (CBR) attack.
  10. Purpose of Damage Control (DC) Central
    1. A shipboard compartment, which is the central site for coordinating efforts to fight shipboard fires and maintain watertight integrity.
    2. Maintains damage control charts.
    3. Ensures the proper condition of readiness is set and maintained.
    4. When there is damage to the ship, determines the ship’s condition and the appropriate corrective actions.
  11. Damage Control Documents
    1. Closure Log
      1. A record of damage control closures and fittings that are in deviation from the material condition of readiness and special DC that are in effect. Every closure or fitting that is locked or inoperative shall be noted.
      2. Normally kept in/on:
        1. -- When in port.
        2. Bridge -- When at sea.
        3. -- During general quarters.
      3. However, if DCC is manned 24 hours-a-day, the closure log is always kept there.
    2. Compartment Checkoff List (CCOL)
      1. A list of all damage control in a ship’s compartment.
      2. A reference for personnel when setting the different material conditions of readiness.
      3. Posed near each to a compartment.
  12. Methods of Damage Control Communication
    1. Effective communication when conducting damage control procedures is vital.
    2. The following methods of communication are used by DC Central and other damage control personnel:
      1. Sound powered telephones - Extensively used aboard ship for voice-powered communications.
      2. Ships service telephones
        1. Are standard telephones
        2. Could be easily knocked out during battle.
      3. Ship’s or system - used to pass warnings or vital information to the entire ship’s crew.
      4. Integrated Voice Communications System (IVCS) - A shipboard computer-controlled system, which can connect into other communication systems, such as ship’s general announcing system, sound-powered telephone circuits, etc.
      5. Messenger - a person responsible for carrying messages between the scene of a casualty and DC Central.
  13. Characteristics of Shipboard Emergency Alarms
    1. General Alarm
      1. A series of tones.
      2. When sounded, report to general quarters and prepare for an emergency.
    2. Chemical Alarm -- A steady - tone.
    3. Collision Alarm -- Same tone as chemical alarm but sounds in a repeated series of three pulses followed by a short pause.
  14. Methods of Correcting Damage to the Ship During Emergencies
    1. Shoring (portable wooden or metal beams) -- Is constructed to temporarily:
      1. Support .
      2. Strengthen weakened bulkheads, decks, hatches, and doors.
      3. Support equipment that has broken loose from permanent mountings.
    2. Patching and Plugging -- There are two general methods of making temporary repairs to holes in the ship’s structure; put something:
      1. In the hole ().
      2. Over the hole ().
    3. Patching is also used to make emergency repairs to piping systems; e.g., soft patch and jubilee patch.
  15. Procedures to apply a jubilee patch to a damaged section of pipe
    1. Before making emergency repairs to a damaged section of pipe, inform DC Central and secure the cutoff valves on both sides of the damage to stop the fluid flow.
    2. Obtain a sheet of packing and a jubilee patch from the repair locker. Packing is a material inserted between the damaged section of pope and the jubilee patch to prevent fluid from leaking.
    3. Place the sheet of packing over the damaged section of pipe to extend over both sides of the hole.
    4. Open and place the jubilee patch over the packing covering the damaged section of pipe.
    5. Tighten the bolts to secure the patch.
    6. Inform DC Central and request permission to open the cutout valves to allow fluid flow through the repaired section of pipe.
    7. Use a jubilee patch for emergency repairs of a low pressure system only.
  16. Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Shipboard Damage Control
    1. Courage -- You may have to display Courage when performing emergency repairs if your ship is under attack and in danger of sinking, for example:
      1. Erecting shoring.
      2. Patching ruptured pipes.
      3. Plugging a hole in the ship’s structure.
    2. Commitment -- Be Committed to learning information about damage control to facilitate job performance, for example:
      1. Knowing the Navy system of compartment and fitting identification is essential to finding locations aboard ship.
      2. Personnel must understand the material conditions of readiness and special purpose damage control classifications to know which openings can be passed through when a condition of readiness is in effect.
      3. Survival of the ship and its crew may depend on personnel being able to recognize the ship’s emergency alarms and taking the required actions.
  17. Relationship between Teamwork and Shipboard Damage Control -- The ship’s crew must work as a team to:
    1. Maintain watertight integrity -- For example, everyone must ensure that doors, hatches, and fittings are secure or open according to the material conditions of readiness and special purpose damage control classifications that are in effect.
    2. During casualties, effectively communicate using sound-powered telephones.
    3. Prevent fire.
    4. Perform emergency repairs to the ship.
