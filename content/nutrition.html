Outline Sheet 2-15-1

A. Introduction

You are about to begin the lesson on Introduction to Nutrition. You will learn the benefits of good nutrition and how your nutritional status impacts your performance.

B. Enabling Objective(s):

  • 67.1 DISCUSS guidelines on changing nutritional habits.
  • 67.2 EXPLAIN the Navy Operational Fueling Series (NOFS).
  • 67.3 DISCUSS the five fundamental principles in developing a successful nutrition plan.

C. Topic Outline:

  1. Introduction
  2. Guidelines on Changing Nutrition Habits
    1. The foods you eat play a large role in how you perform, and how you feel.
    2. Poor nutrition leads to:
      1. Low energy
      2. Injury and disease
      3. Could result in early release from the Navy if body fat standards are not continuously met.
    3. Simple steps are provided to help develop your own plan to achieve your performance goals.
    4. If you want to be successful, use your time training to establish good habits that you can continue.
  3. Navy Operational Fueling Services
    1. Developed to provide a world class nutrition tool to simplify nutrition guidelines.
    2. Scientifically based and a collaboration between Navy Dietitians and the Athletic Performance Institute (API).
    3. Terminology used in this program is exactly what they use when teaching nutrition to professional athletes from all sports.
    4. Navy teamed up with API to bring this tool to Sailors.
  4. Five Fundamental Principles That Assist in Developing a Successful Nutrition Plan
    1. Eat Clean
      1. Come back to Earth -- Choose the least processed foods.
        1. “Whole” foods such as:
          1. Fruits
          2. Vegetables
          3. Lean Protein (meat, beans, nuts)
          4. Low Fat Milk or Yogurt (non-fat or 1%)
          5. Whole grains that include the word “whole” as the first ingredient on the food label.
        2. Processed foods are those found at most fast food restaurants and in the freezer section of the grocery store.
        3. They do not resemble the food that came from the ground or the animal. Think of a hot dog.
      2. Eat Color - Eat a variety of fruits or vegetables daily.
        1. Naturally occurring color in food means more nutrients.
        2. Try to have at least colors at each meal, such as:
          1. Yellow
          2. Orange
          3. Green
          4. Red
          5. White
          6. Blue/Purple
        3. Make half your plate fruits, vegetables and grains.
        4. Artificial color does not count, such as:
          1. Candy
          2. Food Dye on Icing
      3. The less the better - Include lean sources of protein daily.
        1. Choose protein from sources without legs.
          1. Fish
          2. Shrimp
          3. Black Eyed Peas
          4. Kidney Beans
        2. Choose protein from sources with two legs.
          1. Chicken
          2. Turkey
          3. Egg
        3. Limit the ones with four legs.
          1. Burgers
          2. Bacon
          3. Pork Chops
          4. Hot Dogs
          5. Sausage
      4. Eat healthy fats.
        1. Healthy fats provide energy and keep you feeling full.
        2. Choose most of your fat from plant sources.
          1. Nuts
          2. Oils
          3. Seeds
        3. Include Omega 3 fatty acids
          1. Tuna
          2. Salmon
          3. Walnuts
          4. Flaxseeds
        4. Limit fat from animal sources
          1. Butter
          2. High fat meats (bacon)
          3. Fried foods
    2. Eat Often
      1. Eat breakfast every day.
      2. Make every attempt to eat something at breakfast every day.
      3. It does not have to be breakfast foods.
      4. Choose a lean protein source and some fruit, dairy and/or whole grains.
      5. Spread calories out over the day to maintain energy levels and prevent overeating at night.
    3. Hydrate
      1. Stay hydrated.
      2. Drink water instead of sugary beverages, such as:
        1. Soda
        2. Sweet Tea
      3. Fruit Beverages, Electrolyte replacement drinks are not needed unless:
        1. Exercising for greater than 90 minutes.
        2. Working in the heat.
      4. Check your urine color to determine hydration.
      5. Urine that is the color of lemonade is good, apple juice is not.
    4. Mindset - The 80/20 Rule
      1. The goal is to eat well 80% of the time and enjoy other foods outside these guidelines 20% of the time.
      2. This can equate to 150-300 calories each day or including other foods at 3 or less meals per week.
      3. Enjoy your favorite foods, but eat less.
      4. Avoid oversize portions.
      5. Creating limits helps enjoy a well-balanced diet and your favorite foods.
    5. Recover
      1. Don’t waste your workout.
        1. Eat within 30-60 minutes after exercise to maximize energy stores and start muscle recovery.
        2. Eating right away prevents extreme hunger and overeating later.
      2. Sleep
        1. Whenever you can, get 7-9 hours of sleep.
        2. Chronic lack of sleep increases stress hormones in the body, decreases reaction time and does not allow the body to recover properly.
  5. Develop Healthy Eating Habits
    1. Now is the time to develop healthy eating habits.
    2. Pay attention to the regiment in training and practice making good choices.
    3. Try new foods while you are here, such as:
      1. Adding different vegetables to a salad.
      2. Adding fruit to whole grain cereal.
    4. You must continuously make an effort to ensure you fuel and hydrate your body well.
    5. Once training is over, the easy choice will be to return to old habits or turn to other foods, such as:
      1. Energy drinks
      2. Frequent fast foods
      3. High fat and sugar snacks that do not promote optimal performance.
    6. Remember that you can balance your favorite foods and still provide your body with what it needs.
    7. There will always be choices.
    8. Use these simple guidelines to make wise choices.
    9. Resources:
      1. Every command has at least one command Fitness Leader (CFL).
      2. CFLs have access to credible nutrition and fitness resources to assist in developing good habits.
      3. There is a lot of misinformation available.
      4. Seek assistance if you feel you need more guidance on how to improve your nutrition.
  6. Summary and Review
  7. Application

Information Sheet 2-15-2

Nutrition Basics for a Healthy Lifestyle

Important facts to remember:
  1. Energy Balance Is Key
    1. Energy in = Food and fluids consumed
    2. Energy out = Calories burned by the body
    3. If you take in more calories than you burn, your body stores the extra as fat and you gain weight.
    4. If you take in fewer calories than you use, your body burns stored calories and you lose weight.
    5. When energy in equals energy out, body weight is stable.
  2. Energy In – Manage Weight Through Good Food Habits
    1. Eat a variety of foods every day (Use the ChooseMyPlate.gov).
    2. Vary the colors in your diet by eating at least 5 servings of fruits & vegetables per day.
    3. Eat 3 meals a day to distribute calories for better energy utilization. Do not skip breakfast.
    4. Eat smaller portions of foods.
    5. Reduce high-sugar foods (sodas, candy, desserts, limit fruit juice).
    6. Limit high fat foods (fried, mayonnaise or dairy based sauces or dressings).
    7. Avoid a severe calorie restriction.
    8. Keep a diet journal when attempting to lose weight.
  3. Energy In – Carbohydrates
    1. The body uses carbohydrates for immediate energy.
    2. Carbohydrates (starch/sugars) can comprise up to 50%-65% of calories (5-7 g per kg body weight).
    3. Food sources include bread, cereal, pasta, rice, beans, fruit, and vegetables.
    4. Choose whole grains including whole grain breads, legumes, dried beans, fruits, vegetables.
  4. Energy In – Protein
    1. Necessary for rebuilding and repairing body tissue.
    2. Approximately 15% of calories should come from protein (0.8-1.4 g per kg body weight).
    3. Food sources are meat, fish, poultry, eggs, beans, milk.
    4. Choose lean sources of protein (non-fried, without skin).
    5. Protein is used for energy if fat or carbohydrate is lacking in the diet.
    6. Choose seafood at least twice a week as a main protein food.
  5. Energy In – Fat
    1. Fat is needed by the body for organ and nerve tissue.
    2. Fat in the diet carries the vitamins A, D, E, and K to be stored by the liver.
    3. Fat should be about 25-35% of calories, but only 7% of the calories should come from saturated fat or animal fat (butter, bacon, fried food, baked goods).
    4. Fat is calorie dense (9 calories/gram), so portions should be small.
    5. Limit animal sources including cheese, sausages, hot dogs, bacon and butter.
    6. Choose healthy plant fats including olive or canola oil nuts and seeds.
    7. Look for seafood rich in omega-3 fatty acids, such as salmon, trout, tuna, and herring.
    8. Do not strive for a fat free diet. Fat is essential. Moderation is the key.
  6. Energy In – Calcium
    1. Main source is dairy products (milk, yogurt, cheese and calcium fortified soymilk). Choose low-fat (1% or less) choices.
    2. You need at least 3 servings daily (1000+ mg).
    3. Calcium is good for maintaining strong bones and teeth. If you don’t get enough, bones can weaken – leading to stress fractures, broken bones, etc.
  7. Energy In – Water
    1. Needed by every cell of the body.
    2. Drink at least 8 cups of water daily.
    3. High protein/high sodium diets increase water needs.
    4. Drink more when increasing fiber intake to prevent constipation.
    5. Increase fluid intake when exercising a lot to replace sweat losses.
    6. All beverages except for alcohol, count – choose low calorie for better weight control.
  8. Energy In – How Do Vitamins Fit In?
    1. Vitamins and minerals do not provide energy – they can help our bodies get energy from the foods we eat.
    2. Needed to regulate body systems, build/repair tissue, prevent diseases, etc.
    3. Supplements are not warranted if you are eating a balanced diet with plenty of variety.
    4. Do your own research before choosing to take any supplement.
  9. Energy Out – Exercise Advantage
    1. Engage in aerobic exercise 3 – 6 times/week (walk, jog, cycle, etc.).
    2. Engage in strength training exercise 2 – 3 times/week.
    3. Increase lifestyle physical activity (taking stairs, parking far away).
  10. Energy Out – Exercise (FITT Rule)
    1. Frequency: Almost every day
    2. Intensity: The higher the intensity, the more calories you burn in a given time period. Work hard enough to get your heart rate up but still be able to carry on a little conversation.
    3. Time: 30 to 60 minutes
    4. Type: Aerobic and strength
  11. Energy Out – Plan Ahead
    1. Plan your daily schedule so that exercise is a regular part of the routine.
    2. Exercise burns calories and helps to manage stress.

What foods are in the grain group?

Any food made from wheat, rice, oats, cornmeal, barley or another cereal grain is a grain product. Bread, pasta, oatmeal, breakfast cereals, tortillas, and grits are examples of grain products.

Grains are divided into 2 subgroups, whole grains and refined grains.

Whole grains contain the entire grain kernel -- the bran, germ, and endosperm. Examples include:

  • Whole-Wheat Flour
  • Whole Cornmeal
  • Bulgur (Cracked Wheat)
  • Brown Rice
  • Oatmeal

Refined grains have been milled, a process that removes the bran and germ. This is done to give grains a finer texture and improve their shelf life, but it also removes dietary fiber, iron, and many B vitamins. Some examples of refined grain products are:

  • White Flour
  • White Rice
  • Degermed Cornmeal
  • White Bread

Most refined grains are enriched. This means certain B vitamins (thiamin, riboflavin, niacin, folic acid) and iron are added back after processing. Fiber is not added back to enriched grains. Check the ingredient list on refined grain products to make sure that the word “enriched” is included in the grain name. Some food products are made from mixtures of whole grains and refined grains.

What foods are included in the dairy group?

All fluid milk products and many foods made from milk are considered part of this food group. Most Dairy Group choices should be fat-free or low-fat. Foods made from milk that retain their calcium content are part of the group. Foods made from milk that have little to no calcium, such as cream cheese, cream, and butter, are not. Calcium-fortified soymilk (soy beverage) is also part of the Dairy Group.

*Selection Tips

Choose fat-free or low-fat milk, yogurt, and cheese. If you choose milk or yogurt that is not fat-free, or cheese that is not low-fat, the fat in the product counts against your maximum limit for "empty calories" (calories from solid fats and added sugars).

If sweetened milk products are chosen (flavored milk, yogurt, drinkable yogurt, desserts), the added sugars also count against your maximum limit for "empty calories" (calories from solid fats and added sugars).

For those who are lactose intolerant, smaller portions (such as 4 fluid ounces of milk) may be well tolerated. Lactose-free and lower-lactose products are available. These include lactose-reduced or lactose-free milk, yogurt, and cheese, and calcium-fortified soymilk (soy beverage). Also, enzyme preparations can be added to milk to lower the lactose content. Calcium-fortified foods and beverages such as cereals, orange juice, or rice or almond beverages may provide calcium, but may not provide the other nutrients found in dairy products.

Key: Switch to fat-free or low-fat (1%) milk.

What foods are included in the protein group?

All foods made from meat, poultry, seafood, beans and peas, eggs, processed soy products, nuts, and seeds are considered part of the Protein Foods Group. Beans and peas are also part of the Vegetable Group. For more information on beans and peas, see Beans and Peas Are Unique Foods.

Select a variety of protein foods to improve nutrient intake and health benefits, including at least 8 ounces of cooked seafood per week. Young children need less, depending on their age and calories needs. The advice to consume seafood does not apply to vegetarians. Vegetarian options in the Protein Foods Group include beans and peas, processed soy products, and nuts and seeds. Meat and poultry choices should be lean or low-fat.

*Selection Tips

Choose lean or low-fat meat and poultry. If higher fat choices are made, such as regular ground beef (75 to 80% lean) or chicken with skin, the fat counts against your maximum limit for empty calories (calories from solid fats or added sugars).

If solid fat is added in cooking, such as frying chicken in shortening or frying eggs in butter or stick margarine, this also counts against your maximum limit for empty calories (calories from solid fats and added sugars).

Select some seafood that is rich in omega-3 fatty acids, such as salmon, trout, sardines, anchovies, herring, Pacific oysters, and Atlantic and Pacific mackerel.

Processed meats such as ham, sausage, frankfurters, and luncheon or deli meats have added sodium. Check the Nutrition Facts label to help limit sodium intake. Fresh chicken, turkey, and pork that have been enhanced with a salt-containing solution also have added sodium. Check the product label for statements such as “self-basting” or “contains up to __% of __”, which mean that a sodium-containing solution has been added to the product.

Choose unsalted nuts and seeds to keep sodium intake low.

Hydration Needs

Daily Hydration Needs

  • Water should be your main source of hydration.
  • Avoid drinking soda, energy drinks, and fruit drinks. These beverages provide a lot of calories with very little nutritional value.
  • Limit sweetened teas and coffee drinks with added sugar and cream.
  • Only choose sports drinks before, during, and after INTENSE exercise.

Are you hydrated?

  • If you are thirsty, it is too late.
  • Evaluate your urine. It should be light in color and resemble pale lemonade.

To maintain hydration drink water:

  • 0.5-1oz fluid per pound per day (There are about 33oz in a liter (L))
