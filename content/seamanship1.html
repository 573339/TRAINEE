    16. C.Topic Outline
      • 1.Deck Equipment Terminology
        • a.Pier -- A structure extending from land out into the water that provides a mooring for ships.
        • b.Mooring lines
          • (1)Used to secure a ship to a pier or another ship.
          • (2)When a ship is moored it can be:
            • (a)Made fast to a mooring buoy.
            • (b)Secured to a pier or another ship with mooring lines.
        • c.Bollard – Post on the pier, over which the ship’s mooring line is placed.
        • d.Bitts -- A pair of metal posts on the deck of the ship; used mainly to belay (secure) the mooring lines.
        • e.Chocks – Fittings on the deck of the ship, through which mooring lines are led.
          • (1)Three types of chocks:
            • (a)Open – Open at the top.
            • (b)Closed – Closed by an arch of metal across the top.
            • (c)Roller – Contains rollers to reduce friction.
          • (2)When the ship is moored, mooring lines run from the bitts on the deck, through the chocks, and out to the pier.
        • f.Rat Guards – Metal disks that are secured to a mooring line to prevent rats from traveling over the mooring lines and onto the ship.
        • g.Cleats – Pair of projecting horns used for belying line or wire.
        • h.Fenders -- Cushioning devices used to prevent contact between the ship and pier or another ship.
        • i.Lifelines – Used to prevent personnel from falling overboard.
          • SAFETY NOTE: Do NOT lean on the lifelines.
        • j.Anchor – A device, used to hold a vessel fast to the bottom, but still afloat. Weight may vary from 30 lbs. to 30 tons.
        • k.Hawsepipe – Guides the anchor chain through the deck and out the side of the ship.
        • l.Anchor Chain – Metal links that connect the anchor to the ship.
          • (1)Links are connected to make up a shot of chain. A standard shot is fifteen fathoms long (fathom = 6 feet).
          • (2)SAFETY NOTES
            • (a)As a warning when the anchor is dropped, the next to the last shot is painted yellow. The last shot is painted red.
            • (b)Do NOT step over the anchor chain.
        • m.Anchor Windlass – A mechanical system used to raise the ship’s anchor and to handle lines or wires.
          • (1)The wildcat engages the links of the anchor chain.
          • (2)There are two types of windlasses:
            • (a)Vertical
              • 1)Used on combatant ships.
              • 2)Has capstan for handling lines/wire.
            • (b)Horizontal
              • 1)Used on amphibious and auxiliary ships.
              • 2)Has gypsy heads for handling lines/wires.
        • n.Brow – A ramp between ships or between a ship and a pier, that gives personnel access to the ship.
        • o.Accommodation Ladder – A flight of steps rigged and lowered from a ship to provide access to a small boat or pier.
      • 2.Terms Related to the Ship’s Structure
        • a.Hull – The outer skin of the ship.
        • b.Keel – “Backbone” of the ship’s hull.
        • c.Weather Deck – Any deck exposed to the elements.
        • d.Main deck – The uppermost complete deck on most ships.
          • (1)On aircraft carriers the hangar bay is the main deck.
          • (2)Flight deck – the area of a ship located on the weatherdeck where aircraft land and launch.
          • (3)Hangar Bay – the area of a ship where aircraft are stored.
        • e.Fantail – The after end of the main deck.
        • f.Foc’sle (pronounced “FOHKSUL”) -- The forward end of the main deck.
        • g.Superstructure – The portion of the ship that is above the main deck.
        • h.Bridge – A space in the ship’s superstructure from where personnel command the ship. Underway watch station for the Officer of the Deck.
        • i.Bow – The forward most part of the ship.
        • j.Stern – The aft most part of the ship.
        • k.Centerline – An imaginary line running down the middle of the ship from bow to stern.
        • l.Port – Left of the centerline when facing forward.
        • m.Starboard -- Right of the centerline when facing forward.
        • n.Outboard – Toward the side of the ship.
        • o.Inboard – Toward the ship’s centerline.
        • p.Waterline – The water level along the side of the ship.
        • q.Freeboard – The distance from the waterline to the weather deck.
        • r.Draft – The distance from the keel to the waterline. Measured by draft markings located at the bow and the stern of the ship.
        • s.Trim – The difference between the fore (forward) and the aft draft.
        • t.List – Port or starboard incline of a vessel; i.e., the ship leans to one side.
        • u.Quarterdeck -- Location designated by the Commanding Officer as a place to carry out official functions. This is the in port watch station for the OOD.
      • 3.Signal Flags and Pennants
        • a.Used to visually communicate messages to other ships and small boats.
        • b.Aboard ship, signal flags and pennants are flown on flag hoists using lightweight lines called halyards.
        • c.Types of flags and pennants
          • (1)Alfa -- Diver in the water. Keep well clear and travel at slow speeds.
          • (2)Bravo – Taking in, discharging, or handling dangerous materials (e.g., fuel, ammunition).
          • (3)Foxtrot – Flight operations in progress, fixed wing aircraft.
          • (4)Hotel -- flight operations in progress, helicopter.
          • (5)India – In port or at anchor, preparing to come alongside.
          • (6)Kilo -- Men working aloft. All radar and radio antennas in the vicinity are to be secured.
          • (7)Oscar – Man overboard. All personnel muster with their divisions.
          • (8)Papa – General recall. All personnel return to the ship.
          • (9)Quebec – Boat recall. All small boats return to the ship.
          • (10)Romeo – At sea, flown by a ship preparing to replenish.
          • (11)Third Substitution – Flown from the flag hoist when the Captain/C.O. is not aboard.
          • (12)Union Jack – Starred blue flag flown on the jack staff at the bow of a commissioned ship that is not underway.
      • 4.Man Overboard Procedures
        • a.Any person who sees someone go overboard immediately sound the alarm, Man Overboard, port (starboard) side.”
          • (1)Point directly at the man overboard. Continue pointing until personnel on the bridge have the man overboard in sight.
          • (2)Throw a life ring or life jacket into the water, if possible. Keep the person in sight.
        • b.The Officer of the Deck (OOD) will maneuver the ship to a recovery position. At the same time, word is passed twice over the 1MC announcing system, Man overboard, man overboard, port (starboard) side. All personnel muster with your divisions.”
        • c.Six or more short blasts are sounded on the ship’s whistle.
        • d.All personnel muster with your division.
        • e.The quartermaster will hoist the Oscar flag.
      • 5.Relationship Between Teamwork and Man Overboard Procedures
        • a.All crewmembers must work together to successfully recover a man overboard.
        • b.Any watch/lookout or ship crew who observes a crewmember go over the side immediately alerts the ship by calling out “Man Overboard”.
        • c.The OOD directs movement of the ship to perform search and recovery operations.
        • d.Quartermaster alerts other ships by raising the Oscar flag.
