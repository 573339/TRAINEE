A. Introduction

As a person new to the fleet, you will have to know basic deck terminology in order to communicate with others. Basic knowledge about the ship is a requirement for successful performance of your job. The information in this lesson will help you understand the new surroundings on your first ship.

The following Naval Standards will be touched on in this lesson: B273, H039, I310, I320. NAVPERS 18068F, Naval Standards for E1 through E3 may be found in the Recruit Trainee Guide

    3. Bollard – Post on the pier, over which the ship’s mooring line is placed.
    4. -- A pair of metal posts on the deck of the ship; used mainly to belay (secure) the mooring lines.
    5. Chocks – Fittings on the deck of the ship, through which mooring lines are led.
      1. Three types of chocks:
        1. Open – Open at the .
        2. Closed – Closed by an arch of metal across the top.
        3. Roller – Contains rollers to friction.
      2. When the ship is moored, mooring lines run from the bitts on the deck, through the chocks, and out to the pier.
    6. Guards – Metal disks that are secured to a mooring line to prevent rats from traveling over the mooring lines and onto the ship.
    7. Cleats – Pair of projecting horns used for belying line or wire.
    8. Fenders -- devices used to prevent contact between the ship and pier or another ship.
    9. Lifelines – Used to personnel from falling overboard.
      10. SAFETY NOTE: Do NOT on the lifelines.
    10. Anchor – A device, used to hold a vessel fast to the bottom, but still afloat. Weight may vary from 30 lbs. to 30 tones.
    11. Hawsepipe – Guides the chain through the deck and out the side of the ship.
    12. Anchor Chain – Metal links that connect the anchor to the ship.
      1. Links are connected to make up a of chain. A standard shot is fifteen fathoms long (fathom = 6 feet).
      2. SAFETY NOTES
        1. As a warning when the anchor is dropped, the next to the last shot is painted . The last shot is painted red.
        2. Do NOT over the anchor chain.
    13. Anchor Windlass – A mechanical system used to raise the ship’s anchor and to handle lines or wires.
      1. The engages the links of the anchor chain.
      2. There are two types of windlasses:
        1. Vertical
          1. Used on ships.
          2. Has for handling lines/wire.
        2. Horizontal
          1. Used on amphibious and auxiliary ships.
          2. Has heads for handling lines/wires.
    14. Brow – A ramp between ships or between a and a pier, that gives personnel access to the ship.
    15. Accommodation Ladder – A of steps rigged and lowered from a ship to provide access to a small boat or pier.
