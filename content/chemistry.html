  3. Extinguishing a Fire Using the Principles of the Fire Triangle/Tetrahedron
    1. General principle of firefighting -- The removal of any one of the elements of a fire triangle/tetrahedron will extinguish the fire.
    2. Methods of removing each element of the fire triangle/fire tetrahedron.
      1. Removing oxygen (air) -- Fire is by the application of:
        1. Non-combustible gases; e.g., CO2.
        2. Aqueous Film Forming Foam (AFFF) -- Forms a over the surface of a burning liquid to cut off the air supply to the fire.
      2. Removing fuel -- Prevents additional fuel from contacting the fire, for example:
        1. Closing supply lines.
        2. (pushing) a burning aircraft over the side of a carrier.
        3. Moving combustible materials to a safe area.
      3. Removing heat -- Lowers the temperature of the fire by using:
        1. Water
        2. AFFF
      4. Interrupting the chemical chain reaction -- By using or potassium bicarbonate (PKP).
  4. Characteristics and Extinguishing Agents for the Classes of Fire
    1. Fires are classified by the material they burn.
    2. Class Alpha Fire
      1. Involves solids, such as:
        1. Wood
        3. Cloth
      2. Leaves or .
      3. Identified by smoke.
      4. Extinguishing agents
          1. Most common available agent.
          2. Extinguishes fire by reducing the heat.
          3. Applied as a:
            1. Fog
            2. Solid stream
        2. Aqueous film forming foam (AFFF)
          1. A solution made of and AFFF concentrate.
          2. Forms a liquid foam blanket that creates a vapor-tight seal over the fire.
          3. Smothers the fire by cutting off the oxygen supply.
          4. Prevents reflash.
        3. Carbon dioxide (CO2)
          1. Smothers the fire by oxygen.
          2. Most effective in small confined spaces.
          3. WARNING: Does NOT prevent reflash.
    3. Class Bravo Fire
      1. Involves liquids, such as:
        2. Diesel fuel
      2. Also involves gasses, such as:
        1. Acetylene
        2. Propane
      3. Liquid fires are identified by heavy smoke.
      4. Extinguishing agents and methods.
        1. AFFF
        2. Potassium bicarbonate (PKP) dry chemical
          1. Extinguishes by the chemical reaction (as depicted by the fire tetrahedron).
          2. Most effective on small fires.
          3. WARNING: Does NOT prevent reflash.
        3. Water
          1. Water fog (narrow-angle fog pattern) -- Very effective for firefighting; however, must be applied directly to the area to be cooled.
          2. Straight stream -- Ineffective for extinguishing class “B” fires.
        4. Halon 1301
          1. Colorless, odorless .
          2. Extinguishes fire by interrupting chemical chain reaction (as depicted by the fire tetrahedron).
          3. WARNING: Does NOT prevent reflash.
        5. CO2 -- Characteristics same as before.
        6. Aqueous potassium carbonate (APC)
          1. Used in the ship’s galley to extinguish burning and .
          2. A soap-like solution.
          3. the fire.
          4. Prevents reflash.
        7. Secure fuel source
          1. Securing the fuel source is the most important step in controlling a gas fire.
          2. WARNING: Do NOT extinguish a gas fire with extinguishing agents unless the leak is small and the source can be quickly secured. If the fire is extinguished without securing the source, the unburned accumulated gasses could explode.
    4. Class Charlie Fire
      1. Involves equipment.
      2. The electrical should be secured before attacking the fire.
      3. Identified by , arcs, and sparks.
      4. Extinguishing agents
        1. CO2
        2. Water fog
        3. PKP
          1. Use only as a last resort.
          2. Will further damage electrical equipment because of corrosion.
    5. Class Delta Fires
      1. Involves metals and materials.
        1. Magnesium, for example:
          1. Aircraft landing gear, wheels, and engine parts
        2. Titanium; e.g., aircraft engine parts and fire walls
        3. Sodium
      2. Extinguishing agents and methods
        1. Jettison (throw overboard)
        2. Water fog
          1. SAFETY PRECAUTION: NEVER use a solid stream of water on a class “D” fire.
          2. Burning magnesium will break down a solid stream of water into its basic elements; that is, hydrogen and oxygen; and cause an explosion.
  5. Procedures to Prevent Fire and for Firefighting Readiness
    1. Fire prevention
      1. Be fire prevention conscious.
      2. Keep working and living areas .
      3. Keep electrical appliances in good working order.
      4. Report electrical wires to your superior.
      5. Safeguard all flammable materials.
        1. Do NOT store flammable materials near combustible liquids such as fuel oil.
        2. Keep heat sources, such as cigarettes or torches, away from flammable liquids. Both the liquids and their flammable vapors could ignite.
        3. Store flammable liquids, such as cleaning solvents and gasoline, in approved containers.
        4. and spills should be cleaned up immediately.
        5. Aboard ship, books, technical manuals, clothing, and other paper or cloth products should be stored in metal cabinets, drawers, lockers, etc.
    2. Firefighting readiness
      1. Maintain firefighting equipment in order.
        1. All fire stations must function properly and be completely equipped.
        2. All damage control lockers should be stocked with equipment that is in good working order.
      2. All personnel must be trained in firefighting.
