• A.Introduction
    • This lesson provides the basic information about lines and wire ropes that you will need for the basic seamanship laboratories. During your Navy career, you will be working or riding in small boats. This lesson discusses the types of small boats used by the Navy and the safety precautions for small boat passengers. In addition, this lesson will also teach you about sound-powered telephones. They are one of the most commonly used communication devices aboard ship.

  • C.Topic Outline
    • 1.Types and Characteristics of Lines and Wire Ropes
      • a.Natural Fiber Line.
        • (1)Manila – Superior to any other natural fibers in these four ways:
          • (a)Strength,
          • (b)Durability,
          • (c)Ease of Handling,
          • (d)Resistance to weather.
        • (2)Marline
          • (a)Commonly used aboard ship.
          • (b)Dark Brown.
          • (c)Made of two-strand, left-lay, tarred hemp.
      • b.Cotton -- Used for tying down objects.
      • c.Synthetic Fiber (man-made) Line – Most large lines aboard ship are made of synthetic fibers; i.e., mooring lines.
        • (1)Polyester
          • (a)fuzzy in appearance.
          • (b)Almost as strong as nylon, but has less stretch ability.
          • (c)Used for halyards and other applications requiring strength and minimum stretch.
        • (2)Nylon
          • (a)White and shiny; feels slick.
          • (b)Almost three (3) times stronger than manila line of the same size.
          • (c)If nylon line is stretched 40% to 65% of its original length, it will part. Upon parting, it will return instantaneously to its original length (snapback) with deadly force.
      • d.Wire Rope
        • (1)Made by twisting long steel wires around a core.
        • (2)Uses include lifelines, elevators, and cranes.
      • e.Line and wire rope sizes
        • (1)Line less than 1¾ inches in circumference is called “small stuff”. Marline is an example of small stuff; designated by name with no reference to size.
        • (2)Fiber lines are identified by their circumference and type; e.g., 2-inch manila, 3-inch nylon.
        • (3)Any line over 5 inches in circumference is called “hawser”.
        • (4)Wire rope is measured by its diameter.
        • (5)Bitter and Standing Ends
          • (a)Bitter end: End of the line that is tied off.
          • (b)Standing end: Longer part of the line that is under load after the knot/hitch is tied.
    • 2.Purpose of Bowline, Bowline on a bight, Clove Hitch, and Square Knot.
      • a.KNOTS: Knots are used to form eyes or to secure a cord or line around an object, such as a package. In other words, the line is bent to itself.
        • (1)A neckerchief
        • (2)The drawstring of a sweatshirt.
      • b.HITCHES: Hitches are used to bend a line to or around an object, such as a ring, spar, or stanchion.
    • 3.Procedures to Secure Equipment for Sea.
      • a.When going to sea, you will need to secure equipment to prevent damage from rough waters. How equipment is secured depends on the gear and the place of stowage.
        • (1)Use line strong enough to hold the equipment. Make sure the line is in good condition.
        • (2)Belay the objects from at least two points that are near the object.
        • (3)Secure all objects tightly against something solid (such as a bulkhead).
        • (4)Use chafing gear on sharp corners and rough surfaces.
      • b.Frequently check all secured lines and tighten as necessary.
      • c.SAFETY NOTES
        • (1)Never secure a line to electrical cables, small pipes, electric motors, lifeline stanchions or anything not solidly secured.
        • (2)Never block access to vents, fireplugs, switches, valves, doors, or hatches.
    • 5.Basic Line Handling and Anchoring Terms
      • a.Ways to make line/rope ready for use.
        • (1)Coil Down -- Laying the line up in circles, roughly one on top of the other.
        • (2)Fake Down -- Laying the line out in long, flat bights, one alongside the other. The main advantage of working with line that is faked down is that it runs off more easily.
        • (3)Flemish down – Starting with one end, lay circles of line on the deck, in a tight spiral.
      • b.Alongside – By the side of a pier or ship.
      • c.Anchorage -- An area designated to be used by ships for anchoring.
      • d.Anchors Aweigh – When the ship’s anchors are clear of the bottom and the ship bears the weight of the anchor.
      • e.Chafing gear – Material used to protect a line from excessive wear.
      • f.Single up -- Single sections of line between the ship and the pier.
      • g.Dip the eye -- Mooring lines are placed over the same bollard; the second line is led up through the eye of the first; allows either line to be cast off without disturbing the other.
      • h.Double Up – Double sections of line between the ship and pier.
      • i.Belay – To secure a line to a fixed point, such as a cleat.
      • j.Cast Off – An order to let go, or throw off, mooring lines from a bollard.
      • k.Check – To ease out just enough line to prevent it from parting under strain.
      • l.Heave Around – To pull in a line.
      • m.Avast – Stop, as in avast heaving around”; i.e., stop pulling in line.
      • n.Heave – To throw.
      • o.Heaving Line – A small line with an orange ball at one end. The heaving line is thrown across a space; e.g., from the ship to pier, so that a mooring line can be pulled across.
      • p.Pay Out -- To feed out or lengthen a line.
      • q.Slack -- to allow a line to run out.
      • r.Bird’s Nest – Excess mooring line is wrapped around the bits to prevent trip hazards and surface abrasion of the line.

    • 7.Purpose and Characteristics of Sound-Powered Telephone System
      • a.Sound-powered telephones are extensively used aboard ship for voice communications.
      • b.They operate from the sound pressure of the user’s voice thus the name “sound-powered” telephone; they have no batteries or external power source.
      • c.Aboard ship, groups of sound-powered telephones are organized into circuits.
        • (1)Each ship has various self-contained circuits.
        • (2)The mission of the ship determines the number of circuits.
        • (3)Each circuit has a control station where a person monitors and manages communications.
      • d.There are two types of sound-powered telephones:
        • (1)Handset
        • (2)Headset
    • 8.Components of Sound-Powered Telephone (Headset)
      • a.Transmitter -- Operator speaks into this component to send a message.
      • b.Transmitter Button -- Must be depressed to send messages.
      • c.Receivers
        • (1)Receives messages.
        • (2)Can be used to send messages if the transmitter fails.
      • d.Headband
        • (1)Adjustable band.
        • (2)Holds receivers over the wearers ears.
      • e.Yoke -- Holds transmitter in front of mouth.
      • f.Breastplate -- Supports the yoke.
      • g.Neck strap -- Supports breastplate.
      • h.Cord -- Connects transmitter and receivers to the jack.
      • i.Jack -- Plugs into jack box to connect headset to the circuit.

    • 10.Terminology Associated With Sound-Powered Telephone Communication
      • a.“Phone Check” -- Used by the talker at control to find out if stations are manned and ready.
      • b.“On the line”
        • (1)Used to establish communications immediately after plugging in the phone.
        • (2)Tells all talkers on the line/circuit that you are ready to communicate.
      • c.“Silence on the line”
        • (1)Used in emergencies to interrupt a message already in progress, so a vital message can be sent; e.g., reporting a fire.
        • (2)May be used by the control telephone talker to maintain circuit discipline.
      • d.“Belay my last” -- Used to cancel a message before it is completed and acknowledged.
      • e.“Affirmative” -- Used in response to a message requiring a “yes” answer.
      • f.“Negative” -- Used in response to a message requiring a “no” answer.
      • g.“Wait one” -- Used to respond to a message that the receiving talker cannot answer immediately.
      • h.“Request permission to change phone talkers” -- Used by a sound-powered telephone talker to request permission to be relieved by another phone talker.
      • i.“Back on the line” -- Used to indicate that a station has rejoined the circuit.
      • j.“Say Again” -- Used when the message was not understood or a portion or the entire message was missed.
      • k.“Request permission to secure phones” -- Informs the control station that the reporting station is requesting to secure.
    • 11.Procedures for Sending and Receiving Messages
      • a.Sending
        • (1)Three parts:
          • (a)Name of station being called.
          • (b)Identify your station.
          • (c)Send the message.
        • (2)There is no time lapse between naming the station you called and the end of message. Example: “Quarterdeck-- bridge, secure the brow.”
      • b.Receiving
        • (1)Three parts:
          • (a)Repeat back the order or the report to the originator verbatim, so the sender knows that you heard the message correctly.
          • (b)Identify your station.
          • (c)Acknowledge a message sent to you by saying “aye”.
        • (2)Example: “Secure the brow, quarterdeck, aye.”
