A. Introduction

We conduct dangerous or risky actions without realizing it. In the Navy, risky behavior can endanger the lives of many. Operational Risk Management (ORM) is an integral part of the decision-making process for all Navy personnel, on or off duty. TCRM refers to apply ORM at the point of commencing or during execution of a mission or task.

C. Topic Outline:

  1. Introduction
  2. What is Time-Critical Risk Management (TCRM)?
    1. Time Critical Risk Management (TCRM) is using risk management concepts when time and resources are limited.
      1. The ABCD Model is based on fundamental scientific principles, ability to focus individuals, increase their situational awareness (SA), and improve their performance in the time critical environment.
        1. TCRM model consists of graphic representations or icons and the easy to remember mnemonic ABCD.
          1. A-Assess the situation.
          2. B-Balance Resources.
          3. C-Communicate to others.
          4. D-Do the event and Debrief.
      2. TCRM plays a role in improving our chance of completing the task or mission successfully.
    2. The nature of TCRM decisions includes an understanding that:
      1. Using the ABCD Model daily creates a habit and trains the brain to continue thinking under stress or duress. The model is designed to assist when:
        1. Working in a changing environment.
        2. Monitoring a static or routine situation to capture errors.
        3. Making a decision with partial information.
      2. In all three situations, it’s necessary to develop habits that trigger the TCRM process to “Assess”, “Balance”, “Communicate”, “Do and Debrief” the event continuously as necessary.
  3. The ABCD Model of Risk Management
    1. The visual reminder or ABCD (icon) assists in the recall of the concepts and the principles behind the ABCD’s and improves communications during the event.
    2. The Model provides a common language and structure for a measured response when an individual, team or crew is executing a routine task, or when under stress from a more complex situation.
      1. In the execution of the task or mission, where time and resources are most limited, an individual must be proactive and responsive to ensure success.
        1. The ABCD model establishes a structure for standardized thought and communication while executing a task.
        2. Using the ABCD model daily will result in deep memory of those learned qualities.
        3. When executing tasks, leadership and team members have an expectation of a response similar to their experience.
      2. Assess the situation - In a real-time situation, it is essential to consider the event.
        1. Are things going as planned?
        2. What’s different from the plan?
        3. What’s confusing me?
        4. What will happen next?
    3. Situational Awareness (SA)
      1. Situational Awareness refers to the degree of accuracy by which your perception of the current environment mirrors reality.
        1. What’s going on?
        2. What’s going to happen next?
        3. Real risk vs. perceived risk.
    4. Situational Awareness-Risk.
      1. Risk is gauged in three colors:
        1. Green -- Little chance of serious error. Errors may be made but should be caught by the individual.
        2. Yellow -- Higher chance of serious error. Stressed or less focused on task and surroundings. Errors possibly not caught.
        3. Red -- Very high chance of serious error. Error that cannot be caught.
        4. Swooshing Arrow -- Return to good situation awareness.
      2. The target is used during team communication to focus one or more individuals on an understanding of situational conditions or an individual can use it as a self-assessment.
    5. Warning signs of Individual Risk
      1. Operating a vehicle
        1. Making errors when tired or sleepy.
        2. Not wearing a seat belt.
      2. Everyday work/leisure
        1. Distraction or loss of focus.
        2. Use of improper procedures.
        3. No one watching or looking for hazards.
        4. Failure to meet planned targets.
    6. How to identify when shipmates are in the Yellow or Red
      1. Making errors
      2. Poor Communication
      3. Overloaded
      4. Fatigued
      5. Complacency
  4. Not following procedures
  5. Balance Resources
    1. What are my options?
    2. How do I use them?
    3. Knowingly make decisions to mitigate risk.
      1. Prior planning ensures more resources are available.
      2. Discuss resources available to reduce risks:
        1. Checklists
        2. Written Procedures
        3. Job Sheets
    4. Resource Blocks
      1. The blocks represent multiple levels of resources. They provide structure and a pattern for you and the team to learn.
        1. Red Block: Policies, procedures, and routines. Example: General Orders, Recruit Guide and making your bunk.
        2. Green Block: Checklists. Example: Instructions for rack makeup.
        3. Aqua Block: Automation. Example: Fire alarms, traffic lights.
        4. Yellow Block: Briefings and external resources. Example: RDC, shipmate, Chaplin.
        5. Gray Block: Knowledge, skill and attitude. Example: Training, practice drills, PT.
    5. Communication
      1. Ensure everyone knows what is expected and what could go wrong. Communicate while the event is going on to help everyone maintain SA.
        1. In the Green-Ability to maintain Situational Awareness.
        2. In the Yellow-Decrease in Situational Awareness.
        3. In the Red-Situational Awareness continues to fall and tunnel vision occurs.
        4. Getting back in the Green-Effective communication allows you to get back in the green.
        5. Do the mission and Debrief-Do and use the appropriate resources to mitigate risk.
        6. You’re part of a team.
          1. Know your duties and duties of others
          2. Maintain SA
          3. Communicate
          4. Be observant of stress levels
