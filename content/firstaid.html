C. Topic Outline:

  1. Introduction
  2. Definition of First Aid
    1. First aid is emergency care of the sick or injured.
  3. Provides only temporary assistance or treatment until medical help is available.
  4. Purposes and Objectives of First Aid
    1. Purposes of First Aid
      1. Save life.
      2. Prevent further injury. When assisting an injured person, you must know what to do and what not to do.
      3. Prevent or minimize infection.
    2. Primary objectives are to maintain:
      1. Airway
      2. Breathing
      3. Circulation
  5. General Rules of First Aid -- Specific injuries requiring first aid vary; but, in most cases, the general rules apply.
    1. Remain calm.
    2. Shout “HELP” and send for medical help immediately.
    3. Examine the entire body.
      1. Remove only enough clothing to determine the extent of the injury.
      2. Ensure the person does NOT become chilled.
    4. Be confident and reassuring. It is important to keep the patient as calm as possible.
    5. Keep the victim lying down. The head should be level with body lying down EXCEPT when the victim:
      1. Is or bleeding about the mouth. Turn head to side to prevent aspiration (the inhalation of blood, vomit, or water into the lungs).
      2. Has shortness of breath. Place in sitting or semi-sitting position to make breathing easier.
    6. Prevent further injury by not moving the victim unless necessary.
    7. Keep the victim comfortably warm to alleviate or reduce shock.
    8. Do NOT allow the patient to see the injury. This could increase shock.
    9. Do touch open wounds or burns with fingers or objects, unless necessary; e.g., to stop severe bleeding.
    10. Do NOT give an unconscious person anything by mouth to prevent choking or aspiration.
    11. Use extreme care and caution when treating a suspected fracture.
      1. Before moving the victim broken or suspected broken bones.
      2. Do NOT attempt to set fractures (broken bones); this may cause further injury.
    12. Secure victim onto stretcher and transport the victim with feet down ladders and head first up ladders. The person at the head observes the victim’s breathing. A victim can be transported on any of the following stretchers:
      1. Stokes stretcher -- Used to transfer victims from boats or ships.
      2. Miller board -- Used to vertically move victims.
      3. Spine board used to immobilize victims with possible spinal injury.
      4. Reeves Sleeve -- Used to immobilize and transport victims horizontally or vertically through confined spaces (narrow passageways, escape trunks).
      5. Army litter–-Used for transporting battle casualties.
  6. Procedures for Performing the Three Primary Tasks of First Aid
    1. Three Tasks
      1. Maintain breathing
      2. Stop bleeding
      3. Prevent or Reduce Shock
    2. Maintain Breathing (for choking victim)
      1. Conscious Victim
        1. Symptoms
          1. Person will clutch their throat. This is the universal distress signal.
          2. With total airway obstruction, the victim cannot speak, cough, or breathe.
          3. With partial airway obstruction, the victim might be having difficulties breathing or speaking.
        2. Treatment
          1. Ask the victim, "Are you choking?" and "Are you pregnant?"; then tell them you can help.
          2. Perform Heimlich maneuver, if necessary.
            1. Stand behind the person; place a foot between the victim’s feet for control in case the victim becomes unconscious.
            2. Wrap arms around person's waist.
            3. Make a fist, place thumb side against the abdomen and slightly above the navel.
            4. Grasp the fist with your other hand.
            5. Press fist into the person's abdomen with an inward and upward thrust.
          3. If the victim is pregnant or obese, perform a chest thrust.
            1. Before administering the , check for charms or medallions.
            2. Performed same as abdominal thrust with the following exceptions:
              1. Arms are brought through the person's armpit just under the breast.
              2. Give a quick backwards thrust.
          4. Repeat the Heimlich maneuver or chest thrust until the obstruction is clear.
      2. Unconscious Victim (treatment)
        1. Place victim on their back.
        2. Open victim’s mouth.
        3. Perform finger sweep.
          1. Grasp tongue and lower jaw between your thumb and fingers.
          2. Lift jaw.
          3. To dislodge any foreign object, use a hooking motion with your index finger from the base of the victim's tongue along the inside of the cheek.
        4. Open the victim’s .
          1. Place your hand on victim’s forehead.
          2. Place fingers of your other hand under the bony part of victim’s chin. (Do not put pressure under chin).
          3. Tilt victim’s head and lift jaw (do not close their mouth).
          4. Place your ear over victim’s mouth and nose.
          5. Look at the chest; listen and feel for breathing for 3-5 seconds.
        5. If the victim is not breathing, say, “Not breathing” so someone will get help, then give breaths.
          1. Pinch nose.
          2. Open your mouth.
          3. Take a deep breath.
          4. Make an airtight seal around the victim’s mouth.
          5. Give 2 full breaths, each lasting 1-1½ seconds. (Pause between each breath.)
        6. If unsuccessful, perform thrusts.
          1. Straddle victim’s thighs.
          2. Place heel of one hand slightly above the navel and below tip of breastbone.
          3. Place the other hand directly on top (interlock fingers) and push into person’s abdomen with quick upward thrusts 6 to 10 times.
          4. Continue sequence until person breathes, you are exhausted, another trained individual relieves you, or medical assistance arrives.
      3. Self-help -- If you are alone and the victim of an airway obstruction, perform one of the following steps:
        1. Using your own fist, perform the abdominal thrust maneuver.
        2. Use the back of a chair (with a rounded edge) or similar structure to exert abdominal pressure.
    3. Stop Bleeding
      1. Definition: Bleeding is any break in the wall of one or more blood vessels. May be internal or external.
      2. Types of Bleeding
        1. Arterial: Bleeding from an artery, Blood is bright in appearance and spurts from the wound.
        2. Venous: Bleeding from the veins. Blood is dark red or maroon in appearance and flows steadily from the wound.
        3. Capillary: Bleeding from capillaries. The blood is bright red/maroon and oozes from the wound.
      3. Methods for Controlling Bleeding
        1. Direct Pressure: Application of pressure directly on the wound.
          1. Place a dressing firmly over the wound.
          2. If bleeding does not stop, secure another dressing over the first.
          3. If possible, use sterile or clean dressings; but if not available, improvise with clean clothing or other materials.
          4. After the dressing has been applied, use the heel of your hand to apply pressure directly on top of the wound.
        2. Elevation: Elevate or raise the injured area above the level of the heart.
          1. Used with pressure.
          2. Use a stable object to maintain elevation.
          3. Do NOT elevate a limb if a fracture is suspected.
        3. Indirect Pressure (Pressure Points): Application of pressure where a main artery lies near the skin surface and over a bone or firm tissue.
          1. Use with direct pressure and elevation to control bleeding without using a tourniquet.
          2. The compress is to compress the artery against the underlying firm surface, thus slowing the flow of the blood to the wound.
          3. Used in conjunction with direct pressure and elevation to control bleeding without having to use a tourniquet.
          4. Pressure points in the arms and the groin are used most often.
        4. : Used ONLY as a last resort for severe, life-threatening bleeding that cannot be controlled by any other method.
          1. A tourniquet is dangerous; its application may cause tissue injury or loss of the injured limb.
          2. Used only in cases of partial or complete severing of arms, legs, or when bleeding is uncontrollable.
    4. Prevent or Reduce Shock
      1. Shock is a condition in which blood circulation is seriously disturbed; results in a lack of oxygen to the body.
      2. All injuries are accompanied by some degree of shock.Symptoms
      3. Symptoms
        1. Eyes: Vacant (no movement), lackluster (dull), dilated (large) pupils.
        2. Breathing: Shallow, irregular, and rapid.
        3. Skin: Pale, cold, and moist.
        4. Pulse: Usually rapid but may be weak or absent.
        5. May complain of nausea, vomiting, headache, dizziness, weakness, and anxiety.
      4. Treatment
        1. Monitor the airway, , and circulation.
        2. Control bleeding as soon as possible.
        3. Keep victim lying down with legs elevated 6 to 12 inches. Do NOT elevate legs if you suspect:
          1. Head or spinal injuries.
          2. An injured hip or leg.
        4. If vomiting, place person on side.
        5. Keep victim warm; maintain warm body temperature.
        6. If the victim has difficulty breathing, help into a sitting or semi-sitting position.
        7. Do NOT give anything by mouth.
        8. Reassure the person.
        9. Seek medical assistance as soon as possible.
  7. Characteristics and Proper Treatment of Skin Wounds
    1. Types
      1. Incision: Edges are smooth with little damage to surrounding skin. Caused by a sharp cutting instrument such as a knife, razor, or broken glass.
      2. Laceration: Skin is rather than cut. Caused by blunt force such as a tire iron or baseball bat.
      3. Puncture: Skin is pierced by a pointed object, such as a bullet, nail, or knife.
      4. Abrasion: Skin is rubbed or scraped off.
      5. Avulsion: Skin is torn loose or left hanging as a flap. Caused by an accident involving machinery such as lawn mowers and power tools.
    2. Treatment -- If the wound is fresh:
      1. Stop the flow of blood.
      2. Treat for shock.
      3. Reduce the risk of infection by using clean or sterile dressings.
  8. Characteristics and Proper Treatment of Injuries to the Bone and Muscles
    1. Fractures
      1. Two Major Types
        1. Open: Bone is protruding through the skin.
        2. Closed: Bone is NOT protruding through the skin.
      2. Symptoms
        1. Pain and tenderness in the area of the injury.
        2. Loss of .
        3. Discoloration and swelling.
        4. Possible deformity.
        5. Protruding bone, if an open fracture.
    2. Strain: Stretched or muscle.
    3. Sprain: Stretched or torn ligaments and/or tendons. (Ligaments and tendons connect bones, muscles, and organs in the human body.)
    4. Dislocation: Bone is forcibly displaced from its joint.
  9. Types and Proper Treatment of Burns and Scalds
    1. Burns and scalds are caused by exposure to intense heat.
      1. Dry heat is a burn.
      2. Moist heat is a scald.
    2. Burns are classified according to their depth as first, second, or third degree.
      1. First-degree: Minor redness, mild pain and swelling, increased warmth.
      2. Second-degree: Skin is red, mottled (different colors), and blistered. Swelling occurs as time passes. The burn is moist due to loss of fluids through the damaged skin. Victim suffers severe pain.
      3. Third-degree: destruction of nerve endings. May appear charred or white. Severe pain at burn edges but not at burn site.
    3. Treatment
      1. Treatment is the same for burns and scalds.
      2. Examine for and relieve any distress. (Always anticipate this when there are burns to the face.)
      3. Remove jewelry from the victim except, when because of the injury, it is not possible.
      4. To relieve pain apply cold compresses to the affected area or submerge in cold water.
      5. To prevent complications, do NOT:
        1. Attempt to remove charred skin or clothing.
        2. Break blisters.
      6. NEVER apply greasy substances or ointments.
      7. Cover burned area with a dressing.
      8. Treat for shock.
      9. If the victim's hands, feet, or legs are affected, elevate the limb higher than the heart.
      10. Transport to the nearest medical facility.
  10. Characteristics and Proper Treatment of Heat Injuries
    1. Heat Cramps
      1. Causes
        1. Working or exercising in a hot environment.
        2. Loss of large amounts of salt due to heavy sweating.
      2. Symptoms
        1. Muscle pain and spasm due to exertion involving abdominal or leg muscles.
        2. Hot, sweaty skin and body temperature.
      3. Treatment
        1. Move person to cool area.
        2. Give victim one-half a glass of cool water every 15 minutes.
        3. Moist towels to the forehead.
        4. Massage cramping muscles.
        5. Transport victim to nearest medical facility as soon as possible.
    2. Heat Exhaustion
      1. Causes
        1. Working or exercising in a hot environment.
        2. The body loses large amounts of salt and fluids.
        3. Heat cramps could lead to heat exhaustion.
      2. Symptoms
        1. Nausea, headache, or dizziness.
        2. Heavy sweating.
        3. Cool, pale, and moist skin.
        4. Normal or below normal body temperature.
        5. Possible cramps or muscle aches due to loss of salt.
        6. Pupils dilated.
      3. Treatment
        1. Move person to cool area.
        2. Loosen .
        3. Apply wet cloths to head, armpits, and ankles.
        4. Do not allow to become chilled.
        5. Transport to the nearest medical facility as soon as possible.
    3. Heat Stroke
      1. Causes
        1. Working or exercising in a hot environment.
        2. A breakdown of the body’s heat regulating (sweating) mechanism.
      2. Symptoms
        1. Body temperature 105 degrees Fahrenheit (41 degrees centigrade) or higher.
        2. Small to no perspiration.
        3. Skin is hot, dry, , and flushed.
        4. Very small pupils (constricted).
        5. Mental confusion and anxiety.
        6. Convulsions and sudden collapse.
      3. Treatment
        1. Life threatening; requires prompt action. The brain, kidney, and liver may be damaged due to the failure of the body's heat regulating mechanism.
        2. Move person to shade or cooler area.
        3. Reduce body temperature by removing as much clothing as possible.
        4. Sponge person with cold wet towels and fan person to promote evaporation. Never apply ice directly to the skin. This may cause frostbite.
        5. If victim can drink, give them one-half a glass of cool water every 15 minutes.
        6. Treat for .
        7. Transport to nearest medical facility as soon as possible.
  11. Characteristics and Proper Treatment of Chill Condition Injuries
    1. Freezing injuries are characterized by the formation of ice crystals in the skin.
    2. Freezing injuries include:
      1. Superficial Frostbite
        1. Characteristics: Freezing of the top layers of skin.
        2. Symptoms
          1. Skin reddening, pain, and uncomfortable coldness.
          2. Skin color changes to or bluish white.
          3. Area becomes numb and feels cold and hard.
          4. Skin and tissue WILL move over bony ridges.
        3. Treatment
          1. Move the victim to a sheltered area.
          2. Rewarm hands and feet by placing them directly against a warm body.
          3. Rewarm the affected areas by warm water immersion as soon as safe and possible.
          4. Frostbitten areas should NEVER be massaged.
      2. Deep Frostbite
        1. Characteristics: Freezing of the deep layers of tissue.
        2. Symptoms: Same as superficial frostbite except the skin will not move over bony ridges.
        3. Treatment
          1. Severe exposure cases may require cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) before the frozen area can be treated.
          2. Do NOT attempt to thaw the skin if there is a possibility that the area will freeze again.
          3. When adequate facilities and personnel are available:
            1. Carry the victim on a stretcher, to a medical facility as soon as possible.
            2. Rapidly rewarm using warm water immersion at temperatures from 100° to Fahrenheit. During rewarming, watch the victim closely for cardio respiratory failure.
            3. After the area has been thawed, keep it dry and warm.
            4. Protect areas between the fingers and toes from contact pressure with cotton.
  12. Definition of Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) Training and the Importance of Receiving Training
    1. CPR is a first aid procedure that consists of external heart compression and artificial ventilation for a victim that is in cardiac and respiratory arrest; i.e., the victim’s heart and breathing have stopped.
    2. The external compression of the heart provides blood circulation to the organs and tissues of the body.
    3. Victims having life-threatening accidents depend on others to save their lives. The following conditions may cause respiratory and cardiac arrest:
      1. Heart attack
      2. Electrical shock
      3. Drowning and suffocation
      4. Gas .
      5. Overdose of drugs and/or alcohol
    4. This lesson will NOT qualify you to perform CPR. NEVER attempt CPR procedures unless you have received proper training.
    5. If you are NOT CPR qualified, the victim could:
      1. Die due to the lack of qualified medical assistance.
      2. Suffer more injury due to incorrect procedures.
    6. To learn CPR, take an approved course from a qualified instructor.
  13. Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Performing First Aid
    1. Honor: Display a code of ethics, integrity and concern for those injured.
    2. Courage: Have the courage to assist and administer aid, regardless of the severity of the injury.
    3. Commitment: Be committed to performing first aid to the best of your ability.
