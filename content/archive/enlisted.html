A. Introduction

U.S. Navy uniforms are outward symbols of naval organization and of military rank or rate. Differentiating between officer and enlisted personnel is an important element in the moral, pride, discipline, and effectiveness of the Navy.

B. Enabling Objective(s)

C. Topic Outline

  1. Introduction
  2. Terms Related to Pay Grade and Job Specialty
    1. Rating -- An enlisted person’s occupation or job specialty within the Navy. Example: Operations Specialist, Gunner’s Mate.
    2. Pay Grade -- Used by all services to indicate the level of pay.  Applies to E-1 through E-9 and O-1 through O-10.
    3. Rate -- Identifies personnel by rating (occupation) and pay grade. Example: Boatswain’s Mate Third Class (BM3).
    4. Nonrated person -- A member that is E-1, E-2, E-3.
      1. Apprentice -- Nonrated personnel belong to a general occupational field. Example: airman, constructionman, fireman, hospitalman or seaman.
      2. Striker -- A nonrated person that is working for promotion towards a specific rating. Example: ETSN, MMFA.
    5. Rated Person
      1. A member that is from E-4 through E-9.
      2. Has a specific occupation (rating).
    6. Rank -- Relates to Officer pay grade. Example: ensign, captain.
  3. Navy Enlisted Rate, Insignia, and Headgear
    1. Personnel E-1 through E-3
      1. Wear diagonal stripes to indicate their rate.
      2. The number of stripes depends on pay grade.
        1. E-1: no stripes.
        2. E-2: two stripes.
        3. E-3: three stripes.
      3. The apprenticeship field determines the color of stripes worn on dress uniforms.
        1. Airmen wear green stripes on all uniforms.
        2. Constructionmen wear light blue stripes on all uniforms.
        3. Firemen wear red stripes on all uniforms.
        4. Hospitalmen and Seamen wear white stripes on blue uniforms and navy blue stripes on white uniforms.
      4. Above the diagonal stripes:
        1. Graduates of seaman, fireman, and airman apprentice schools wear apprentice symbols.
        2. Strikers wear marks identifying their prospective rating.
      5. Rate marks are centered between the elbow and shoulder on the left sleeve of the following uniforms:
        1. Service dress blue and service dress white jumpers
        2. Female service dress blue coat.
    2. Personnel E-4 through E-9 (petty officers)
      1. Wear rating badges, which consist of three parts:
        1. Perched eagle with wings extended.
        2. Specialty mark indicating the wearer’s rating.
        3. V-shaped chevron(s), rocker, and star(s) to indicate the wearer’s pay grade.
      2. The color of the background of the rating badge matches the color of the uniform worn.
        1. On navy blue uniforms, personnel with less than 12 years of service wear red chevrons with a white eagle and specialty mark. Personnel who have 12 consecutive years of good conduct wear gold chevrons with a silver eagle and specialty mark.
      3. On white uniforms, the eagle, specialty mark, and chevrons are dark blue rating badges are positioned the same way as group rate marks and are worn on the following uniforms:
        1. Service dress white and service dress blue jumpers (PO3 through PO1)
        2. Service dress blue coat (females PO3 and above and males CPO and above)
        3. Peacoat (PO3 through PO1)
      4. Chevron(s), rocker, and star(s) indicate the wearer’s rate.
        1. Petty Officer Third Class (PO3): One chevron.
        2. Petty Officer Second Class (PO2): Two chevrons.
        3. Petty Officer First Class (PO1): Three chevrons.
        4. Chief Petty Officer (CPO): Three chevrons and one rocker.
        5. Senior Chief Petty Officer (SCPO): Three chevrons and one rocker with a silver star above the eagle.
        6. Master Chief Petty Officer (MCPO): Three chevrons and one rocker with two silver stars above the eagle.
          1. Command Master Chief (CMDCM): Wears three chevrons and on rocker with two silver stars above the eagle and a single silver star in place of the specialty mark.
          2. Fleet/Force Master Chief (FLTCM/FORCM) Wears three chevrons and one rocker with two gold stars above the eagle and a single gold star in place of the specialty mark.
          3. Master Chief Petty Officer of the Navy (MCPON) wears three chevrons and one rocker with three gold stars above the eagle and a single gold star in place of the specialty mark.
      5. Collar insignia indicate the wearer’s paygrade.
        1. Petty Officers Third Class through First Class have two different types of collar devices.
          1. Anodized (highly polished miniature collar insignia on:
            1. The black relaxed fit jacket
            2. The blue all-weather coat (raincoat)
          2. Sewn-on embroidered miniature sized device tapes on:
            1. Coveralls
            2. Navy Working Uniform shirt
        2. E-2 through E-6 personnel wearing the Service Uniform Khaki Shirt/Overblouse will wear anodized (high polished) miniature collar devices on both shirt collar points.
        3. Chief, Senior Chief, and Master Chief Petty Officers wear:
          1. Gold fouled (entangled) anchor with silver block letters “USN” superimposed on the shank of the anchor.
          2. One, two, or three silver stars are attached above the anchor stock to indicate:
            1. Senior Chief
            2. Master Chief
            3. Master Chief Petty Officer of the Navy (MCPON)
          3. Insignia on both collar points except for the shirts worn under the dress blue coat.
          4. Black on dark green uniforms (aviators).
      6. Chief petty officers wear soft black shoulder boards with anchor insignia.
    3. Headgear -- Varies with a person’s rate and gender.
      1. Seaman Recruit through Petty Officer First Class.
        1. Male
          1. Navy/Command Ball Cap
          2. White Hat
          3. Garrison Cap
          4. Eight-Point Cover
        2. Female
          1. Navy/Command Ball Cap
          2. Combination Cap
          3. Garrison Cap
          4. Eight-Point Cover
          5. Beret
      2. Chief Petty Officer through Master Chief Petty Officer
        1. On all covers, Chief Petty Officers and above wear a gold fouled (entangled) anchor with silver block letters “USN” superimposed on the shank of the anchor. One, two, or three silver stars are attached above the anchor stock to indicate:
          1. Senior Chief
          2. Master Chief
          3. Master Chief Petty Officer of the Navy (MCPON)
        2. Male
          1. Combination Cap -- Comes with khaki, white and green covers.
          2. Garrison Cap -- Comes in khaki, black or green.
          3. Eight-Point Cover.
        3. Female
          1. Combination Cap -- Comes with khaki and white covers.
          2. Garrison Cap -- Comes in khaki, black or green.
          3. Eight-Point Cover.
          4. Beret -- Black semi-rigid feminine style cover.
  4. Service Stripes
    1. Service stripes are diagonal stripes worn on the lower left sleeve of the service dress:
      1. Service Dress Blue Coat
      2. Aviation Working Green Coat
      3. Service Dress Blue Jumper
      4. Service Dress White Jumper
    2. One service stripe is earned for every four years of service.
    3. The color of service stripe must be the same color as the chevron(s) on the rating badge.
    4. Gold rating badges and service stripes are worn on the service dress blue coat and service dress blue jumper when good conduct in active naval service totals twelve consecutive years.
  5. Summary and Review (Enlisted)
  6. Navy Officer Rank, Insignia, and Headgear
    1. Insignia characteristics common to all ranks
      1. Sleeve Stripes: Gold or black stripes worn on the sleeve of certain uniforms. Can be ¼, ½, or 2 inches in width.
        1. Worn near the bottom of both sleeves.
        2. Gold on service dress blue and female service dress white uniforms.
        3. Black on dark green uniforms (aviators).
      2. Shoulder Boards: Gold stripes are worn on the shoulders of the following uniforms:
        1. White shirt of service dress blue uniform
        2. Bridge coat
        3. Reefer (Peacoat)
        4. Sweaters
      3. Collar Devices: Metal devices worn on collar points.
        1. The color and shape of the device depends on the officer’s rank and specialty.
        2. Collar devices are worn on the following uniforms:
          1. Service
          2. Summer khaki and working khaki
      4. Oversized Devices: Metal devices worn on the epaulets of the following uniforms:
        1. Jackets (khaki and black)
        2. All-weather coats
    2. Chief Warrant Officers
      1. Chief Warrant Officer (CWO2)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ½’ gold stripe with three blue breaks.
        2. Collar Device:  Gold bar with three blue breaks.
      2. Chief Warrant Officer (CWO3)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ½’ gold stripe with two blue breaks.
        2. Collar Device -- Silver bar with two blue breaks.
      3. Chief Warrant Officer (CWO4)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ½” gold stripe with one blue break.
        2. Collar Device -- Silver bar with three blue breaks.
      4. Chief Warrant Officer (CWO5)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ½’ gold stripe with 1/8” blue stripe and one blue break
        2. Collar Device -- Silver bar with ¼” blue stripe.
    3. Commissioned Officers
      1. Ensign (ESN)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ½” gold stripe.
        2. Collar Device -- One gold bar.
      2. Lieutenant Junior Grade (LTJG)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ½” gold stripe and one ¼” gold stripe.
        2. Collar Device -- One silver bar.
      3. Lieutenant (LT)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- Two ½” gold stripes.
        2. Collar Device -- Two silver bars.
      4. Lieutenant Commander (LCDR)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- One ¼” gold stripe between two ½” gold stripes.
        2. Collar Device -- Gold oak leaf.
      5. Commander (CDR)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- Three ½” gold stripes.
        2. Collar Device -- Silver oak leaf.
      6. Captain (CAPT.)
        1. Sleeve/Shoulder Board -- Four ½” gold stripes.
        2. Collar Device -- Silver spread eagle.
      7. Rear Admiral (Lower Half) (RDML)
        1. Sleeve -- One 2” gold stripe.
        2. Shoulder Board/Collar Device -- One silver star.
      8. Rear Admiral (Upper Half) (RADM)
        1. Sleeve -- One 2” gold stripe and one ½” gold stripe.
        2. Shoulder Board/Collar Device -- Two silver stars.
      9. Vice Admiral (VADM)
        1. Sleeve -- One 2” gold stripe and two ½” gold stripes.
        2. Shoulder Board/Collar Device -- Four silver stars.
      10. Admiral (ADM)
        1. Sleeve -- One 2” gold stripe and three ½” gold stripes.
        2. Shoulder Board/Collar Device -- Four silver stars.
    4. Headgear Insignia
      1. Officer Crest
        1. A silver federal shield over two crossed, gold, fouled anchors surmounted by a silver eagle.
        2. Worn by commissioned officers and warrant officers.
      2. Fretting -- Gold embroidering on the cap visor or headband.  Commonly referred to as “Scrambled Eggs”.
        1. Ensign through Lieutenant Commander headgear has no fretting.
        2. Commander and Captain wear partial fretting (one row of gold oak leaves and acorns).
        3. Flag Officers, Rear Admiral (Lower Half) through Admiral, wear full fretting (two rows of gold oak leaves and acorns).
    5. Headgear Types
      1. Male
        1. Combination Cap
          1. Gold chinstrap
          2. Comes with detachable covers in white, khaki, and green.
        2. Garrison Cap
          1. Comes in black, khaki, and green.
          2. Miniature officer’s crest worn on the left side.
          3. Rank insignia worn on the right side. (Only officers wear insignia on the right side of the garrison cap.)
        3. Eight-Point Cover
          1. Comes in digital camouflage.
          2. Embroidered rank insignia tape worn on the front center flat.
      2. Female
        1. Combination Cap
          1. Gold chinstrap.
          2. Comes with detachable covers in white, and khaki.
        2. Garrison Cap
          1. Comes in khaki, black, and green.
          2. Miniature officer’s crest worn on the left side.
          3. Rank insignia worn on the right side.
        3. Eight-Point Cover
          1. Comes in digital camouflage.
          2. Embroidered rank insignia tape worn on the front center flat.
        4. Beret -- Black semi-rigid cover.
  7. Line and Staff Officer Insignia
    1. Line officers, who are eligible to command a ship or station, wear a five-point star above sleeve stripes and on shoulder boards.
    2. Staff corps officers, who command only in their designated field, wear devices to show their specialty.
      1. Medical Corps -- Gold spread oak leaf, silver acorn.
      2. Medical Service Corps -- Gold spread oak leaf attached to a slanted twig.
      3. Nurse Corps -- gold spread oak leaf.
      4. Dental Corps -- Gold spread oak leaf with silver acorn on each side of stem.
      5. Chaplain Corps
        1. Jewish -- Star of David attached to the Tablets of the Law.
        2. Christian -- Latin Cross
        3. Buddhist -- Prayer Wheel
        4. Muslim -- Crescent Moon
      6. Supply Corps -- Sprig of three gold oak leaves and three gold acorns.
      7. Judge Advocate General Corps -- Two gold oak leaves, silver mill rind in center.
      8. Law Community -- Two gold oak leaves with silver mill rind in center.
      9. Civil Engineer Corps -- Two gold sprigs of two oak leaves, with a silver acorn on each sprig.
    3. Admirals are referred to as Flag Officers.
      1. They have the privilege of flying their personal flag over the ship or station they command.
      2. A line admiral’s personal flag is blue with white stars.
      3. A flag officer not eligible for command at sea uses a white flag with blue stars.

  9. Officer Pay Grade and Insignia of Other U.S. Armed Services. (Refer to Information Sheet 1-07-2)

  10. Relationship between the Navy Core Values, and Enlisted Rate and Officer Rank Recognition.
    1. Honor - Accountable for personal and professional behavior.
      1. Always wear your rate insignia with pride.
      2. Only wear ribbons that you earned.
    2. Commitment - Always strive to recognize the rate and rank of personnel and give then the respect that they deserve.
