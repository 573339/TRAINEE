C. Topic Outline:

  1. Introduction
  2. Equal Opportunity Terms
    1. Equal Opportunity (EO) -- The right of all persons to participate in and benefit from programs and activities for which they are qualified.
    2. -- An attitude, usually negative, about a group of people that is not based on fact.
    3. Discrimination -- Action based on one’s prejudice; i.e., behavior that denies a person equal opportunity.
    4. Sexual Harassment: UNWELCOMED sexual behavior directed toward someone.
      1. A form of discrimination.
      2. The behavior involves UNWELCOMED:
        1. Sexual advances.
        2. Requests for sexual favors.
        3. Other verbal or physical sexual behavior.
      3. To be sexual harassment, the behavior must occur in the work environment.
    5. Work environment -- The workplace or a work-connected place. Examples include:
      1. Offices
      2. Buildings
      3. Department of Defense installations
      4. Department of Defense ships, aircraft, or vehicles
      5. Anywhere -- When engaged I official business
      6. Command sponsored social, recreational, or sporting events
    6. Complainant -- Anyone who submits allegations of discrimination or sexual harassment.
    7. Formal complaint -- allegation of assault or sexual harassment that is submitted in writing through the chain of command.
    8. Informal complaint -- Allegation of discrimination or sexual harassment that is made either in writing to the offending party of verbally, and is not submitted as a formal complaint.
    9. Informal Resolution System (IRS) -- A system for resolving equal opportunity complaints at the lowest possible level in the chain of command.
    10. Stereotype -- an exaggerated belief that goes beyond existing evidence; e.g., believing that all politicians are crooks.
    11. Reasonable person standard -- A test used to determine if behavior is discrimination or sexual harassment.
      1. The test requires that a person take a reasonable point of view in the same circumstances as the complainant.
      2. When using this standard, if the behavior is offensive, it is considered discrimination or sexual harassment.
    12. Reprisal -- Taking or threatening unfavorable actions, or withholding or threatening to withhold favorable actions, in response to a complaint of sexual harassment or discrimination, e.g., unjustified:
      1. Reduction in evaluation marks.
      2. Increased duty assignments.
    13. Ethnic group -- A group socially set apart by others because of cultural or nationality characteristics; e.g., American, Oriental, Polish.
    14. Race -- A division of human beings identified by the possession of traits that are inherited from one’s ancestors; e.g., black, Hispanic, White.
  3. Effects of Social Background on Prejudice and Discrimination
    1. From birth, we gather information about our world to form our beliefs and attitudes. We learn from many sources:
      1. School
      2. Family
      3. Neighbors/friends
      4. Community
      5. Media (for example, books, movies, and TV)
    2. Anytime you experience favorable or unfavorable feelings about a person not based on actual experience, you are guilty of prejudice. When you act upon these feelings, you are guilty of discrimination.
    3. Prejudices and discrimination cause individuals to exclude members of another race, color, religion, gender, age, national origin, ethnic background, or sexual orientation from:
      1. Employment
      2. Housing
      3. Political rights
      4. Educational opportunities
      5. Social interaction
    4. Prejudicial views can manifest into violent actions.
      1. For instance, a perception of homosexuality does not give an individual the right to act in a harassing or violent manner. Awareness of how prejudice can lead to harmful behavior helps us to prevent its occurrence.
      2. In October 1992, a Navy airman was beaten to death by two of his shipmates in a public restroom in Sasebo, Japan, due to rumors that he was a homosexual.
      3. Awareness of how prejudice can lead to harmful behavior helps prevent its occurrence.
    5. Responses to discrimination and violence may cause a “chip on the shoulder” attitude. Victims of discrimination can respond in one of two ways:
      1. Positively, for example:
        1. Participate in peaceful demonstrations.
        2. Study civil rights issues.
      2. Negatively, for example:
        1. Acting against one’s own group.
        2. Withdrawing from family and friends.
        3. Joining extremist behavior groups.
  4. Extremist Behavior Groups
    1. Gangs -- A group of persons organized for a common purpose, which often involves violation of the law.
    2. Supremacist groups -- Organizations whose members believe they are superior to others by virtue of their race, ethnicity, religion, gender, or sexual orientation. A few of the most established groups include:
      1. Ku Klux Klan (KKK)
        1. White supremacist group
        2. Anti-black and Semitic
      2. Neo-Nazis
        1. White supremacist group
        2. Anti-black and Semitic
      3. Skin Heads
        1. American Neo-Nazi group
        2. White supremacist group
        3. Anti-foreigners, Jews, and racial minorities
      4. Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS)
        1. Muslim supremacist group
        2. The aim of ISIS is to create an Islamic state across the Sunni areas of Iraq and Syria.
        3. Known for carrying out public executions to further their cause.
        4. Declared they will destroy westerners.
  5. Navy Policy on Participating in Extremist and Discriminatory Activities
    1. The Department of Defense is intolerant of organizations or activities that are discriminatory.
    2. Navy personnel are prohibited from participating in organizations that:
      1. Support supremacist causes.
      2. Practice discrimination.
      3. Encourage the use of force or violence.
      4. Deprive others of their civil rights.
    3. The term “participate” includes:
      1. Acts or conduct such as:
        1. Demonstrating
        2. Attending a rally
        3. Fundraising
        4. Recruiting
        5. Training
        6. Organizing
        7. Leading
      2. Engaging in activities that are detrimental to:
        1. Good order and discipline
        2. Mission accomplishment
    4. There will always be individuals with conflicting outside interests who would rather act secretly than conform to the Service’s expectations. Their activities can have a devastating effect on the good order and conduct essential to the military. Such behaviors can result in disciplinary action or even discharge.
    5. Commanders have the authority to take disciplinary action, such as recommending separation from the Navy, against personnel who discriminate based on racism, sexual preference, etc.
    6. Strict military standards are necessary to ensure a command climate that fosters respect for all individuals.
  6. Types of Behavior that are Considered Sexual Harassment
    1. Verbal sexual harassment
      1. Telling sexually explicit jokes, epithets (abusive words or phrases), and making derogatory comments.
      2. Talking about sex, or sexual orientation; using sexually explicit or offensive language.
      3. Offering work-related benefits in return for submitting to sexual advances.
    2. Physical sexual harassment
      1. Touching in a suggestive manner.
      2. Groping.
    3. Visual sexual harassment
      1. Giving a person unwelcome letters, cards, or gifts or a personal nature, particularly when these items have sexual overtones.
      2. Displaying sexually oriented posters, calendars, cartoons, or phrases.
      3. Displaying photos of nude people.
  7. Types of Behavior that are Considered Sexual Harassment, Zones of Behavior as Related to Sexual Harassment, and Responsibilities of Individuals who are sexually Harassed
  8. Navy’s Policy on Sexual Harassment -- Sexual harassment in the Department of the Navy, of both military and civilian personnel, is prohibited because of it:
    1. Causes hard feelings between co-workers.
    2. Harms morale.
    3. Interferes with productivity.
  9. Navy’s Equal Opportunity Policy
    1. Provide equitable treatment and equal opportunity for all personnel.
    2. It is the responsibility of all Service members to promote an environment free from personal, social, or institutional barriers that prevent fellow Service members from rising to the highest level of responsibility possible.
    3. Service members are evaluated only on individual merit, fitness, and capability.
    4. Equal opportunity shall apply without regard to:
      1. Race
      2. Ethnicity
      3. Religion
      4. Gender
  10. Command Managed Equal Opportunity (CMEO) Program
    1. The command Managed Equal Opportunity (CMEO) Program is a tool for commanding officers to:
      1. Communicate the Navy’s Equal Opportunity and sexual harassment policies to their personnel
      2. Assess the level of equal opportunity within their command.
      3. Manage equal opportunity-related issues.
    2. The CMEO manager assists the commanding officer by:
      1. Overseeing the resolution of equal opportunity/sexual harassment problems
      2. Assesses the command’s equal opportunity climate by examining factors such as:
        1. Morale
        2. Teamwork
        3. Communication
    3. Command EO assessments may be accomplished by:
      1. Group and/or individual interviews
      2. Observations
      3. Surveys -- Can be administered by a Command Assessment Team (CAT).
      4. Reviews of records and reports.
  11. Personal Responsibilities in Dealing with Equal Opportunity
    1. You can promote equal opportunity by:
      1. Treating others with dignity and respect.
      2. Setting a positive example.
      3. Reporting only legitimate grievances to the chain of command.
    2. Avoid public demonstrations that are against military rules and regulations, such as Ku Klux Klan rallies and parades.
  12. Procedures for Reporting Sexual Harassment/Equal Opportunity Complaints
    1. Personnel who believe they are victims of sexual harassment or Equal Opportunity violations can report legitimate complaints, without fear of reprisal, by either a/an:
      1. Informal complaint
        1. Individuals should first attempt to resolve the complaint with the person(s) involved.
        2. Resolution of the conflict at the lowest level is strongly encouraged using the Informal Resolution System (IRS).
        3. There are three options in the IRS to reach a satisfactory resolution.
          1. Direct -- Approach the person involved directly.
          2. Informal third party
            1. Request assistance from another person.
            2. Request to speak with the Command Managed Equal Opportunity (CMEO) Officer, Command Master Chief, or other personnel in the chain of command.
            3. Request for Commanding Officer’s Mast -- If the complaint cannot be resolved with the other methods, submit a request in writing to speak to the CO.
          3. Request training -- Request presentation of training materials to communicate inappropriate behavior.
        4. If resolution of the informal complaint is still considered unsatisfactory, use the formal grievance procedures.
  13. Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Equal Opportunity
    1. Honor -- All persons must be accountable for their behavior by treating others with respect and dignity. Everyone must be given the same chance to succeed.
    2. Courage -- Everyone must have the courage to report equal opportunity violations, even when faced with opposition from others.
    3. Commitement -- All persons must be committed to uphold Navy equal opportunity policies.
    4. Formal complaint
      1. The Navy Equal Opportunity (EO) / Sexual Harassment (SH) Formal Complaint Form NAVPERS 5354/2 is the primary method to report an EO (discrimination or sexual harassment) formal complaint.
      2. If the grievance is against a superior other than the Commanding Officer, submit the formal complaint to the Commanding Officer. If the complainant considers the resolution unjust, a complaint can be submitted against the commanding officer.
      3. A complaint against the Commanding Officer should be:
        1. Prepared with legal assistance.
        2. Submitted via the chain of command in accordance with UCMJ Article 138, “Complaints of Wrongs”.
      4. While at Recruit Training Command, you will use the Grievance/Complaint Report, (RTC-GL 5350/4) Rev. 6/02.
        1. Each ship has a supply of complaint forms and pre-addressed envelopes on the quarterdeck.
        2. Most buildings at RTC have complaint forms and envelopes near their entrances.
        3. When completing this form:
          1. Be concise and accurate.
          2. Do not use profanity or slang.
        4. Seal the completed form in the pre-addressed envelope and place it in the grievance box located on the Quarterdeck.
        5. Reports are picked up daily by the Ship’s Officer and delivered to the Fleet Commander.
        6. The Fleet Commander will attempt to resolve the problem at their level.
    1. Navy personnel will NOT knowingly make false accusations of sexual harassment. Persons reporting unfounded incidents will be punished.
    2. Existing mechanisms such as the chain of command or the Inspector General should be used to file complaints that do not fall under Sexual Harassment or Equal Opportunity such as those pertaining to:
      1. Marital Status
      2. Sexual Orientation
      3. Health Status
  14. Procedures for Resolving Sexual Harassment/Equal Opportunity Complaints
    1. All reported incidents of sexual harassment will be investigated and resolved at the lowest appropriate level.
    2. All incidents, whether formal or informal, will be investigated within three days of initial notification. Confidentiality will be maintained to the maximum extent possible.
    3. The complainant should be notified that the:
      1. Investigation has begun.
      2. Complaint is resolved.
    4. Counseling support and social services are made available for the personnel involved in incidents of sexual harassment.
    5. Personnel found guilty of sexual harassment may receive one or more of the following:
      1. .
      2. Negative entries into evaluations.
      3. Punishment under the UCMJ, Article 92, “Failure to obey order or regulation”

        4. For example, as a result of a Captains Mast for using racially explicit language, an airman recruit:

        1. Was required to forfeit ½ month’s pay ($596.00).
        2. Lost their nuclear clearance eligibility and was reclassified.
        3. Received an entry in their service record to document the misconduct.
      4. Separation from the Navy.
    6. Military personnel shall be processed for administrative separation on the first substantiated incident of sexual harassment involving:
      1. Actions, threats, or attempts to influence another’s career in exchange for sexual favors.
      2. Physical contact of a sexual nature, which is a violation of the UCMJ.
  15. Fraternization in the Navy
    1. Defined as personal relationships between seniors and subordinates that:
      1. Are unduly familiar.
      2. Do not respect the differenced in grade or rank.
    2. Fraternization could be between:
      1. Officer - Enlisted
      2. Senior Officer - Junior Officerli>
      3. Senior enlisted - junior enlisted; e.g. Chief petty officer - E-6 and below.
      4. Instructor - student
    3. Fraternization is gender-neutral. The key element is inappropriate relationships between seniors and subordinates.
    4. Fraternization is:
      1. Detrimental to good order and discipline.
      2. In violation of long-standing orders.
      3. Prohibited.
    5. Fraternization could result in:
      1. The questioning of a senior’s objectivity.
      2. Actual or perceived special treatment.
      3. Undermining the authority of a senior.
      4. Compromising the chain of commands.
    6. Fraternization could involve:
      1. Dating
      2. Living together
      3. Sexual relations
      4. Business partnerships
    7. Fraternization may result in administrative or punitive action.
    8. Fraternization is NOT excused by a subsequent marriage of the offending parties.

Report of Grievence / Alleged Incident of Discrimination

Recruit Bill of Rights

The policy of the Navy and this command is to conduct training free from discrimination, and to ensure equal opportunity and fair treatment for all persons without regard to race, color, religion, gender, age, or national origin. This policy is carried out per the laws and regulations governing the Department of the Navy.

Procedures for Reporting Your Complaint/Grievance:

  1. Individuals who experience sexual harassment should make it clear that such behavior is offensive and report sexual harassment to the appropriate supervisor within the chain of command.
  2. If the above is unsuccessful, you may use the Navy Grievance Procedures, the Uniform Code of Military Justice or Request Mast.
  3. If you are still unable to resolve the complaint or grievance, you can lodge a complaint via one or more of the following hotlines:
    1. RTC Command Master Chief –(847) 688-3493
    2. RTC Legal Office- (847) 688-4791/4792
  4. Department of the Navy Equal Opportunity/Sexual Harassment Advice Line: 1-800-253-0931.

SEXUAL HARASSMENT

Sexual harassment is a form of sexual discrimination that involves unwelcome behavior directed toward someone when:

  1. Submission or rejection of such conduct is an expression or condition of a person’s job, pay, or career, or
  2. Submission to or rejection of such conduct is made by a person and used as basis for career or employment decisions or
  3. Such conduct interferes with an individual’s work performance or creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive working environment.
