  • C.Topic Outline
    • 1.Purpose and Responsibilities of Watchstanding
      • a.Navy personnel are assigned duty as watchstanders to perform many jobs in port and at sea.
      • b.Most watch assignments last four hours.
      • c.Watchstanders serve many vital functions:
        • (1)Provide security to prevent:
          • (a)Sabotage
          • (b)Damage to property
          • (c)Theft
          • (d)Unauthorized personnel entering a restricted area
        • (2)Ensure the safety of personnel and equipment; e.g., check for fire, flooding, and machinery problems.
        • (3)Provide an important role in fulfilling the Navy's mission.
      • d.A watchstander has many responsibilities.
        • (1)For new watchstanders, apply yourself to training on the requirements of the watch using the Personnel Qualification Standards (PQS) program.
        • (2)Report to your watchstation 30 minutes before assigned watch time.
        • (3)Use the first 15 minutes to perform a turnover before assuming the watch.
        • (4)Use customary Navy terminology when writing reports and talking to others.
        • (5)Answer the telephone using proper procedures and DO NOT discuss classified information unless on a secure line.
        • (6)Take appropriate action for any emergency; e.g., fire, flooding, or bomb threat.
        • (7)Remain at your watch station until properly relieved.
        • (8)Follow all legal orders.
    • 2.Types of Orders
      • a.General orders
        • (1)The purpose is to cover routine and foreseeable situations.
        • (2)Same for all watchstanders and they never change.
      • b.Special orders
        • (1)Cover a certain time or situation.
        • (2)Cover specifics of a particular post.
        • (3)May be written or verbal.
          • (a)Written orders
            • 1)Remain in effect until canceled or changed.
            • 2)Are called "Standing Orders."
            • 3)Are normally kept in a pass-down log for each watch station. Read the log before assuming the watch.
          • (b)Verbal orders
            • 1)Come from a higher authority; e.g., CDO, OOD, etc.
            • 2)Usually cover a situation of very short duration.
    • 3.Consequences of Improper Watchstanding
      • a.Improper watchstanding for any reason is inexcusable.
      • b.You will be held accountable for violations of any rule or regulation that governs your watch.
      • c.Improper watchstanding could result in:
        • (1)Non-judicial punishment under one of the following UCMJ Articles (or others):
          • (a)Art. 78 -- Accessory After the Fact.
          • (b)Art. 92 -- Failure to Obey Order or Regulation.
          • (c)Art. 107 -- False Official Statement.
          • (d)Art. 113 -- Misbehavior of a Sentinel or Lookout.
        • (2)Reduced chance of advancement due to lower performance marks on your periodic evaluation.
        • (3)Disqualification from the watch, requiring more intense training and re-qualification.
        • (4)Counseling (formal or informal).
    • 4.Purpose of the Official Watch Log
      • a.The Official Watch Log is a complete chronological log maintained to ensure an accurate permanent record of the command's activities.
      • b.Used to record every circumstance of importance or interest which concerns the crew and the operation and the safety of the ship or station.
      • c.Can be used as evidence before courts and other legal bodies.
    • 5.Procedures For Making Log Entries
      • a.When making log entries, use a ballpoint pen containing black ink.
      • b.Make entries in capital block letters.
      • c.All entries must be complete and accurate, and written in standard Navy language.
        • (1)Procedures to correct errors.
        • (2)Erasures are prohibited.
        • (3)Draw a single horizontal line through the mistake, so that it remains legible.
        • (4)Initial the mistake in the left hand margin of the page.
        • (5)Continue the entry correctly
        • (6)The watchstander who signs the log is the only person authorized to make corrections, additions, or changes to the log.
      • d.Making a log entry for assuming/relieving the watch.
        • (1)The first line after the heading should relate to assuming the watch.
        • (2)Upon completion of the watch, write on the last line "Properly relieved by (oncoming watches name)”.
          • (a)Sign your name on the next line.
          • (b)Print your name under your signature.
      • e.Making late entries.
        • (1)There are times when events occur too quickly for the watchstander to make entries in the log.
        • (2)To insert the entry when there is time would cause the log to lose its chronological sequence.
        • (3)Steps for recording late entries:
          • (a)In the left margin corresponding to where the entry should have been, place an asterisk (*).
          • (b)Enter the "late entry" on the next available line and place another asterisk (*) in the left margin.
          • (c)Put the original time the event took place followed by the information.
      • f.Ending a page
        • (1)Frequently, the end of a 24-hour day will not be at the bottom of a log page.
        • (2)From the bottom right of the last line of information, draw a diagonal line to the lower left corner of the page.
        • (3)Print on the diagonal line "NO FURTHER ENTRIES THIS PAGE" and initial the entry.
    • 6.Duties and Responsibilities of Shipboard Watches
      • a.Fire watch
        • (1)A fire watch is stationed near workers who are welding or cutting through metal with a torch, to extinguish any fire that might ignite.
      • b.Engineering watches
        • (1)Engineering watchstanders operate and monitor readings on engineering machinery.
      • c.Bridge watch team
        • (1)A group of watchstanders that ensure underway safety, navigation, and communications, for example:
          • (a)Helmsman -- Steers the ship.
          • (b)Lee Helmsman -- Controls the speed of the ship; i.e., the main propulsion engines
          • (c)Lookouts
            • 1)Scan the sea and sky and report significant objects and events as a supplement to radar. Radar cannot detect things such as smoke, flares, or people in the water.
            • 2)Verify the validity of radar contacts. Radar sometimes gives false readings.
            • 3)The number of lookouts posted will vary with the size and type of ship. There could be lookouts:
              • a)Port and starboard on the bridge wings.
              • b)Aft located as close to the ship’s waterline as possible.
            • 4)Lookouts scan their assigned sectors of the horizon.
            • 5)A lookout watchstander should immediately report everything seen and heard to the Officer of the Deck (OOD). Do NOT delay to get a better look!
              • a)Trash in the water could indicate a ship has passed.
              • b)Discolored water may indicate the ship is entering a shoal (shallow water).
          • (d)Sound-Powered Telephone Talker -- Maintains communication with other watchstanders, such as lookouts and engineering personnel.
          • (e)Messenger -- Delivers messages, answers telephones, and carries out other duties assigned by the Officer of the Deck.
        • (2)The bridge watch team is responsible, via the OOD, to the Commanding Officer.
      • d.Quarterdeck Watch Team
        • (1)A group of watchstanders that stand by to coordinate the ship's operation in port, for example:
          • (a)Officer of the Deck (OOD) -- Responsible for the safety, security, and proper organization of the unit.
          • (b)Petty Officer of the Watch (POOW)
            • 1)Principle assistant to the OOD.
          • (c)Messenger
        • (2)The quarterdeck watch team ensures the safety and security of the ship.
      • e.Sounding and Security Watches and Patrols
        • (1)Assigned to increase the physical security of the ship or station.
        • (2)Conducts inspections throughout the ship to check for security of all spaces and classified materials.
        • (3)Report to the OOD and enter in the ship’s deck log the results of soundings and inspections.
        • (4)Immediately report unusual conditions to the OOD.
      • f.Barracks Security Watches and Patrols
        • (1)This watch provides for:
          • (a)Protection against fire.
          • (b)The safety of personnel and material.
        • (2)The watch is responsible for:
          • (a)Knowing and carrying out the provisions of the fire bill, emergency bill, and barracks regulations.
          • (b)Maintaining good order and discipline.
          • (c)Ensuring that unauthorized personnel do not enter the barracks.
        • (3)Barracks security is usually a roving watch.
    • 8.Purpose of the Watch, Quarter, and Station Bill (WQSB)
      • a.Displays your duties for each emergency and watch condition, and your administrative and operational duties.
      • b.Lists divisional stations you will be assigned, such as:
        • (1)Cleaning Station
        • (2)General Quarters (also referred to as Battle Stations)
        • (3)Watch Details
        • (4)Fire Details
        • (5)Rescue and Assistance Details
        • (6)Abandon Ship
        • (7)Man Overboard Details
      • c.You are responsible for checking the Watch, Quarter, and Station Bill (WQSB) daily:
        • (1)For changes in your assignment.
        • (2)To refresh your memory on assignments for seldom-used details.
    • 9.Duties and Responsibilities of Recruit Watches
      • a.Recruit Watchbill Procedures.
      • b.Roving/Night Security Watches:
        • (1)The responsibilities are to make rounds ensuring order, discipline, and cleanliness of the compartment.
        • (2)Posted no later than 1-5 Day of Training (DOT).
        • (3)Uniform of the Day with Ball Cap.
        • (4)Roving Watch may be secured at the discretion of the LCPO for curriculum event, except when Sick in Quarters (SIQ) Recruits are in the compartment.
        • (5)The Night Security Watch is stationed only between Taps and Reveille.
      • c.Snow Watches
        • (1)Posted from 1 November to 31 March.
        • (2)Recruits must be Fit for Full Duty (FFD).
      • d.Junior Officer of the Deck (JOOD)
        • (1)Stands watch on the ship’s quarterdeck in the uniform of the day with cover, less Outergarments.
        • (2)Guards the Quarterdeck. To gain access to the ship, personnel must pass the JOOD.
        • (3)Check ID cards against the access list and the Plan of the Week (POW); if the visitor is not listed, notify the LCPO/OOD.
        • (4)Log visiting personnel in the quarterdeck log and direct them not to leave the quarterdeck area unless escorted by a staff member on the access list. Visitors may wait in the RDC lounge.
        • (5)Answers the telephone using proper procedures.
        • (6)Notifies the OOD of any incoming emergency telephone calls. If in doubt, notify the OOD.
      • e.Messenger of the Watch (MOOD)
        • (1)Makes hourly tours of the ship’s doors. Notify the OOD if doors are not secure.
        • (2)Logs compartment temperatures.
          • (a)During periods when heat is required, temperature should be a minimum of 68o F.
          • (b)Report abnormal temperatures to the OOD.
    • 10.Watchstander Procedures During a Breach of Security
      • a.Immediately report to the Quarterdeck or OOD if a visitor loses their escort or evades the watch.
      • b.If the OOD is unavailable, the JOOD will announce a security alert throughout the ship via the 1MC general announcing system.
      • c.The OOD/LCPO will direct a search of the ship using the watches and any other personnel required, checking all spaces systematically until the intruder is found or the ship is cleared.
    • 11.Basic Components of a 9mm Pistol
      • a.Pistol Grip - Provides for placement of the firing hand.
      • b.Trigger - When squeezed, fires the weapon.
      • c.Trigger Guard - Protects the trigger from being accidentally depressed.
      • d.Slide - Cocks the weapon during its rearward movement.
      • e.Slide Stop - After the last round is fired, locks the slide rearward, in the open position.
      • f.Safety - When on, secures the weapon so it will not fire.
      • g.Magazine - Stores 15 rounds of ammunition.
      • h.Magazine Release Button - When depressed, releases the magazine from the pistol.
      • i.Chamber - Before firing, holds a round in the aft portion of the barrel.
      • j.Holster - Used to carry or transport the weapon.
    • 12.General Weapon Condition Codes
      • a.Condition 1: The magazine is inserted into the weapon, there is a round in the chamber, the slide is forward, and the safety is on.
      • b.Condition 2: Not applicable to the 9mm pistol.
      • c.Condition 3: The magazine is inserted into the weapon, the chamber is empty, the slide is forward, and the safety is on.
      • d.Condition 4: The magazine is removed from the weapon, the chamber is empty, the slide is forward, and the safety is on.
    • 13.Watchstanding Weapons Turnover
      • a.SAFETY PRECAUTIONS
        • (1)Treat all weapons as if they are loaded.
        • (2)When performing weapons turnover:
          • (a)Keep the trigger finger straight and outside the trigger guard.
          • (b)Keep the pistol pointed into the clearing barrel.
          • (c)Attach a lanyard to the pistol.
        • (3)Do NOT perform weapons turnover from memory; always refer to the posted Clearing Barrel Instructions.
      • b.Off-Going Watch
        • (1)Unholster the weapon, keep trigger finger straight, and point the pistol directly into the clearing barrel.
        • (2)Ensure the SAFETY is engaged.
        • (3)With the non-firing thumb, depress the magazine release button, remove the magazine and hand it to the oncoming watchstander.
        • (4)Lock the slide to the rear.
        • (5)Visually and physically inspect the chamber and magazine well to ensure a clear and safe weapon.
      • c.Oncoming Watch
        • (1)While keeping the pistol directed into the clearing barrel, take control of the weapon by grasping the pistol grip, and the trigger finger straight along the weapon.
        • (2)Visually and physically inspect the chamber and magazine well to ensure a clear and safe weapon.
        • (3)Release slide.
        • (4)With the non-firing hand, insert the magazine.
        • (5)Holster the weapon.

    • 15.Definitions of “Hangfire” and “Misfire”
      • a.Hangfire - Delay in the explosion of the charge after the trigger is pulled
      • b.Misfire - Failure of a round to fire after trigger is pulled.
    • 16.Deadly Force Terminology and Use of Deadly Force.
      • a.Force - To do violence, to compel by physical or intellectual means or to achieve or win by strength in struggle or violence.
      • b.Deadly Force - Force that a person uses causing, or that a person knows or should know, would create a substantial risk of causing death or serious bodily harm.
      • c.Serious Bodily Harm - Does not include minor injuries, such as a black eye or a bloody nose, but does include fractured or dislocated bones, deep cuts, torn members of the body, serious damage to the internal organs and other life-threatening injuries.
    • 17.Guidance on the Use of Deadly Force
      • a.Deadly force is justified only under conditions of extreme necessity, and when lesser means have been exhausted, are unavailable, or cannot be reasonably employed.
    • 18.Deadly Force Triangle
      • a.Capability
        • (1)A hostile or dangerous person must possess the actual ability to inflict death or serious bodily harm to another person such as possessing a gun, knife, club or other object, which can be utilized in a manner resulting in death or serious bodily harm, or has “hands on” ability to place or attach explosives on vital assets.
      • b.Opportunity
        • (1)U.S. Navy personnel, another service member or designated asset is in the effective range of a weapon system or improvised explosive device.
      • c.Intent
        • (1)A hostile or dangerous person demonstrates, through aggressive actions or display of a weapon in a threatening manner, the willingness to cause death or serious bodily harm.
    • 19.Guidance For the Use of Firearms
      • a.A weapon should not be removed from the holster or un-slinging unless there is reasonable exception that use of the weapon may be necessary.
      • b.Warning shots are strictly prohibited.
        • NOTE: Drawing or un-slinging a weapon does not mean the individual MUST use the weapon.
      • c.When a firearm is discharged, it will be fired with the intent of rendering the person(s), at whom it is discharged, incapable of continuing the activity or source of behavior prompting the individual to shoot.
      • d.Shots shall be fired only with regard for the safety of innocent bystanders.
      • e.Heads of DoD may establish additional considerations in implementing procedures over the use of firearms.
    • 20.Force Protection Conditions (FPCONs)
      • a.FPCON Alpha - A general threat of possible terrorist activity, but the nature and extent of the threat is unpredictable.
      • b.FPCON Bravo - An increased or more predictable threat of terrorist activity exists.
      • c.FPCON Charlie - Applies when an incident occurs or intelligence is received indicating some form of terrorist action or targeting against personnel or facilities is likely.
      • d.FPCON Delta - Applies in the immediate area where a terrorist attack has occurred or when intelligence has been received that terrorist action against a specific location or person is imminent.
    • 21.Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Watchstanding
      • a.Honor: Take responsibility for your watch station and your actions for maintaining a proper watch.
      • b.Courage: Have the courage to report violations even when it involves your shipmate or friend.
      • c.Commitment: Stand your watch with the highest degree of moral character, professional excellence, and competence.
