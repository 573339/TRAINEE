A. Introduction

The first part of this lesson provides the knowledge needed to safely handle the 9mm pistol and 12-gauge shotgun. In the second part of the lesson, you will fire the pistol and shotgun. Inattention or negligence on your part can have serious consequences.

    Safety is the NUMBER 1 concern at the live fire range
    1. General Rangel Safety Rules
      1. Before firing any small arm, inspect the weapon.
        1. Safety - Ensure safety is in position.
        2. Magazine - Ensure the magazine is empty.
        3. Chamber - Ensure the chamber is .
        4. Safety - Again ensure safety is .
      2. Always make sure the bore is free of obstructions.
      3. Never discharge a fire arm while running.
      4. Always be aware of what is the target, and the potential impact zone.
      5. Use only the proper size ammunition. Inspect ammunition to ensure projectiles are properly seated (not loose) in the round casing; the cartridge case is not dented and no corrosion is present.
      6. Never leave weapons loaded and/or unattended, even for a brief period of time.
      7. Never indulge in horseplay when carrying a firearm or while on the firing range.
      8. Weapons shall be clear before any personnel go down range. Weapons or ammunition shall not be handled or manipulated in any way if there is anyone down range.
    2. If any unsafe condition is observed, immediately call “Cease Fire” or repeat the command if initiated by another shooter. Anyone can call a cease-fire on the range.
  4. Small Arms Safety on the Firing Range
    1. Universal Weapons Safety Rules
      1. Proper weapons handling is the responsibility of the person in control of the weapon. When armed with any weapon where chambering a round has been authorized, personnel must ensure that the safety remains in the safe position until immediately prior to firing.
      2. There are FOUR rules that must be observed at all times, whether in training or in combat. These rules are the foundation for responsible weapons handling.
        1. Rule 1: Treat every weapon as if it were loaded.
        2. Rule 2: Never point a weapon at anything you do not intend to shoot.
        3. Rule 3: Keep your finger straight and off the trigger until you are ready to fire.
        4. Rule 4: Keep weapon on SAFE until you intend to fire.
  5. Definitions of “Hangfire” and “Misfire”
    1. Hangfire -- Delay in the explosion of the charge after the trigger is pulled.
    2. -- Failure of a round to fire after trigger is pulled.
    3. If the weapon fails to fire after the trigger is pulled:
      1. Ensure weapon remains sighted in on your target.
      2. Count to ten.
      3. Keep the weapon level and pointed down range; return to the standing ready position.
      4. Signal for a Training Time Out and wait for assistance.
  6. Components, Characteristics, and Functions of 9mm Pistol
    1. - -- Provides for proper placement of hands for firing.
      1. Proper Two Handed Grip -- Back-strap fits into “V” created by thumb, fingers and palm. Thumbs stacked on same side. Wrist locked to prevent vertical displacement of shots.
      2. Finger Position -- Trigger finger along slide assembly until ready to fire.
    2. Magazine Well - Receives the magazine.
    3. 9mm -- Stores a maximum 15 rounds of ammunition.
    4. button -- When depressed, releases magazine from pistol.
    5. -- After pistol is fired, the recoil of the assembly:
      1. the empty cartridge.
      2. Cocks the hammer.
      3. Loads next round.
    6. Slide Stop -- After the last round is fired, automatically holds the slide to the rear.
    7. Decocking/Safety Lever -- Used to “safe” the weapon. When the safety is on, the:
      1. Hammer is released to the forward position.
      2. Weapon will NOT fire.
    8. and sights -- Used to align weapon and obtain sight alignment/sight picture.
    9. -- Used to fire weapon. Always squeeze trigger. Do not jerk trigger.
    10. -- When trigger is squeezed, drops forward causing the round to fire.
  7. Procedures to Load and Unload a 9mm Pistol
    1. Pistol Loading
      1. Insert a loaded magazine into the magazine well of the pistol until a click is heard. This ensures proper placement of the magazine.
      2. To load first round, press down on slide stop.
    2. Pistol Unloading/Magazine Removal
      1. Keep your weapon and pointed range at your target.
      2. Place decocking/safety lever in the “” (down) position.
      3. If you are left-handed, ensure your right hand is under the magazine. Depress the magazine release button with your right middle finger and catch the magazine with your right hand.
      4. If you are right-handed, ensure your left hand is under the magazine. Depress the magazine release button with your right thumb and catch the magazine with your left hand.
  8. 9mm Pistol Stances
    1. Standing isosceles
      1. Face the target.
      2. Stand with body erect and balanced.
      3. Ensure your toes are pointed forward and feet are placed -width apart.
      4. Arms straight out from your body.
      5. Ensure the weapon is level, pointed down range, and in the upright position.
      6. Keep your finger the trigger.
    2. Kneeling Isosceles
  9. Weapon Condition Codes
    1. Prior to loading the 9mm pistol, the weapon handler should be knowledgeable of weapon-specific condition codes. These codes enable the weapons handler to quickly identify weapon status and clearly convey that information to others.
    2. A weapon’s readiness to fire and its corresponding safety status are described, by one of four weapon condition codes. The condition codes for the 9mm are as follows:
      1. Condition 4: Magazine removed, slide forward, NO round chambered, safety on.
      2. Condition 3: Magazine inserted, slide forward, NO round chambered, safety on.
      3. Condition 2: Not Applicable.
      4. Condition 1: Magazine inserted, slide forward, round chambered, safety on.
  10. Weapons Commands
    1. Weapon commands direct personnel to safely load, unload and employ small arms. The following commands shall be used in weapons handling and on the firing range.
      1. “LOAD”: This command is used to take a weapon from condition 4 to condition 3.
      2. “MADE READY”: This command is used to take a weapon from condition 3 to condition 1.
      3. “LOAD AND MAKE READY”: This is a combined command used to take a weapon from condition 4 to condition 1.
      4. “FIRE”: This command is used to engage targets.
      5. “Holster”: A non-verbal command used at RTC Directs a shooter to place the weapon in their holster. Signified by two taps on the shoulder.
      6. “Stand-by”: used as a preparatory command indicating the shooter has permission to grip the weapon still in the holster. The weapon will NOT be drawn,
      7. “CEASE FIRE”: This command is used to cease target engagement.
      8. “UNLOAD” This command is used to take a weapon from condition 1 or 3, to a condition 4 weapon.
      9. “UNLOAD, SHOW CLEAR”: This command is used to require a second individual to check the weapon to verify that no ammunition is present before the weapon is put into condition 4.
    2. When showing clear; utilize the four safety steps, verifying each of the following, both physically and visually in sequential order:
      1. Safety - Ensure safety is in safe position.
      2. Magazine - Ensure magazine is empty.
      3. Chamber - Ensure chamber is clear.
      4. Safety - Again, ensure safety is on safe.
  11. Navy Handgun Qualification Course of Fire
    1. 3 yards - 12 rounds
    2. 7 yards - 12 rounds
    3. 15 yards - 24 rounds
    4. Scoring - This is a qualification course. Minimum score required 180 points. This course counts towards earning a ribbon/medal.
  12. Components, Characteristics and Functions of 12-Gauge Shotgun
    1. Butt Stock -- Aids in holding the weapon steady.
    2. Receiver -- Supports all major components.
    3. Barrel -- Directs the buckshot to the target.
      1. Chamber - The portion of the barrel that holds the shotgun shells.
    4. Front Sight -- Fixed bead. For aiming at the target.
    5. Trigger -- When squeezed, fires the weapon.
    6. Trigger Guard -- Protects the trigger to prevent unintentional firing.
    7. Safety Button -- When fully to the rear, prevents the weapon from firing; when in the forward position, allows the weapon or fire.
    8. Ejection Port
      1. Opening through which expended shells are ejected.
      2. Can be used to load shotgun shells into the chamber.
    9. Magazine Tube
      1. Stores five shells.
      2. Shotgun shells are loaded into the magazine tube through an opening below the weapon.
    10. Action Slide Assembly -- When moved forward, loads shotgun shells into the chamber; when moved to the rear, ejects expended shells from the magazine tube through the ejection port.
    11. Action Lock Lever -- When the weapon is cocked, lock the action slide assembly in the forward position. To bring the action slide assembly to the rear position, either:
      1. Press up on the action lock lever.
      2. Shotgun shells are loaded into the magazine tube through an opening below the weapon.
  13. Procedures for Loading the 12-Gauge Shotgun
    1. Chamber Loading (Combat Load)
      1. Push Safety to “ON” position.
      2. Depress the action lock lever.
      3. Pull the action slide assembly fully to the rear.
      4. Place cartridge/shell into open ejection port.
      5. Push action slide assembly forward to load cartridge into the chamber.
    2. Magazine Loading
      1. Push Safety on “ON” position.
      2. If the action slide assembly is open, push completely forward to close.
      3. Do NOT turn the shotgun over.
      4. Push the cartridge into the magazine tube until the rim of the cartridge snaps past the cartridge stop into the magazine.
  14. Twelve-Gauge Shotgun Stances
    1. Standing Ready (Tactical Carry)
      1. Face your target.
      2. The Range Safety Officer will instruct you to half-right face if you are right-handed; half-left face if you are left-handed, He/she will then hand you a shotgun.
      3. Stand with body erect and balanced.
      4. Hold the weapon at waist level, with the barrel pointed upward at approximately 45 degrees. Place your:
        1. Forward “weak” hand on the underneath portion of the action slide assembly.
        2. Rear “strong” hand on the butt stock, just aft of the receiver.
    2. Standing (Shoulder Firing Stance)
      1. Place the butt stock into your “strong” shoulder.
      2. Place your “strong” arm parallel with the deck forming a pocket in the shoulder.
      3. Pull the weapon tightly into the pocket by the “strong” hand on the grip of the butt stock.
      4. Place your “weak” arm vertical under the weapon with “weak” hand on the action slide assembly.
      5. Place cheek tightly against the butt stock with “strong” eye in line with the sight bead.
      6. Bend your forward knee and place your weight forward. Lean into it.
    3. Shooting from the hip
      1. Lock your forward arm downward, just forward of the body.
      2. Keep the shotgun tucked tightly between your strong arm and hip.
      3. Bend your forward knee and place your weight forward. Lean into it.
    4. Procedures for Firing the 12-Gauge Shotgun
      1. Course of fire for shotgun.
        1. 3 rounds from the shoulder
        2. 2 rounds from the hip
  15. Weapons condition codes
    1. Condition 4: Weapon on “safe,” chamber empty, Magazine tube empty, action closed.
    2. Condition 3: Weapon on “safe,” chamber empty, Magazine tube filled, action closed.
    3. Condition 2: Not Applicable.
    4. Condition 1: Weapon on “safe,” round chambered, Magazine tube filled, action closed.
  16. Range Operations
    1. Range Safety Officer:
      1. All firing sequences and range commands shall be given by the Range Safety Officer (RSO).
      2. Shooters must listen carefully, concentrate on and adhere to all instructions and commands given by the RSO.
    2. Range Coaches (Line Coach):
      1. Responsible to the RSO.
        1. They have direct and immediate authority over the shooter(s) under his/her supervision while on the firing line.
      2. Line coaches will be on the firing line behind the shooter(s), and will assist with shooting fundamentals.
      3. They will analyze, score targets, assist in performing remedial actions to clear stoppages/malfunctions and ensure all safety rules are being followed.
    3. Firing Line:
      1. All live fire shall take place at the firing line as designated in the course of fire.
      2. Weapons shall not be handled at the firing line until specific instructions are given by the RSO.
      3. No one shall move downrange, or go forward of the firing line, unless directed to do so by the RSO.
      4. The pistol shall not be drawn, or removed from the holster until at the firing line facing the designated target, and only when instructed to do so by the RSO or his designated representative (Line Coach).
    4. Weapons Malfunctions:
      1. If you experience a malfunction or stoppage:
        1. Signal for a TTO
        2. Wait for further guidance by your Line Coach
        3. DO NOT attempt to fix the problem yourself
    5. Personal Protective Equipment:
      1. Small Arms Personal Protection Equipment (PPE)
        1. In addition to following and applying range safety procedures when handling and firing small arms, you should take steps to protect your hearing, particularly when you are exposed to repeated small arms fire, such as on a firing range.
      2. Hearing protection is required at all times when firearms are being discharged during training and qualification.
      3. Approved hearing protection such as sound attenuators that reduce noise levels to 84 decibels or below is required.
      4. There are two general types of ear protective devices:
        1. Ear Plugs (Insert Type)
        2. Circumaural (Mickey Mouse ears)
      5. Blast noise from small arms fire may cause a temporary or permanent hearing loss.
        1. The extend of injury depends on a number of factors, such as:
          1. Intensity of the noise,
          2. Length of exposure,
          3. Your own sensitivity to noise hazards, and
          4. Type of hearing protection utilized.
      6. When conducting range operations on an indoor range; both types of hearing protection are required due to increased intensity level of noise.
      7. Shatterproof eye protection is required when shooting.
        1. American National Standards Institute (ANSI) approved glasses are required.
      8. Once the range has been declared “Hot” (live fire exercises have commenced) all personnel on the range are required to have safety glasses and hearing protection.
        1. This encompasses all personnel on the firing line and/or behind the ready line whom are observing.
