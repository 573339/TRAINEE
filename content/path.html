    1. Formal Navy schools
      1. Apprenticeship training
        1. Teaches personnel in pay grades E-1 through E-3 the basic shipboard skills and knowledge required for entry into groups of ratings.
        2. There are three apprenticeship schools.
      2. “A” schools -- Provide technical knowledge and skills required for job performance in a Navy rating.
      3. “C” schools -- Provide advanced and skills to perform a particular job.
    2. On-the-job training (OJT)
      1. Takes place during daily and maintenance situations.
      2. Most widely used form of training in the Navy.
      3. or informal instruction pertaining to a specific job, skill, or task.

    3. Professional military training correspondence courses
      1. Military requirements nonresident training courses.
        1. Used to prepare for .

  3. Factors That Affect a Person’s Advancement in Rate
    1. Performance
      1. Your key to success in the Navy is sustained performance.
      2. Your supervisor observes your and submits input to the chain of command.
      3. Evaluations are submitted . They are based on a 5.0 grading scale and are retained in your service record throughout your Navy career.
      4. Personnel will be evaluated on the categories listed on their Evaluation Report and Counseling Record:
        1. Professional -- Technical knowledge and practical application.
        2. Quality of -- Standard of work; the amount of supervision required to perform the job.
        3. Equal opportunity -- Contribution to:
          1. Command
          2. Unit
          3. The command’s Equal Opportunity Program.
        4. Military /character -- Appearance, conduct, physical fitness, adherence to Navy Core Value.
        5. Personal job accomplishment and -- Taking responsibility for doing the job.
        6. Teamwork -- Contributions to building.
        7. Leadership (optional for E-1 to E-3) -- Organizing, motivating, and developing others to goals.
    2. Navy-wide advancement examination and final multiple score
      1. Navy-wide advancement examinations are given to all advancement candidates, E-4 through E-7, who are:
        1. Fully qualified for .
        2. Within readiness/body standards.
        3. Recommended for advancement by their Commanding Officer.
          1. Most important eligibility requirement.
          2. A recommendation is not automatic; it must be earned. The chain of command’s input is vital in obtaining CO’s recommendation.
      2. Exams are given in and September for petty officers 3rd class, 2nd class, and 1st class.
      3. Exam can NOT be taken while in “A” school.
      4. The final multiple score is used to rank all individuals who took the same rating exam. It is determined by:
        1. Performance mark
        2. score
        3. Time in rate/ (amount of time spent in your current pay grade)
        5. Previous examination performances; i.e., Passed Not Advanced (PNA) points
          1. Passed the exam, but the final exam score is not high enough to be advanced in a particular rating.
          2. Extra points are awarded toward the next rating exam.

  5. Career Navigator Program
    1. The goal of the Career Navigator is to help sailors make informed decisions about their personal and professional career opportunities by placing the power of career management systems at their fingertips.
      1. Under Career navigator all enlisted active component (AC) and component (RC) career management policies, process and information technology (IT) support systems are organized into one overarching program.
    2. The Career Navigator Program allows Sailors to explore and choose available career opportunities. It encompasses all policies and procedures for career events, such as:
      1. Initial rating choice,
      2. Reclassification during initial skills training. (if needed).
      3. Career Development Boards (CDBs)
      4. PACT designation
      5. Retirement
      6. Converting to another rating
      7. Transition between active and reserve components
      8. Billet assignments (orders negotiation)
      9. Electronic service record management
      10. Career opportunity feedback surveys
      11. Preparing for advancement
    3. Career Navigator provides self-service capability so that Sailors will be able to monitor their own information, opportunities and applications.
    4. Career is the IT system that supports key Sailor decisions about the next career choice. The enlisted career management processes in Career Waypoints include:
      1. Reenlistment
      2. Reclassification
      3. Professional Apprenticeship Career Track (PACT) designation.
      4. Conversion
      5. Transition between components.
    5. A career viewpoint survey will be available in the Navy Integrated Personnel System (NSIPS) so that Sailors are provided direct feedback on their Navy opportunities at major decision points in their career.
    6. For further information about the Career Navigator Program you can look at policy, information and training materials located on the Navy Personnel Command Website: http://www.public.navy.mil/bupers-npc/pages/default.asps or contact your Command Career Counselor.
