• A.Introduction
    • The Navy’s policies governing pregnancy and family care responsibilities are vital to ensure readiness and mission accomplishment. This lesson describes personal responsibilities in planning for a family, policies for pregnant service women, and care for family members in respect to single parents or in cases where both parents are in the military.
  • B.Enabling Objective(s):
    • IDENTIFY your personal responsibility for planning a family.
    IDENTIFY the personal, economic, and professional considerations for planning a family.
    EXPLAIN servicewomen’s responsibilities regarding pregnancy.
    EXPLAIN the Navy’s responsibility to provide obstetrical care.
    EXPLAIN the Navy policy for separating pregnant servicewomen from the Navy.
    EXPLAIN the Navy policy for the assignment of pregnant servicewomen.
    EXPLAIN the Navy policy for post-delivery convalescent leave.
    EXPLAIN the Navy family care policy.
    EXPLAIN the consequences for failing to maintain an up-to-date family care plan.
    EXPLAIN the relationship between the Navy Core Values and meeting family obligations.
  • C.Topic Outline
    • 1.Personal Responsibility for Planning a Family
      • a.It is never too early to plan. You may currently be:
        • (1)Single and not thinking about it.
        • (2)Single and seriously thinking about it.
        • (3)Recently married.
        • (4)Married for a while.
      • b.Regardless of your marital status, start thinking about the effects of becoming a parent now. You should identify your:
        • (1)Resources
          • (a)Current and projected financial status.
          • (b)Ability to house, feed, clothe, and care for new family member.
          • (c)Support network
            • 1)Family
            • 2)Friends
        • (2)Short-range goals
          • (a)Complete boot camp.
          • (b)Complete follow-on training.
          • (c)Meet initial command and advancement qualifications.
          • (d)Begin savings/investment plans.
          • (e)Begin furthering education.
        • (3)Long-range goals
          • (a)Complete advanced command qualifications.
          • (b)Gain more responsibility through advancement.
          • (c)Continue higher level of savings/investment plans in conjunction with advancement.
          • (d)Complete desired education level.
          • (e)Marriage.
          • (f)Children.
      • c.Starting a family is a lifetime commitment. You must be prepared to meet the challenges of:
        • (1)Infant needs.
        • (2)Childcare obligations.
        • (3)Childhood illnesses.
        • (4)Teenage defiance.
        • (5)Continuing educational needs.
      • d.Communicate openly and honestly with your partner and your command.
        • (1)Agree on the best time to start a family.
        • (2)Understand each parent’s responsibilities for the new child.
        • (3)Outline a financial budget that includes the new expenses.
        • (4)Discuss how to deal with family separation associated with military duties.
    • 2.Personal, Economic, and Professional Considerations for Planning a Family
      • a.Personal Considerations
        • (1)Discuss with your partner what it means to begin a family, to include emotional, spiritual, and physical support of each other.
        • (2)If your partner is a service member (active or reserve), discuss how having a baby will affect both parents’ military obligations and career desires.
        • (3)Discuss the major lifestyle changes that come with having a baby and if you both are willing to accept these changes.
        • (4)Discuss with your partner if you are both ready to accept the responsibilities of parenthood.
      • b.Economic Considerations
        • (1)Current debts and savings.
        • (2)Current earnings.
        • (3)Is current automobile able to safely accommodating children needs; e.g., mechanically sound, fits a car seat, etc.
        • (4)Increase in grocery bill.
        • (5)Day care charges.
        • (6)Adequate living space in current home.
          • (a)If housed in barracks, must move out to civilian community and/or await base housing.
          • (b)Most bases have a waiting list for base housing ranging from a few weeks to two years.
      • c.Professional Considerations
        • (1)Current duty station duration.
        • (2)Future duty assignment locations for both parents (if spouse is active or reserve military member).
          • (a)Many Sailors leave their civilian spouse and children behind or with relatives if the upcoming duty assignment is cost prohibitive.
        • (3)A civilian spouse may desire to remain behind because of a job or education needs for the children or self.
        • (4)Reduced opportunity to prepare for advancement due to parental obligations -- Finding time and a quiet place to study is drastically hindered by a crying baby and/or a partner who needs time for himself/herself.
        • (5)Professional commitments -- For example, constantly changing work schedules, duty requirement, deployments, temporary assigned duty away from base, etc. Reservists and active duty personnel may have to leave their families within 24 hours of notification of mobilization or deployment.
        • (6)Requirement to remain worldwide assignable -- Sailors must leave their family members behind if the next duty assignment location cannot support their medical, educational, or housing needs.
    • 3.Servicewomen’s Responsibilities Regarding Pregnancy
      • a.Plan the pregnancy to meet both family and military obligations.
      • b.Confirm the pregnancy at a military medical treatment facility.
      • c.Notify the commanding officer or officer in charge of the pregnancy no later than two weeks after diagnosis.
      • d.Perform military duties while pregnant within limits established by their conditions.
      • e.Comply with work-site and task-related safety and health recommendations.
      • f.Report to the military treatment facility to establish a prenatal care program.
      • g.Have the same rights and responsibilities and are subject to the same administrative and disciplinary actions as all other Naval personnel.
    • 4.Navy’s Responsibility to provide Obstetrical care -- When pregnant servicewomen remain at their duty stations, maternity care will be provided at the military treatment facility designated, provided:
      • a.It has OB-GYN capabilities.
      • b.The servicewoman resides in the facility’s inpatient area.
    • 5.Navy Policy for Separating Pregnant Servicewomen
      • a.Servicewomen will be discharged from the Navy without maternity benefits when their pregnancy:
        • (1)Existed before entrance (EPTE) into Recruit Training.
        • (2)Is certified during Recruit Training.
      • b.Pregnant servicewomen may request separation before the 20th week of pregnancy, although such requests are NOT normally approved.
      • c.For former servicewomen who separate from active duty whole pregnant, care for pregnancy can only be provided by Navy Medical Treatment Facilities through the first six weeks following delivery.
      • d.Prior to separation, servicewomen should receive counseling by a Patient Administration Officer/Representative about current information regarding health care benefits available to separating servicewomen
    • 6.Navy Policy for the Assignment of Pregnant Servicewomen
      • a.Overseas -- Based on medical considerations; no servicewoman may be assigned overseas or travel overseas AFTER the completion of the 28th week of pregnancy.
      • b.Continental United States (CONUS)
        • (1)May be assigned in CONUS without restriction provided servicewoman does not fly after the 36th week of pregnancy.
        • (2)Will not be transferred to a deploying unit during the period from the 20th week of pregnancy through 12 months after expected date of delivery.
      • c.Shipboard
        • (1)The Commanding Officer, in consultation with medical personnel, shall decide whether the individual may safely continue her shipboard assigned duties.
        • (2)Pregnant servicewomen shall not remain aboard ship if the time for medical evacuation to a treatment facility is greater than six (6) hours.
        • (3)Servicewomen shall not remain onboard a deployed unit beyond the 20th week of pregnancy.
        • (4)Transfer assignments are deferred up to 12 months following delivery, unless the service member volunteers for early rotation.
      • d.Aviation squadron
        • (1)Pregnancy is considered disqualifying for designated flight status personnel. For example, a female Sea, Air and Rescue Swimmer would be disqualified until after her recovery period.
        • (2)Enlisted servicewomen who become pregnant while assigned to sea duty aviation squadrons due for deployment should be reassigned to a squadron not scheduled for deployment from their 20th week of pregnancy through the recuperative period.
      • e.Military schools -- if a servicewoman becomes pregnant during training, the school’s commanding officer will determine if the student can complete training based on projected delivery and recuperation dates.
    • 7.Navy Policy for Post-Delivery Convalescent Leave – Six (6) weeks (42 days) will normally be granted by the Commanding Officer upon advice from the attending physician.
    • 8.Navy Family Care Policy
      • a.Applies to single service members and dual military couples (i.e., husband and wife both in the military) who have custody of children under 19 or other dependents.
      • b.Members who will be deployed and a designated caregiver are required to complete the NAVPERS 1740/6 (Family Care Plan Certificate) and the NAVPERS 1740/7 (Family Care Plan Arrangement) forms. These forms:
        • (1)Certify that dependents will be cared for during the member’s absence.
        • (2)Identify relocation and financial arrangements.
      • c.The following documents must be provided to the designated caregiver(s):
        • (1)Powers of attorney authorizing:
          • (a)The caregiver to act as the parent.
          • (b)Medical care for the dependents.
        • (2)Identification cards for all eligible dependents.
        • (3)A will, with guardianship provisions.
      • d.If the designated caregiver is not in the local area, there must be:
        • (1)A non-military escort for family members requiring assistance, such as infants, children, or elderly disabled adults.
        • (2)Financial support necessary to transport the family or caregiver to a designated location.
    • 9.Consequences for Failing to Maintain an Up-To-Date Family Plan
      • a.Can subject the member to separation from the Navy.
      • b.Commanding Officers may separate members who:
        • (1)Are unable or refuse to maintain an updated family care plan.
        • (2)Do not remain worldwide assignable.
        • (3)Are unable to perform their professional and military duties.
    • 10.Relationship between the Navy Core Values and Family Obligations
      • a.Honor -- Be accountable for personal behavior.
        • (1)Practice abstinence or safe and responsible sex.
        • (2)Provide adequate care and financial and emotional support for your partner and children.
      • b.Courage -- Moral and mental strength to do what is right, even when tempted.
      • c.Commitment -- Navy personnel should be committed to balancing their family and Navy obligations.
        • (1)Navy personnel and their partners must be dedicated to sharing family responsibilities.
          • (a)Caring for children
          • (b)Performing work at home
          • (c)Planning finances
        • (2)Plan what to do about family matters during deployments.
        • (3)When separated, couples should maintain their commitment to each other; e.g., communicate by letter, telephone, e-mail, etc.
